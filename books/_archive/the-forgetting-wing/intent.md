## Premise

The State erases one day of memory from every citizen, every year. The Wing chooses the day. It is, the State explains, a public-health measure. Some days are toxic. Some witnessed things the public does not need to carry. After the procedure you go on; you cannot miss what you do not know is gone.

Maren is seventeen. The morning after her treatment she wakes in an alley with three days of memory she should not have, a paring knife in her hand, and a man at her feet she has never seen before. He has been dead for some time.

Across the city, the Forgetting Wing's youngest Inquisitor — Sael, sixteen, an orphan of the program himself, fitted with a polygraph implant that reads micro-expressions as lies surfaced on the skin — is called in. The case is unusual. The victim was a Wing employee. The killer left no biometrics the Wing's archive can match. And one of the days the killer remembers belongs to a class of erasure that, officially, does not exist.

She has to find out what happened in the three days she wasn't supposed to know about. He has to find her before she does. Both are wrong about who the other is. Both are right about what the Wing is hiding. They will meet, and they will keep meeting, because the city is small and the Wing is watching both of them — for reasons that have nothing to do with the body in the alley.

## Theme

What does a person owe the version of themselves the State has decided to forget? The book asks whether memory is something done *to* a person or something done *by* a person — and what it costs to insist on remembering when the world has agreed not to.

Secondary theme: the closest people in your life can be the ones you've been most carefully edited away from.

## Promise to the reader

- Visceral, immediate, anime-style prose. Tight close-3rd alternating between Maren and Sael. Past tense. Set-piece-built — every chapter has at least one image, action, or revelation worth remembering.
- A psychological cat-and-mouse on the scale of Death Note: two prodigies, opposite sides, each capable of believing they are the good one.
- A power system with rules and costs: the State's memory technology, and the small black-market countertech that lets people remember. Both grounded in biotech / pharmacology, not magic.
- Deep character development for both POVs. Sael gets the full antagonist-treatment — the reader will sympathize, sometimes more than with Maren. Both arcs interrogate the lie they each believe.
- Suffering that transforms. People will break and be remade. Some will not be remade and will die. Death is on the page when it earns the page.
- An ending that resolves the immediate murder/erasure case but pulls the camera wider — the Wing's deeper purpose is glimpsed, not yet exposed. Series-open, but this book stands.

## What this book is NOT

- Not paranormal, supernatural, or occult. The Wing is biotech. The technology has plausible scientific framing — neural ablation, targeted memory consolidation interference, propofol-adjacent compounds. No spirits, demons, gods, prophecies, magic.
- Not a dystopian YA romance. There is no love triangle. There may be charged tension between Maren and Sael, but it does not resolve in this book and is not the engine.
- Not a procedural. The plot is not "case of the week." It's one rapidly escalating case that exposes the system.
- Not torture-porn. Suffering is rendered, not exploited. Pain transforms; pain does not get dwelled on for spectacle.

## Comparable titles

- *Death Note* — the engine: dual prodigies, opposite sides, prose-equivalent.
- *Never Let Me Go* (Ishiguro) — the institutional cruelty rendered with restraint and devastating interiority.
- *Annihilation* (VanderMeer) — the dread of an institution that does not tell the truth about what it studies.
- *The Power* (Alderman) — biotech premise interrogated through character.
- *Psycho-Pass* — the city-as-system, the Inquisitor archetype.

## POV plan

- Alternating close-3rd, past tense. Chapter granularity: usually one POV per chapter. Mid-book a few back-to-back single-POV chapters when the engine requires it.
- Both POVs have full interiority. Sael is NOT a Light-Yagami villain; he believes the Wing is necessary. Maren is NOT an obvious hero; she may have done worse things in the days she's now remembering than the body in the alley.
- POV distinction enforced at the voice level. Maren thinks in incomplete sentences and second-guesses. Sael thinks in lists and time-counts. The drafter must keep their internal registers distinct enough that the reader knows whose head they are in from the first paragraph of any chapter.

## Voice profile

`library/voice/anime-thriller.md` is the active profile. Drafter loads it on every chapter. Voice-scrubber rules still run in full after drafting.

In addition, the book carries two specific voice rules unique to itself:

1. **Memory ambiguity** — when Maren is in a "remembered" passage, the prose marks it not with italics or asides but with a slight grammatical drift (more present-tense fragments, fewer determiners, sensory rather than narrative). The reader should feel the difference without being told.
2. **Sael's count** — Sael thinks in numbers. Every chapter from his POV contains at least one explicit time-count or measurement in his internal voice. The pattern is part of the character; do not strip it during line-edit.

## Open notes for the plotter

- The climax should be Maren and Sael alone, finally, on the question of what to do with what they each now know. NOT a fight scene — a decision. The fight scene is two chapters before.
- The body in the alley is solved by the midpoint. The midpoint inversion is the discovery that the body is a setup — someone wanted Maren to be caught remembering, and someone wanted Sael to be the one to catch her.
- The deeper mystery — what class of erasure does not officially exist — is the engine of the back half. Resolution at the climax is partial: Maren and Sael learn what was erased; they do not yet know who is choosing. That answer is book 2.
- Plant at least three threads explicitly for book 2:
  - The Wing has a director nobody has met.
  - There is a category of erasure called (working name) "Wing-internal," whose existence the Wing's own field staff do not know about.
  - Sael's own erased days are not what he believes they were.
- Mandatory beats from the genre file (thriller + sci-fi): ticking clock, escalating stakes, end-of-chapter hooks, at least one scene of physical action where Maren is the threat (anime-faithful — the small one wins).
- Both POVs should hit at least one personal-loss beat that bends the arc. Do not duplicate the same beat for both characters.

## Series scope (open-ended)

Book 1: solve the murder; expose the existence of off-the-books erasure; Maren and Sael end on uneasy alliance or hostile understanding, not friendship.

Book 2 (provisional): the political layer. Who runs the Wing. What it was built to forget. Wider POV roster possible.

Book 3+: open. The Wing as a global institution? A pre-Wing remembering underground? Leave room.

The plotter for THIS book should plant book-2 setups but not over-commit the series arc. The first book has to land on its own.
