# Treysa Vinn

role: rival / foil to Sael / Wing peer
pov: no
arc: flat (steadfast in the Wing's frame; will not break in book 1; her steadfastness is the foil)
first-appearance: chapter 4 (Wing corridor); chapter 10 (joint case-review)
aliases: Inquisitor Vinn (formal); Trey (her own cohort, never Sael); Citizen Vinn (citizen-facing)

## Why she exists in the cast

Treysa is Sael's contrast foil and the cleanest pressure test of his lie. Where Sael is the protégé being shaped by Iren and (unknowingly) Mire, Treysa is the Inquisitor who came up cleanly without a patron and who has, by eighteen, already made peace with what the Wing is. She is what Sael could be if he keeps choosing the institution: settled, capable, untroubled, slightly cold. She gives the reader the version of Sael that completes the negative arc and lets the reader feel the future before Sael does. She is not the antagonist. She is the picture of the room he is walking into.

## Want (external goal)

To be promoted to the senior field tier by the end of the calendar year and to take the Tessen Row apartment that becomes available with that promotion. She has been on the waiting list two years. She wants Sael's casework on Sennet's Alley to fail in a way that closes the alley file but leaves a small procedural smudge on his record — not catastrophic, just enough that her promotion is timed before his.

## Need (internal truth)

To recognize that the calm she has cultivated about her work is not equanimity but anesthesia, and that her instinct to be conservative under pressure has been the Wing's instrument for selecting against the questions she would otherwise have asked. She will not get there in book 1. Her flat arc requires her not to. (She becomes book-3 material if the series goes there.)

## Wound

Year four of her training, age fifteen. Her cohort's small dog — a corridor pet the trainees had adopted unofficially and named Sequence — was put down by Wing facilities after a senior officer complained about the smell. Treysa had been the dog's primary feeder. She filed a written objection through the trainee complaint channel. The objection was returned to her with a single annotation in red ink: *Inquisitor candidates do not file objections about animals.* She read the line, threw the form away, and never filed another objection about anything. The wound is not about the dog. The wound is the form returned and the lesson learned: objection is what disqualifies you. From that month on she has been the cohort's most procedurally compliant trainee. She graduated first in her class. Sael, who arrived a year after the dog was put down, has heard the story from two cohort members and has misread it as a triumph of professionalism.

## Lie they believe

*Equanimity is virtue.* Treysa believes that her ability to conduct an interview without distress, to file her reports without lingering, to sleep through the nights Sael cannot sleep through, is the mark of a good Inquisitor. The lie is sophisticated and self-rewarding — the Wing rewards equanimity and the rewards confirm the lie. It will not be tested in book 1. The plot uses her lie as the picture of the resolved negative arc Sael is walking toward.

## Ghost

The morning the dog was gone. Treysa came down to the trainee corridor with the dog's small bowl in her hand and found the bowl's usual corner empty. She stood with the bowl for some seconds and then walked the bowl to the disposal chute and let it fall. She did not cry. The not-crying is what she still admires about that morning. (The drafter may, in ch 18, give her a single line of dialogue about a dog without context. The reader does not need to know. Sael does not know.)

## Competence

- **The Lattice, conservatively applied.** Treysa is slightly less talented than Sael at the Lattice and substantially better at not overreading it. Where Sael forces an ambiguous reading into a confident category, Treysa lets ambiguity sit. Her interrogations close more slowly and more correctly. The plot uses this in ch 10, when she and Sael co-review the Sennet's Alley file and she names — without conviction, without confrontation, just professionally — three of the case's procedural anomalies. Sael hears her. He does not act.
- **Paperwork.** Treysa is the cohort's best at the Wing's case-writing standard. Her files are model. The plot uses this when she files a clean parallel case-summary in ch 18 that does not contradict Sael's but does not endorse it either — a piece of bureaucratic restraint that protects her from any fallout he is about to generate.
- **Reading other Inquisitors.** Six years in the trainee corridors have made her a precise reader of Wing personnel. She has noticed, since ch 4, that Sael is on carrothenidine at a dose that is no longer therapeutic. She has not said.

## Contradiction

She raises orchids in her quarters at Tessen Row — three plants, each a different cultivar, on the south-facing windowsill. She talks to them, briefly, every morning, in the lower-register Lowmaren-Saxonate her own grandmother used (Treysa is the only cohort member raised in part by a Lowmaren grandmother; the other Inquisitors are Estre-Hand wards top to bottom). The orchids are a private practice of attachment she has not been able to dismantle. They are also, in their way, an evasion: she loves what cannot file a complaint.

## Flaw

She mistakes professional conservatism for moral seriousness. Where Sael's flaw is forcing ambiguity into certainty, Treysa's is the opposite: she will let evidence sit, file it, and move on. In a case where the right action is to push past the conservative read, she will not push. The plot exploits this in ch 18-19: Treysa has, by then, identified more of the case's anomalies than Sael has, and she chooses not to escalate them. Iren reads her summary, registers the restraint, and reads it as her wisdom rather than her failure.

## Voice

- **Vocabulary:** Wing-formal Latinate, learned at the ward school and polished by six years of report-writing. She has a thinner first-vocabulary layer than Sael — she was a state ward from age four, no Cant under the Latin. The Lowmaren-Saxonate she uses with her plants is the only first-language register she has, and she uses it only in private.
- **Sentence length default:** Medium and complete. Slightly more clipped than Iren, slightly more flowing than Sael at his most procedural. She is the cohort's most consistent prose stylist; her reports read like model reports because they are.
- **Avoids talking about:** The dog. The grandmother. Her promotion timeline (she will not name it; she only ranks herself in conversation). Carrothenidine, hers or anyone else's. Sael's mother — she has, in fact, read Sael's case file and knows the official version of the orphaning, and she has registered, without naming it, that the file is slightly thin in a way she has seen on other files that turned out to be W-I adjacent. She has not pursued the thought.
- **Speech tics:** *Conservatively read,* — used to soften an interpretation that another Inquisitor would force. *I would not stake my Lattice on it* — her standard hedge. *The cohort line is* — used to invoke a consensus position she may or may not personally hold.
- **Subtext default:** When stressed, she becomes more polite, less brief, and more careful. The mask is courtesy. Sael's mask under stress is procedural; hers is courteous; they are different shapes of the same Wing-trained reflex.

## Physical

Eighteen. Average height, sturdy build — the cohort's training regimen has put muscle on her where it left Sael narrow. Hair dark brown, cut to regulation, slightly grown out at the nape because she is between cuts. The matte port behind her left ear is the Inquisitor-issued model with the cohort-year inlay; she has one cohort year more than Sael, a small visible difference that registers to anyone Wing-trained. The Lattice scar at the jaw-hinge is mirrored to Sael's — same procedure, same surgeon, two years earlier. She has the same right-cheekbone flush from the same carrothenidine; on her it is harder to see because she has more colour at rest. Hazel eyes that read as friendly until she chooses otherwise.

## Arc beats (flat; her steadfastness is the engine)

- **Planted:** ch 4 — She greets Sael in the corridor with the polite, slightly distant warmth that has characterized their interactions for a year. She offers him a cohort joke he half-laughs at. The drafter shows a peer at peace with what Sael cannot yet be at peace with.
- **Tested (her conservatism holds):** ch 10, 18 — She is given two opportunities to escalate her reading of the Sennet's Alley case and declines both. The Wing's reward structure endorses her decision. Her file lengthens; she is named in a Wing internal communication as a candidate for the year's promotion shortlist.
- **Resolved (held):** ch 21-23 — When Sael returns from the climax confrontation with Maren and begins, quietly, to ask cohort members for the names on certain old files, Treysa is the one he does not ask. She is also the one who, asked by Iren what she thinks of Sael's mood, will give Iren a clean, conservative, professionally caring read — *Inquisitor Korrin is fatigued, conservatively read; his case-craft remains sound* — that protects Sael from immediate Audit scrutiny and protects herself from any responsibility for what Sael does next. The kindness is the foil. The reader sees what the Wing's best self looks like and sees that the Wing's best self is not enough.

## Knowledge

- **knows:**
  - Sael is on carrothenidine at a dose past therapeutic. She has not said. She will not say.
  - The Sennet's Alley file has more procedural anomalies than Sael has logged.
  - Three of the anomalies she has identified point to a senior signing hand above Iren. She has the procedural form-memory; she does not have a name.
  - Iren is being prepared for retirement and his recommendations are being honored more easily this year than next year's recommendations will be honored.
  - That her own promotion is conditional on the Sennet's Alley case closing in a particular shape. She has not been told this explicitly. She has read it from the schedule.
- **believes (incorrectly):**
  - That her promotion timeline is meritocratic. (It is being managed.)
  - That her conservative reading of the case is moral as well as professional. (It is professional. The morality is provisional.)
  - That the Wing has no individual director.
  - That the Lattice is a closed loop.
- **doesn't know:**
  - Wing-Internal as a named category.
  - That her own state-wardship origin is structurally identical to Sael's — both ward placements were ordered by the same office that approved both of their mothers' Reviews. Treysa's mother is not in House Andren; her mother died for real in a smearing she was, as a refusal-response carrier, partly responsible for. The Wing does not advertise this. Treysa knows the official cause and does not pursue the unofficial one.
  - That she has been selected, like Sael, as Audit-track. Her timeline is parallel to his.
  - That Andren Sault is alive.
  - That Halver Mire has read her last six case summaries.
