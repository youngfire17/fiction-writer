# Biotech System

Use for: any scene involving the Review, the polygraph implant, the black-market countertech, or a character recalling, refusing, or resisting an erasure. This is the hard-system file. The drafter must not invent capabilities outside it.

## The Wing's procedure — what Annual Consolidation Review actually does

The Review is a combinatorial procedure with three stacked layers. None of them on their own is novel; the State's innovation is the protocol.

1. **Targeted reactivation.** A clinician selects the target day. The selection itself is a Ministry decision, not the clinician's; the clinician receives a sealed cartridge keyed to the citizen's biometric index. Cues from the target day — sounds, images, contextual scents pulled from the citizen's logged location data — are reintroduced through a contoured headset while the citizen is in light sedation. The reactivated memory becomes briefly labile. This is real neuroscience extrapolated forward: reconsolidation windows are measured in hours.
2. **Pharmacological softening.** During the labile window, an intravenous infusion of a propofol-adjacent compound trade-named Lethonal-7 disrupts the protein synthesis required for the memory to be restored to long-term storage. The day's index entries — the hippocampal handles that let the cortex retrieve the episode — are degraded faster than they can be rewritten.
3. **Local ablation.** A micro-focused ultrasound array, delivered through the scalp port (the matte disc behind the left ear is a docking site for the array and the IV manifold), thermally pares the specific engram clusters identified by the reactivation. The ablation is sub-millimeter and produces no clinically detectable lesion. The port is the procedure; without the port, no Review.

The Wing markets this as "consolidation interference." Internally, technicians call it threading: you thread the day out of the weave.

## Cost

- **To the citizen:** twenty-four to seventy-two hours of cognitive fog after the procedure; mild anterograde slippage for two weeks (new memories form, but with weaker timestamps). Small persistent deficits accumulate over a lifetime: roughly one in four citizens over sixty struggles with sequencing recent events even when they remember them. The Ministry calls this Late-Stage Cognitive Smoothing and frames it as benign.
- **To the operator:** Memory Technicians are exposed to mirror-recall — fragments of citizens' days bleed into the technician's own awareness during the reactivation. Untreated, this causes intrusive imagery and what the Wing's internal medicine calls compositional dissociation (the technician begins to confuse remembered scenes with their own). Technicians receive monthly partial Reviews of their own work. Their attrition is high. Many die younger than the citizens they treat.
- **To the State:** the procedure is expensive (estimated 0.4% of city GDP annually) and requires a continuous biometric surveillance infrastructure to know what to thread. The Wing is the most expensive single institution in Halvern-Estre's budget and the only one whose accounts are not published.

## Hard limits — what the Wing's tech CANNOT do

1. **It cannot select a day at the moment of the procedure.** The cartridge is built five to ten days in advance, from logged data. Citizens taken on emergency intake are kept overnight while the cartridge is assembled. There is no improvisation in the Wing's clinical theaters.
2. **It cannot remove memory older than approximately fourteen months.** Long-consolidated memories are systemically distributed across the cortex and cannot be reliably reactivated. The Wing's whole legitimacy rests on annual cadence because the tech only works on relatively fresh material.
3. **It cannot remove a specific event without removing the day around it.** Resolution is calendrical, not surgical. You lose dawn-to-dawn, not the bad ninety seconds.
4. **It cannot remove what was never encoded as episodic memory.** Skills, language, faces seen frequently, emotional dispositions — all survive. A citizen who learned to fear someone on the taken day will still flinch from them; she will not know why.
5. **It cannot work on a brain that has not been ported.** The scalp port is implanted at age fifteen, in the year before First Review. Children, the unported, and citizens whose ports have been damaged are immune to the procedure until reconstruction. Port reconstruction takes six weeks and is the most common reason a citizen "misses" a Review legally.
6. **It does not reliably erase memories that were re-narrated to a second party in close detail.** If a citizen has told the story of the day, in full sensory detail, to someone else within the last seventy-two hours before Review, the act of retelling rewrites the engram in distributed cortex and the ablation misses fragments. The Wing accounts for this with biometric monitoring of social contact and a forty-eight-hour pre-Review quiet period, but the gap is real. Black-market countertech exploits this.
7. **It cannot remove a memory the citizen has been pharmacologically primed to retain.** This is the seam the resistance has crawled into.

## The countertech — what Maren (or her network) has used

The black-market drug is called paramnestine, street name **stitch**. It is taken as a sublingual film in the days before a scheduled Review. Mechanism: a beta-adrenergic potentiator combined with a protein synthesis stabilizer that locks recent engrams against the Lethonal-7 disruption window. Stitch does not block the Review; it makes the targeted day flicker back, fragmentary and out of order, in the weeks after. The citizen wakes up nominally erased and then, over days, remembers in pieces.

- **Cost of stitch:** liver damage with repeated use (a stitch user past thirty starts to yellow). It also produces vivid, partially false reconstructions — the brain fills gaps with plausible material, and the user cannot reliably tell which fragments are real. A stitch user's testimony is unreliable on principle.
- **Hard limits of stitch:** it only works against an imminent Review. It cannot restore memory already taken in previous years. It is single-use per Review. It cannot restore the day; it can only let the user re-encode fragments as they resurface. Crucially, **it does not tell the user which day was the targeted one** — the user remembers fragments of the taken day mixed with fragments of the days around it, with no internal flag.
- **Distribution:** a film no larger than a postage stamp, smuggled inside the foil-lined wrappers of legal cigarettes (the brand is **Mareth's Black**, a State-licensed cheap cigarette). Possession is a Class A offense; the State executes for distribution, not for use.

## Sael's polygraph implant — the Truth Lattice

Sael is fitted with a Truth Lattice (internal designation TL-7v): a sub-dermal sensor mesh over the left jaw and temple that reads micro-expression activity, sub-vocal pharyngeal tension, and facial blood-flow asymmetries. Output is delivered as a soft pressure on the implanted node behind his ear — a tap-code that distinguishes confident truth, hedge, suppression, and fabrication. He has been trained for six years to read the taps.

- **Cost:** chronic jaw inflammation; about a fifth of Truth Lattice operators develop trigeminal pain that does not respond to standard analgesics. Sael takes a Wing-issued opioid, **carrothenidine**, two tablets at the start of each shift. He does not consider himself dependent. He is.
- **Hard limits:**
  - The Lattice reads bodies, not minds. It cannot distinguish a true belief from a confident lie told by someone who believes it. Sociopaths and the religious occasionally defeat it. So do people on certain beta-blockers.
  - It cannot operate at distance. The subject must be within about three meters and facing him at close to thirty degrees of frontal angle.
  - It is heuristic, not deterministic. The Wing's official line is 94% accuracy on hostile interrogations. The actual rate is closer to 82%, a number Sael privately knows from one stolen audit.
  - It fatigues. After roughly forty minutes of active reading, the operator's interpretive accuracy drops sharply. Long interviews are conducted in three-person rotations.
  - It cannot be turned off. When Sael is at dinner with a colleague, he is reading her. He has stopped having close friends.

## Failure modes

- **Bleed.** Roughly 0.4% of Reviews leave the citizen with fragmentary intrusive imagery from the targeted day, surfacing months later as nightmares or sensory flashes. The Wing has a follow-up protocol; the citizen is brought back in and re-threaded. About a third of these patients become Bleeders, chronic — the engrams have distributed too widely to fully ablate. Bleeders are quietly retired to long-term care facilities in the outer ring.
- **Smearing.** Aggressive or repeated procedures damage adjacent days, eroding two or three days on either side of the target. A smeared citizen has a five-day hole and a noticeable disorientation. About one in eight thousand annual Reviews.
- **Cascade.** Rare (estimated single digits per year, citywide). The reactivation triggers a chain that ablates a wider memory network; the citizen wakes without the previous six months, or without the names of family members. Cascade patients are not returned to public life. The Wing's official cause of death for them is cardiac, after a brief illness.
- **Refusal-response.** A small subset of brains, when threaded, generate a defensive consolidation surge that locks the targeted memory rather than losing it. The citizen wakes up with the day intact and a deep, articulate hatred of the State. The Wing screens for the markers of refusal-response in the pre-Review intake. They miss some. Maren's mother, in the unsurfaced history, was one they missed.
