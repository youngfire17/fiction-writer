# Iceberg

Use for: every subagent, every chapter. These facts are canonical and binding. They will not appear on-page in book 1 except as faint pressure — a sentence here, an inference there. The drafter must not contradict them. The plotter may seed clues toward any of them but may not resolve them within this book. Items are numbered for reference.

## 1. The Director of the Wing is a person, and is Andren Sault

Andren Sault did not die. The official record marks his death in 2031 (a quiet retirement followed by an unremarkable funeral in Hael-by-Plate). He is alive, in his late seventies, and operates as the Director of the Wing's internal directorate, working from Floor Minus Eleven of the Wing's building. Senior staff refer to him only by an internal designation — **the Chair** — and almost none of them have met him. He was the architect of the Cognitive Hygiene Bureau, the unwritten primary author of the Concord Charter, and the founder of the program category called Wing-Internal (see item 3). His office is reached by a single dedicated lift requiring two-key clearance. The Chair has approved every Wing-Internal cartridge personally for thirty years.

No character in book 1 will meet Sault. His name may appear once or twice (Charter context, an old plaque, a missing monument) and will not be paid off here. He is the book-2 antagonist.

## 2. The Wing exists in other cities

Halvern-Estre is not the only city with a Wing. There are at least four others, in cities the bible names but does not place on a map yet: **Korr-Astane**, **Vetreva**, **Esmer-Pavette**, **Loharn**. They are coordinated by a body whose name has not appeared in citizen-facing literature, the **Concord Inter-Cities Liaison**. The Wings cooperate on rare cross-city memory crime cases and share certain biometric standards. Halvern-Estre's Wing is the oldest and the methodological lead. The Director-equivalents in the other cities answer, informally, to Sault.

In book 1 no Wing other than Halvern-Estre's appears on the page. The drafter may, at most, allow a single passing reference to a "Korr-Astane intake protocol" if the moment is right; it is not necessary.

## 3. Wing-Internal is a real category

The class of erasure that, officially, does not exist is internally called **Wing-Internal** (W-I) cartridges. These are Reviews administered to **citizens at the State's discretion outside the annual cadence**, used to erase specific events the State judges politically catastrophic — witnesses to Bureau-era documents, off-the-books deaths in Wing custody, the rare citizen who stumbles into a piece of the Cold Archive. W-I cartridges are built by a small team on Floor Minus Eight, signed off by the Chair, and administered in a theater on Floor Minus Eleven that does not appear on any building plan.

The body in the alley was a W-I technician. He had begun keeping his own date-journal. Someone in the Wing — not Sault, not yet identified — wanted him removed and wanted Maren framed for it because Maren was already flagged as a refusal-response carrier. The setup is a corrective action against two problems at once. Sael is assigned to her because Sael is also a problem someone in the Wing wants resolved — see item 7.

The book will surface the existence of W-I as the back-half reveal. The book will not name Sault or the Chair. The book will end with the protagonists understanding *that* W-I exists. They will not yet know *who runs it*.

## 4. The rest of the world thinks Halvern-Estre is a public health success

Outside the Cordon — and outside this book — there is a world of nations that have not adopted the Wing model. They watch Halvern-Estre with a mixture of envy and unease. Trade with the city is light; diplomatic exchange is formal and cool. The international consensus is that Halvern-Estre's crime rate, suicide rate, and reported civic satisfaction are extraordinary, and that the Concord model is an interesting experiment that has not yet been judged.

No outside-world character appears in book 1. The Cordon is the edge of the readable world for now.

## 5. What is beyond the Cordon

Forest, the foothills of the range, a state highway running east-west, the ruins of two settlements that were part of Halvern's pre-Charter hinterland and which were depopulated in the Long Damage and never resettled. The depopulation is publicly attributed to Plate Fever. It was, in fact, a Bureau decision to clear the area for early human trials. The ruins are not on official maps. A character who has been outside the Cordon (none in book 1) would recognize them.

## 6. Maren's mother

Maren's mother, **Eira**, was a refusal-response case — a Review that locked rather than erased, leaving her with an intact day and an articulate hatred of the State. She was not killed. The Wing observed her. She was, for fourteen years, an unwitting longitudinal study subject: the Wing wanted to know how a refusal-response carrier raised a child. Eira passed her resistance pattern to Maren — not genetically (the response is not heritable in that sense) but pedagogically. She taught Maren, without saying so, the habits of mind that make stitch work. Eira died when Maren was twelve in a cascade event during what was officially Eira's fifteenth Review. Maren believes her mother died of cardiac failure. The cascade was deliberate.

This is the personal-loss beat for Maren that the drafter must protect. It surfaces in book 1 as suspicion, not certainty.

## 7. Sael's erased days are not what he believes they were

Sael has been Reviewed three times in his life so far (twice as a ward, once as an Inquisitor — the Wing reviews its own staff at non-annual cadence). He believes the taken days were operational matters — interrogations whose content the Wing deemed unsuitable for him to carry. In fact, one of the three was the day in his seventh year on which he met his mother again. His mother is alive. She is in House Andren in Vell-Outer as a registered Bleeder. The Wing placed Sael in State wardship at age three under a false circumstance-of-orphaning. Someone in the Wing — possibly the same person who set up the W-I technician's death — wants Sael to discover this, eventually, because Sael's awakening is part of a longer internal play against the Chair.

This is the personal-loss beat for Sael. The book will plant clues. The book will not resolve.

## 8. The stitch supply chain leads upward, not downward

The black-market drug network that distributes paramnestine is not, as Inquisitors are trained to assume, an organic counterculture phenomenon. The synthesis pathway for paramnestine requires precursors only available through State-licensed pharmaceutical channels. Stitch is produced, ultimately, inside the same Lowmaren manufactories that produce Lethonal-7. Someone inside the Ministry of Public Cognition tolerates the leak. The plausible reading is that the Ministry uses the stitch network as a tracer — citizens who buy stitch self-identify as resistance-prone and are flagged. The deeper reading is that someone in the Ministry is running a long game against the Chair using the stitch network as the channel. This someone is not yet named in the bible and will not be named in book 1.

## 9. The Cold Archive is not destroyed

The Wing's official position is that threaded engram data is destroyed within ninety days of each Review. In fact, the data is retained in the Cold Archive on Floor Minus Eight. Every day taken from every citizen since 2019 is in there, indexed. This is the Wing's most carefully guarded fact. A leak of the existence of the Cold Archive would end the Concord. Book 1 may surface that the Archive exists. Book 2 is what happens when someone tries to read it.

## 10. The Truth Lattice has a back-channel

Sael's Truth Lattice does not just deliver tap-codes to him. It also broadcasts an encrypted summary of every reading to a Wing supervisor in real time. Sael does not know this. He has been told the Lattice is a closed loop. It is not. Every interrogation he conducts is being audited by someone above him. This will become significant in book 2 when he begins lying to himself.

## 11. Halvern-Estre's name

The compound name is pre-Charter. **Halvern** was the historical city. **Estre** was a separate village absorbed in the 19th century. The hyphenation is a Charter-era affectation — the old documents say *Halvern and Estre* — adopted to suggest civic unity. Citizens shorten it to **Halvern** or, in Marrow argot, simply **the Plate**. This is canon for any dialogue.

## 12. The fish

The Plate's fish populations have changed measurably since the chiller compressors began venting. The Wing-side shore fish are warmer-water species not native to the lake. Lowmaren families have a folk taboo against eating them; the taboo is not in any literature and the State has never acknowledged it. This is texture, not plot, but the drafter may use it.
