## Premise

Once a year, the city votes on who deserves to die. Anonymous ballots, publicly tabulated. The top seven names face the Ceremony — a public execution by a method drawn by lot, seven days after the names are read. The system has existed for a hundred years. Citizens trust it. The poor especially trust it: it's the one place their voice matters.

Erys is seventeen. A baker's apprentice. Low-tier. She has never done anything to anyone.

On the morning the names are read, she finds her own name at the top of the list — with more votes than any name in the Ceremony's recorded history.

She doesn't know anyone who hates her. She doesn't know anyone who would notice her enough to hate her.

Each name on the list is allowed one Petitioner — a citizen who, between the reading and the Ceremony, can lobby publicly to commute the verdict, with proof of error. Each year, between zero and three of the seven are saved this way. The Petitioner system is the only release valve in the ritual. By the morning of day three, Erys discovers that her actual Petitioner cannot file for her. He is already bound to another case — by his own filed Intent to Represent, signed three weeks before the Reading on the basis of a fabricated case file delivered to him through routes he trusted. The case is a trap and he is in it. Whoever wants her dead did not just want her dead. They used the Petitioner's own hand against him, knowing the slot rule would do the rest.

The man who would have been her Petitioner is the most decorated Petitioner of the last decade. A former public defender. He has saved more names from the list than anyone alive. He has never met her. He noticed her name in a rumor-column item before the Reading, but by long personal discipline he never files for a named citizen until the list is read. He intended to file for her on the morning of Day 0. He could not.

She has six days. Then five. Then four.

## Theme

What is justice in a system that produces it through votes? When the system has been right enough, often enough, that people have trusted it for a hundred years — what happens when one wrong verdict reveals what was always true: that any system this efficient at killing has been gamed since its inception?

Secondary theme: when a stranger has chosen to save you before you knew you needed saving — who do you become to deserve it?

## Promise to the reader

- A countdown that tightens every chapter. Seven days, six, five — and every chapter has a hook that makes the next one unrefusable.
- A genuine mystery solved on-page, not hand-waved: who put Erys on the list (the Architect, through Sailmaker's parish clerk-clusters), how the Petitioner was trapped (a manufactured-case bait three weeks before the Reading), why she specifically was the target (her mother kept a ledger of the same kind of manipulation fourteen years ago and the Architect met her before her death), and who benefits (the Architect and the wider network the book only glimpses). The deepest layer — that the Architect arranged her mother's death — is not resolved in book 1.
- Two protagonists who are NOT enemies but whose investigation methods clash — Erys's terror-driven momentum vs. the Petitioner's quirky, infuriating, eccentric process. An antagonist whose POV the reader gets in small surgical doses without ever being named.
- Set-pieces that breathe on the page: the morning of the reading, public Petitioner hearings, the night someone tries to kill Erys before the Ceremony can, the vote-buyer's parlour, the morning of the Ceremony.
- A protagonist whose competence grows on the page. Erys is not a chosen one. She has nothing in her by chapter 1 except panic. By chapter 25 she has become someone capable. The reader watches it happen.
- An ending that closes the Ceremony question at the level of *this* year's verdict but opens the wider one — has this been done before? Is this the only city with the system? Setup for book 2 without cheating book 1.

## What this book is NOT

- Not paranormal, supernatural, occult, or fantasy-with-magic. The Ceremony is a human institution with rituals, rules, and costs. The Petitioner system is a legal mechanism. No spirits, no chosen-one prophecies, no power-from-the-bloodline.
- Not a YA romance. Erys and the Petitioner share charged proximity and grudging trust; it does not resolve in this book and is not the engine.
- Not a procedural. The plot is one rapidly escalating week. There are no side cases.
- Not a literary novel about grief and memory (the Forgetting Wing miss). The prose serves propulsion, not virtuosity. Every chapter ends on a hand on the next chapter's throat.

## Comparable titles

- **Death Note** (Ohba/Obata) — the Light/L dynamic, dual prodigies on opposite sides of a moral question, mental cat-and-mouse with surgical antagonist-POV scenes. Prose-equivalent.
- **Mirai Nikki / The Future Diary** (Esuno) — the name-on-the-list survival-clock engine, the system that has decided you die.
- **The Promised Neverland** (Shirai/Demizu, first arc) — the kid in a system that has been lying about its purpose; the discovery, the planning, the cost.
- **Liar Game** (Kaitani) — the trap that looks fair, the legalistic engine that hides the real game.
- **Babel** (R.F. Kuang) — for prose register: propulsive, declarative, set-pieces breathe, ideas dramatized through action.
- **Red Rising** (Pierce Brown, book 1) — for pacing and voice: first-arc Darrow's hunger and panic; sentences that move; every chapter ends in the next chapter's mouth.

## POV plan

- **Erys (primary, ~55%).** Close-3rd, past tense. Sentence length skews short to medium; doctrinal fragments allowed but not as a constant tic. She doesn't think in lists or numbers — she thinks in *next steps*. Voice register: a working-class seventeen-year-old whose interiority is action-first, plan-first, fear-checked. When she goes quiet on the page, the reader feels the gear-shift.
- **The Petitioner (~35%).** Close-3rd, past tense, but with a *distinct* register that crackles. He is L-coded: eccentric, hard to place an age on, with a precise physical tic (TBD by character-designer — something concrete: maybe he only eats one specific food in interrogations, maybe he never sits in a chair the normal way, maybe he speaks in questions almost exclusively). His interior monologue carries quirks Erys's doesn't: italicized intrusive thoughts allowed, present-tense interior asides allowed, micro-tangents about the system's history allowed. The reader should know whose POV they're in within the first paragraph.
- **The Conspirator (~10%, 3-4 interludes).** Close-3rd but **anonymous** — never named on-page, never given physical specificity that would let the reader picture them, never given relationships that locate them. Voice goal: the reader feels the architect's pleasure in their work without ever quite seeing the face. The interludes are surgical: each one raises the dread (here is something being prepared, here is a phone call being made, here is a person being paid) without giving Erys/Petitioner what they need to win. Distribution rough plan: ch ~3 (introduction by action — the bait already in motion), ch ~12 (mid-book, learning of a night attempt against Erys he did not order, moving to contain its consequences without exposing his hand), ch ~22 (late, a threat to his plan), ch ~28 (the Ceremony day — final move).

## Voice contract (binding)

Lessons learned from *The Forgetting Wing*. The plotter, drafter, and voice-scrubber are bound by these:

1. **Propulsive over virtuosic.** No three pages of sensory cataloguing before something happens. Chapter 1 opens with action and the protagonist *moves* by paragraph 5 at latest.
2. **No body-position-on-waking opener.** Every Erys chapter must open with action, dialogue, image-with-implication, or a hard fact landing — not with her noticing her own body parts in order.
3. **Sentences move.** Bimodal toward shorter on average than Forgetting Wing — mean sentence ~12-16 words, not 22+. Long sweeps reserved for set-piece pauses.
4. **No McCarthy register.** No "the cold had had time" virtuoso sentences as the chapter's center of gravity. They can land occasionally as accents. They are not the texture.
5. **Comp prose register:** Pierce Brown (*Red Rising*, first arc — first-person hunger), R.F. Kuang (*Babel*, *The Poppy War* first arc — ideas through action), Brandon Sanderson on his cleaner days (efficient, action-forward), light-novel-translation Death Note (pacy, direct).
6. **Every chapter ends on a hook.** Image, line, threat, decision, reveal. Not on theme. The reader should never be able to close the book at a chapter break without effort.
7. **Mean chapter length 2.7-3.0k.** Cap soft 3.2k. Forgetting Wing's 3.4k chapter-1 was at the ceiling — we will not repeat that. Tighter chapters mean more cliffhangers per hundred pages.
8. **`library/voice/anime-thriller.md` still applies** for the high-level register goals (close POV, internal monologue forward, action choreography in granular beats, named-techniques-and-systems-in-character, numeric specificity). The deltas above sharpen the execution.
9. **`library/anti-ai/tells-catalog.md` still binds.** No banned words, no banned constructions. Codex-review pass before any chapter is locked.

## Locked: the Architect (the conspirator's identity)

**The Architect is the Petitioner's former mentor.**

A revered ex-Petitioner, now in his seventies. He founded the modern Petitioner system fifty years ago and has spent the half-century since being celebrated as the system's moral conscience. He trained the Petitioner-we-know as a young man and remains the only person the Petitioner truly trusts. Erys meets him around day 3-4 at the Petitioner's office, where he has come (he says) to help. He takes her under his wing — warm, fatherly, profound. He sits with her on the worst night. He explains the system's history to her. The reader will love him.

He engineered the trap on the Petitioner. He has been gaming the system for decades — the system he himself founded. His motive operates at the level of conviction, not greed: he believes the Ceremony serves a function the public would never accept, and Erys's death is necessary to preserve it. The specific link (why *Erys*, why now) is locked in `series-bible/world/iceberg.md`: she is the daughter of Lirien Maris, a parish-charity records-keeper who fourteen years ago kept a ledger documenting the same kind of clerk-cluster manipulation the Architect has been running for fifty years. The Architect arranged her death; her three-year-old daughter survived; the daughter is now seventeen, and a newly-appointed Examining Magistrate is sharp enough to pull the thread if it is ever pulled.

**He is the conspirator-interlude POV.** All 3-4 anonymous interludes are him. The interludes show what the Architect *does* (engineers a manufactured-case bait for his protégé, learns of a night attempt against Erys he did not order and quietly moves to contain its consequences, watches the Petitioner's window from his garden, receives a coded message at dinner) without ever naming him, describing him physically, or locating him in a way that would let a reader connect him to the on-page mentor. The interlude voice must NOT match the mentor's on-page voice exactly — but it should not contradict it either; the careful reader, on a re-read, should be able to feel that the two voices were always the same person.

**Craft contract — binding on the drafter and the voice-scrubber:**

1. **Every on-page scene with the mentor must read both ways.** Warm and lethal, simultaneously. After the reveal, a re-read must find the second meaning in lines that, on first read, played as comfort. Example: "you do not have to be brave today; bravery is what we save for the morning" reads as fatherly comfort on day five and reads as the architect's foreknowledge of the Ceremony morning on re-read.
2. **His love for both the Petitioner and Erys is REAL, not performed.** He is not a sociopath wearing a mask. He genuinely cares for them. That is what makes the betrayal land. He is killing someone he loves to preserve something he loves more.
3. **He is the system's true believer.** From his POV he is the protagonist. The book grants him no monologue at the climax. He does not explain himself in soliloquy. The reader pieces his conviction together from his interludes and his actions; Erys and the Petitioner, at the climax, do not get the satisfaction of his confession.
4. **He is on-page in chapter 5 at the latest.** The reveal cannot work if he arrives in act 2.
5. **The climax reveal lands without dialogue.** Morning of the Ceremony: the Petitioner walks Erys into the plaza. The mentor is in the front row. He meets her eyes. He smiles at her with love. She understands.

**Why this works (for the drafter to keep in working memory):** the strongest betrayal-reveals are not "the warm character was secretly evil" — they are "the warm character's love was real AND the betrayal was real, and both are true at the same time." Promised Neverland's Mama Isabella. Askeladd in Vinland Saga. The reader's first thought on reveal is not "I should have seen it" but "I want to re-read those scenes." Every mentor scene must be written to survive that re-read.

## Open notes for the plotter

- **The climax should be the Ceremony morning, not the day before.** Erys and the Petitioner have to walk her *into* the Ceremony plaza — because she chooses to — having put the proof in motion that will save her not by the Petitioner's voice but by the city's. The Death Note Light-vs-L convention: the protagonists choose the field of battle.
- **The mid-book inversion (≈ ch 13-14) is the discovery that the second-slot purchase is not the conspiracy's *first* move — it is the *third*.** The reader and the protagonists discover together that this has happened before to other names on lists in other years, and the system has been gamed since at least a decade ago.
- **Mandatory set-pieces:** (a) the Reading of the List in the public square; (b) the moment Erys realizes she cannot file her Petitioner — the legal hearing she attends only to be told the slot is taken; (c) a night attempt on her life by someone trying to make sure the Ceremony does not happen at all; (d) the vote-buyer's parlour scene where someone will sell Erys the name behind the purchase, for what she will not pay; (e) the Ceremony morning.
- **Plant three book-2 setups** (do not pay off in book 1):
  - The system exists in other cities.
  - The Petitioner has a previous case he lost — the loss defines him; the loser's story will return in book 2.
  - The actual architect — the person at the top of the conspiracy, not the operator we see — is referenced obliquely (a phrase, a sigil, a habit the conspirator-POV character mentions) but never identified.
- **The Petitioner's quirk is load-bearing.** The character-designer should commit to it early and the drafter must honor it every chapter. L's sweets habit is not a tic — it is a *signature*. Our Petitioner's signature should be equally specific.
- **Genre-mandatory beats from `library/genre/thriller.md`:** ticking clock (built into the premise), escalating stakes (every day shortens), end-of-chapter hooks (per voice contract above), at least one scene of physical action (the night attempt on Erys), set-piece climax (the Ceremony).
- **POV-distinction self-check:** the strip-tags test from `library/character/voice-and-dialogue.md` must pass for all three POVs. A reader should know within the first paragraph of any chapter whose head they are in — without dialogue tags, without a header, without the name being mentioned.

## Series scope (open-ended)

- **Book 1 (this book):** Close the Ceremony case. Save Erys (or fail to, leaving a small ember of resistance). Expose that the system has been gamed, at least in this city. Erys and the Petitioner end on uneasy alliance — bound by what they now know, not by what they feel.
- **Book 2 (provisional):** The city's Ceremony system as a thread in a wider net. Other cities. The architect's identity. The Petitioner's old case.
- **Book 3+ (open):** Leave room. The plotter for THIS book plants book-2 setups but does not over-commit the series arc. Book 1 must land on its own.
