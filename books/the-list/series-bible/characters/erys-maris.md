# Erys Maris

role: protagonist
pov: yes
arc: positive
first appearance: chapter 1

## Want / Need

- **Want (external):** Get her name off the list. Live to see Frostmonth 9. Concretely, in the first half of the book: find a Petitioner who will file. After Day 3: prove the fraud well enough that the city itself commutes her by public force.
- **Need (internal):** Accept that being unseen is not the same as being safe. She has spent seven years (since her father died) making herself invisible — useful to Holbren, no trouble to the parish, no entry in any record except the bare-minimum voter-roll. The list found her anyway. She has to learn to *speak* — to be visible on her own terms — in a culture that rewards stoicism in the poor and that has trained her, specifically, to keep her mouth shut.

## Wound / Lie / Ghost

- **Wound:** Triple bereavement before she was eleven. Mother at three (Lirien Maris, recorded as fever). Godmother at eight. Father at ten (Holm Sailer, fever, real). Two years in the parish charity-house. The compounding fact, not any one of them: every adult who loved her left, and the parish noticed her only when there were forms to fill out.
- **Lie:** *If I take up no room, nothing more will be taken from me.* The parish-charity ledger taught her she was a body in a bed and a name on a chit, and Holbren's bakery taught her she could earn quiet by being useful before being asked. The lie has kept her alive at the bottom of a hard city. It is also why the Architect could put her at the top of a list and no one noticed she was a person.
- **Ghost:** Age ten. The afternoon after her father's funeral. The parish bell-keeper handed her a folded paper with her new ward address on it and waited, patiently, for her to read it back. She could read it. She didn't. She kept her mouth shut while a kind man wrote *unable to read* in the margin and then signed the chit. She has not corrected the parish record since.

## Competence / Contradiction / Flaw

- **Competence:** Five years in a working bakery. She is *physically* competent in a way the Brass Ward and Pearl Quarter underestimate. She can be on her feet for fourteen hours, lift a sack of flour, judge a dough by the way it pulls against her thumb, count change in pennies at speed, navigate the Low Quays' alley grid in the dark. She also reads better than her parish chit says — slowly, mouthing words, but she can read a broadsheet and the back of a ballot sleeve. (This is the small competence the plot will tax: she can read evidence the Architect assumed she could not.)
- **Contradiction:** A working-class girl raised to civic deference who, when cornered, has a flat refusal she did not know she owned. She has folded blank ballots in two of the last three Ceremonies — not from political principle but because she could not bring herself to name a person. The book reveals that refusal as the seed of who she becomes.
- **Flaw:** She trusts kindness without auditing it. The Architect's warmth on Day 4 lands on her like the first uncomplicated kindness she has had from an adult since Holbren took her in — and Holbren's is gruff. Saerin's is gentle. She will let him sit closer than is safe. This is the flaw the antagonist exploits in plain sight.

## Voice
- **vocabulary:** Concrete, plain, quay register. Words from the bakery and the parish ('leaven', 'tally', 'sleeve', 'chit', 'pennies', 'Tellingday'). She does not use Brass Ward words ('procedure', 'cluster', 'remit') until late in the book, and when she does, the borrowing is visible — the reader feels her reaching for a tool.
- **sentence length:** Short to medium. Mean ~10-13 words. Fragments are allowed under fear or focus, not as a constant tic. When she goes long, the sentence is *doing* something — climbing a stair, threading an alley — not reflecting.
- **avoids talking about:** Her mother. Her father. The charity-house. Being scared. The parish word for the kind of girl she has been — *unparished* — which she has never said aloud. She will say *I don't know* a great deal, even when she does.
- **speech tics:** Says *aye* (Sailmaker's parish flatness) where Brass Ward speakers say *yes*. Counts pennies under her breath when she is working a problem ("ninety, ninety-one, ninety-two..."), substituting for the breath-counting she would do if she had been taught to. Will not swear. The one time she does (late book), the drafter is to treat as an arc beat, not flavor.
- **internal monologue:** Thinks in *next steps*, not lists. *The window. The latch. The stair.* Plan-first, fear-checked. Does not narrate her feelings to herself. Reader infers state from the choice she takes.

## Physical

Five foot two. Lean — the lean of a working teenager, not a starved one; Holbren feeds his apprentices. Hair the color of wet straw, kept short and tied back with twine because long hair near the oven catches. A flour-burn scar across the back of her right hand from a sliding peel three winters ago — pale, raised, the shape of a crescent. Eyes grey-green; her mother's mouth (the Architect will see this on Day 4 and not look away fast enough). Hands hard with oven-callus and rope-callus from hauling flour sacks; nails kept short. Wears the same two aprons in rotation, one grey, one bleached out from washing.

## Arc beats

- **Planted (ch 1, the morning of the Reading):** The lie in action. Erys hears her own name read at the top of the list and her first impulse is *do not make a scene* — she stands quiet in the crowd, hands flat at her sides, while the city looks for the girl whose name was just read. Holbren finds her. He guides her out of the square. She does not cry until they are back in the bakery's flour-store.
- **Tested (ch 11-14, the lapsed filing and the Crooked Rule):** The lie keeps producing losses. The afternoon of Day 3 she watches her Petitioner-slot lapse because she did not know how to be *loud enough* in the right rooms early enough. At the Crooked Rule she is offered the truth at a price (Vell's cost) and she refuses — not from courage yet, but because she can't yet pay it. The refusal is the hinge: she has begun, for the first time, to choose *what* she will not say rather than to default to silence.
- **Resolved (ch ~25, the Plinth at dawn):** She speaks. From the Petitioners' Plinth, in front of the city, by procedural right under the Charter, in language she has been gathering for six days. She does not perform. She tells the city what she knows the way she would tell Holbren the bread is burning. The competence is visible. The lie is broken. She does not get to be invisible again.

## Knowledge (at start of book)

- Her name, her age, her trade, her parish, her voter-roll number, her ward address.
- That her mother died of fever when she was three. That her father died of fever when she was ten. That she has no living blood kin.
- The Ceremony's public procedures at the level a quay-folk seventeen-year-old learns by attending and by gossip: the Reading is at dawn, the list is sealed, the Petitioner system exists, Petitions sometimes succeed, the Bowl is drawn by the named.
- That she has folded a blank ballot in two of the last three Ceremonies and that this was a private decision she has not told Holbren.
- She does NOT know: that her mother kept a ballot-pattern ledger. That her mother met Saerin Korran twice before her death. That the *unable to read* notation followed the chit into her parish-charity file and from there into her current parish-roll record — she remembers the bell-keeper writing it, and she remembers not correcting him, and she assumes the notation was specific to that day and that chit. That Tomas Veren noticed her name in *The Common Hour* nine days before the book opens. That she has ever been on anyone's radar.
