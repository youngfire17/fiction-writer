# Tomas Veren

role: protagonist (co-lead) — the Petitioner-we-know
pov: yes
arc: positive (held under a flat surface — see below)
first appearance: chapter 2

## Naming convention

- Public address: *Petitioner Veren*, in broadsheets and at the Hall.
- The Architect calls him *Veren* (surname, the way old teachers do), never *Tomas* in front of others, and only *Tomas* twice in the whole book — once in a private moment of real tenderness on Day 4, once at the climax when the second meaning hits.
- Erys calls him *Veren* once she has decided he is hers; she addresses him as *sir* on Day 3 and stops on Day 5.
- Madam Vell calls him *the green-door man.*
- He calls himself, in interior monologue, *Veren* — he has spent fifteen years working *as* his title.

## Want / Need

- **Want (external):** Save the next name. Always the next one. In this book, specifically: get out from under the manufactured-case slot, file the truth about Erys, and pull the trap inside-out. He cannot file. He spends the book trying to find every other instrument the Charter allows.
- **Need (internal):** Accept that the system he loves was *built* to be gamed by the man who built it. He has spent twelve years filing inside an apparatus his teacher designed; he has assumed, the way the city has assumed, that the apparatus is the thing. The Need is to choose Erys over the apparatus when the choice arrives — and the climax is constructed so the choice arrives without the apparatus's permission.

## Wound / Lie / Ghost

- **Wound:** Veris Calden, three years ago. He filed clean, spoke for an hour, produced the parish-clerk's ledger; the Examining Magistrate ruled against him; Calden drew the wheel on Frostmonth 8 and died on the trestle in Argent Square while Veren stood in the front of the crowd. He has reviewed his hour of speech a hundred times and not understood. The wound is the *not-understanding* — the case was clean and it lost, and his entire methodology depends on the case being the load-bearing variable.
- **Lie:** *If I prepare the case well enough, the system rules correctly.* The Calden case did not break this lie because he has not yet faced the reason it failed (that he was beaten by his teacher's twenty-year cultivation of the bench, not by his own preparation). He has folded the Calden loss into the lie as *I prepared insufficiently*, and his subsequent practice has been more careful, more thorough, more refusing to take a case he can't win. He files fewer cases than he used to. He wins more of them. The book breaks the lie by showing him a case where preparation is not the variable — the variable is the teacher.
- **Ghost:** A dramatized image, late book: the Architect, in the rain outside the Petitioner's Hall, walking him home in silence five years ago after the riot-printer case. He has carried this scene as the warmest moment of his adult life — the silence was acceptance, the rain was theatre, the hand on his shoulder at the door was *I am proud of you*. On re-read after the climax, the same scene means *I am still teaching you.* It was the night the Architect decided, looking at how trusting his protégé was, that the protégé could be baited.

## Competence / Contradiction / Flaw

- **Competence:** The highest save-rate of any active Petitioner. He builds a case the way a watchmaker builds a movement — small parts, fitted, tested, the whole timed against the public lobby. He is unusually good at *parish-clerk-cluster forensics*: given the raw tallies and the parish-bell-keeper's daily roll, he can find the cluster signature of organized voting in an hour. (This will matter in the back half — he will recognize the Sailmaker's cluster signature on Erys's tally and will not, at first, recognize where he has seen the same hand before.)
- **Contradiction:** A Brass Ward solicitor with quay-folk eating habits. He took his food register from his father (a Sailmaker's parish rope-walker; the family's middling-tier ascent came through Veren's scholarship at sixteen, not through trade) and never lost it. The class-marker mismatch reads visibly on the page: his coat is correct, his diction is correct, his lunch is rye and anchovy on a folded broadsheet. The Architect, who built him, encouraged the retention as a kind of authenticity. Veren has not asked why.
- **Flaw:** He loves his teacher more than he loves his own judgment. He has trained, for fifteen years, to defer to Saerin Korran on procedural questions. The book's central pressure on his arc is that *every check he might run to confirm what is happening is a check he is psychologically incapable of running against his teacher's name*. The Architect is not in his suspect set, structurally. The reader feels the missing column.

## Voice
- **vocabulary:** Brass Ward solicitor's professional register, layered over a quay-folk substrate that surfaces under fatigue or feeling. Says *cluster*, *Intent*, *remit*, *parish-roll signature*, *clerk-written tally* with the casual familiarity of someone who works with them daily. Says *aye* once or twice when tired (his father's word) and notices himself doing it.
- **sentence length:** Bimodal. Short clipped interrogatives when working a case (often three or four in a row). Long winding sentences when explaining a procedural mechanism — he genuinely loves the architecture and the love comes through as length. Mean ~14-17 words across both modes; the bimodality is the voice signature.
- **avoids talking about:** The Calden case unless prompted. His mother (deceased, no story-load). His own age (he is 37 and looks 32, and dislikes both readings). His teacher's reputation — he does not perform admiration; he assumes it is shared.
- **speech tics:** Asks questions when uncertain, declares when certain — inverted from the cultural norm. ("Was it the same clerk?" not "It must have been the same clerk." / "It was a manufactured case." not "Don't you think it was a manufactured case?") This is the speech-pattern half of his L-coding.
- **physical tic (working-state):** **Never sits in a chair the normal way.** Sits on the back-rail with his feet on the seat. Sits on the table with his feet on the chair. Sits on the floor with the chair turned around and his elbows on the seat-back. On the rare occasions he sits normally — the climax, the Architect's first on-page visit, the moment Erys realizes she is going to trust him — it is a tell the drafter must deploy.
- **food tic (working-state):** Eats only one thing while working a case: cold rye toast spread with anchovy paste, eaten standing, off a folded square of broadsheet. He does not eat anything else from the moment he opens a file until the moment the case rules. The house-keeper has stopped trying. Erys, the first time she sees the toast on the workroom table, will read it as a quay-folk meal and reassess him.
- **internal monologue:** Crackles. Italicized intrusive thoughts allowed: *Wrong shape. Re-cluster.* Present-tense interior asides allowed: *the slot rule is a knife and someone has been holding it for fifty years.* Micro-tangents about the system's history allowed in passing — he genuinely cannot help thinking historically.

## Physical

Five foot ten. Lean in the wrong way — Brass Ward solicitor lean, not athlete lean; he stands with the cant of someone who reads in bad light. Hair black, cut close, going grey at the temples earlier than he has noticed. A small old scar through his left eyebrow (a childhood quayside fight, never explained). Long hands. Always cold. Wears a charcoal coat over a collarless shirt and the same scuffed boots in all weather; one ring on his right hand, a plain iron band that was his father's. The Architect once asked him about the ring and he changed the subject.

## Arc beats

- **Planted (ch 2, opening of Day 0 from his POV):** He intends to file for Erys at the Reading; he has the Intent paper in his coat pocket; he discovers, when he reaches the Petitioner's Hall annex, that his earlier Intent for the Brass Ward citizen is active and binding. The first page of his POV shows him acting on the lie — *I prepared. I will prepare more.* He believes, in chapter 2, that more work will solve this.
- **Tested (ch 12-14, mid-book inversion):** The discovery that this is the *third* such manipulation in a decade, not the first. The teacher who taught him to recognize parish-clerk-cluster signatures was the only person in the city who could draw them this cleanly. He registers the thought once, in ch 13, in a single line he cannot finish, and then he buries it. The reader sees the burial.
- **Resolved (ch ~24-25, the Plinth and after):** He stands beside Erys on the Petitioners' Plinth at dawn on Frostmonth 8 and yields her the first word, which he has never done in twelve years. He speaks second. He names the manufactured-case bait without naming the man who set it (he still cannot). The Architect, in the front row, smiles at Erys. Veren does not see the smile. He sees Erys see it. He understands. The Need is fulfilled in his choice to keep speaking anyway — to finish his argument while looking at the man who made him, and to let his teacher hear him do it.

## Knowledge (at start of book)

- The full public procedure of the Ceremony and the Petitioner system.
- The Sailmaker's parish-clerk-cluster signature pattern, from his own twelve years of practice.
- That his Intent for the Brass Ward citizen was filed three weeks ago in the ordinary course of his practice; he believes the case is genuine. He does not know it is fabricated.
- That he noticed Erys Maris's name in *The Common Hour*'s rumor column nine days ago and intended to file for her at the Reading.
- The Calden case in full procedural detail, and his teacher's training (received over fifteen years) that shaped how he chose, prepared, and filed it. He does not know the magistrate who ruled against him was his teacher's protégé.
- His teacher's reputation, his teacher's biography in its public version, and his teacher's morning routine (tea, broadsheets, garden walk). He does not know about the brass glass on the tripod in the garden.
