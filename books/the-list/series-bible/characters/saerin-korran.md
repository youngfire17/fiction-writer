# Saerin Korran (of the Iron-Lily House) — the Architect

role: antagonist
pov: yes — anonymous-interlude POV only (never named, never located on-page; 3-4 chapters across the book)
arc: flat-and-negative simultaneously (he does not change; the book proves him wrong; he never accepts that he is wrong)
first appearance: ch ~3 (anonymous interlude) — first on-page in-body appearance: ch 5 (Petitioner's office)

## Naming and address conventions

- Personal name: **Saerin** (Brass Ward register; *-an* suffix on the patronymic Korran, his birth-tier surname).
- Birth-tier surname: **Korran** — middling-tier Brass Ward family, his father a senior solicitor at the Counter Square chambers. He kept the surname in legal documents after marrying into the Iron-Lily House; in social usage he is "of the Iron-Lily House."
- Quay-folk call him *the master of the Iron-Lily.*
- Brass Ward folk call him *Saerin Korran* or *the Petitioner-emeritus.*
- Pearl Quarter peers call him *Saerin of the Iron-Lily.*
- The Petitioner-we-know calls him *teacher* — exclusively. Has done so for fifteen years.
- Erys, instructed on Day 4 to call him *Saerin* if she wishes, will not. She calls him *sir* the first day and nothing the second and third.
- In the anonymous interludes the drafter must NEVER use *Saerin*, *Korran*, *the Architect*, *the teacher*, or any noun that locates him. The interludes use only *he*, third-close, and his interior frame.

## The two voices (binding craft contract)

This is the load-bearing voice work in the book. Saerin's voice on-page (mentor scenes with Veren and Erys) and his voice in the interludes are the *same man, in two contexts*. They must be distinguishable enough that the reader does not register the trick on first read, and continuous enough that a careful re-read finds them obviously the same.

### On-page mentor voice
- Warm, fatherly, profound. Speaks more slowly than he thinks. Old enough to have earned slow speech.
- Uses procedural language with love. Will explain the slot rule to Erys with the same attention he would give to a piece of music. He does not condescend.
- Asks questions of others before he tells. Listens with his whole body — the small lean-forward, the hand half-open on the desk. Veren and Erys both register this as the quality that makes him trustworthy. (And he *is* listening; he is not faking. He listens because he needs to know what they know.)
- Does not lie. Will redirect a conversation he does not want to have ("That is a question for another day, Tomas") but will not state a falsehood about a procedural matter. He believes lying about procedure would be a corruption of the apparatus he built. He has no scruple about lying by omission.
- Sentence length: medium-long, periodic, with a tendency to land on a short clean closing. ("The Petitioner's Hall has rules. Some of those rules are mine. They are still the rules.") Reads as warm gravity.
- Vocabulary: educated Brass Ward register elevated by fifty years of Pearl Quarter usage. Says *honorable*, *integrity*, *the spirit of the Charter*, *the public lobby*. Means them. Says *Tomas* twice in the whole book (see ghost beat below).
- Emotional pitch at rest: gentle. Profound. Sorrowful in a way the on-page characters read as wisdom about mortality.

### Interlude voice (anonymous)
- Patient, procedurally elegant. Reads like the voice of someone deeply at home in his own work.
- Numeric specificity *higher* than the on-page voice. He thinks in fourteen years, nine days, three weeks, the second slot. The interludes use Veren's L-coded numerical density but redirected toward administration rather than forensics.
- Sentence length: slightly shorter on average than his on-page voice. Cleaner. A page of the interlude is a page of his actual interior; on-page he is performing warmth (real warmth, but performed for the room he is in), and the performance softens the rhythm. Alone with his work, the rhythm crisps.
- Vocabulary overlap is total — same words, same register — but the *frequency* shifts. Interludes use *seam*, *thread*, *the line*, *the cost*, *the Charter* more often. On-page he uses *honorable*, *integrity*, *brave*, *the public lobby* more often. Both vocabularies are his.
- Emotional pitch at rest: quietly sorrowful, satisfied by precision, not by cruelty. He does not enjoy what he is doing to Erys. He enjoys the fact that he has built an instrument that makes it possible without violating the Charter he wrote.
- Strict prohibitions in interludes: never named, never described physically (no age, no clothes, no eye color); no relational labels that locate him in the cast (banned: *his protégé*, *his student*, *his teacher*, *his housekeeper*, *the girl*, *the Petitioner-we-know*, *Veren*, *Erys*, *Tomas*, *Mira*; required: non-relational functional references — *the man at the green door*, *the girl whose mother kept the ledger*, *the woman who brings the tea*); no setting names that identify the Iron-Lily House (banned outright: *the house*, *Wellspring Road*, *the lilies*, *Iron-Lily*, *the Pearl Quarter*; rationed: *garden* allowed at most once before the reveal, *library* allowed at most once before the reveal, *brass glass* reserved for the final interlude — its first appearance is the reveal beat; *tea* is universal and unrestricted).

The careful re-reader, on second pass, will recognize: the long sentence that ends short, the love of procedure, the *eleven seconds* / *three weeks* / *fourteen years* counting pattern, the use of *seam* in the interlude that he uses once on-page in ch 18.

### The doubleness, made concrete

The Architect's love for Erys is not an act. The on-page warmth is real because the love beneath it is real, and the love beneath it is real because Erys is, in his interior frame, *the daughter he was finally going to picture* — the math collapsing on him after fourteen years of refusal. The book's craft requirement is that the drafter must give the reader, on first read, a Day 4 or Day 5 scene where the Architect could have manipulated Erys tactically (steered her toward a procedural dead-end, advised her in a way that wasted her Day 5, planted a misdirection in her about Veren) — and chose, instead, to simply sit with her. The choice has a small visible tactical cost on his timeline; the reader feels his kindness without yet understanding what it is costing him. After the reveal, the same scene reads as the moment he gave her hours of his own work to spend with her, because the daughter who did not survive would have been thirty-three this Frostmonth and the girl across the room is seventeen and has her mother's mouth. He loves and he kills, and the loving did not stop the killing because his deeper conviction (the Ceremony's secondary function) outweighed even this. That is the doubleness. It is not pretense. It is the moral architecture of his life.

## Want / Need

- **Want (external):** Cut the one external thread that connects any future investigator to Lirien Maris's name. Erys must be on the Plinth at Frostmonth 8 and the Bowl must do its work. Around that core: the Petitioner's slot must be bound elsewhere (engineered three weeks before the Reading); Madam Vell's silence must be procedurally bound (the second-slot option exercised during the Silent Days); his protégé must walk Erys into the plaza personally (this part he does not need but wants — the moral symmetry of the gesture, his student walking his target's daughter into the square, is the kind of completeness Saerin finds satisfying without enjoying).
- **Need (internal):** Accept that the secondary function of the Ceremony — the harbor-economy stabilization the public would never sanction — is not worth Erys's life. He does not, in book 1, accept this. The book denies him the satisfaction of confession; he leaves the climax with his conviction intact and his instrument broken. (The negative half of his arc: the world has proved him wrong, and he refuses to register the proof. The flat half: he is the same man on the last page that he was on the first.)

## Wound / Lie / Ghost

- **Wound:** Three woven wounds, fifty years apart. (1) At twenty-three, he watched the Reapmonth grain-broker hang at the Ceremony after losing his Petition cleanly — the experience that forged the modern Petitioner system. He understood, in that hour at the south flagstones, that the Compact's mechanism was both the city's salvation and its weapon, and that the right hand on the weapon was a moral necessity. (2) At forty, his wife died in childbirth in the Iron-Lily House. The child did not survive — a daughter. The house-keeper found him sitting by the bed at dawn with a closed book in his lap; he did not weep then or later. He has lived alone for thirty-three years and has not allowed himself a private grief he can name. (3) The daughter who did not survive would have been thirty-three at the opening of book 1 if she had lived. Erys is seventeen. He has not allowed the math to surface in his interior in fourteen years; he has not pictured Erys's face once in those fourteen years because he understood, on some level, that the day he was finally going to picture her would be the day a girl arrived on his doorstep — and the day he could not afford a girl on his doorstep was the day he was finally going to take action against the thread. On Day 4, when she enters the Petitioner's office and he sees her face for the first time, he registers — in a single sentence — that she has her mother's mouth, and also that she has the working-class courage of a girl who has lost everything, which his own daughter would have grown into if she had lived. The resonance is not engineered. It is discovered. It is the third wound and it is on him for the rest of the book.
- **Lie:** *The right hand on the weapon is a moral necessity, and I am the right hand.* He believes the Ceremony's stabilizing effect on the Compact has prevented a second Year of Knives for a hundred years; he believes the secondary function (the senior families' quiet pruning of threats to the harbor economy) is the *cause* of the stability rather than a side-effect of it; he believes the alternative is four thousand bodies in twelve months; he believes Erys's death is the price of the alternative not happening. The lie is layered: at the bottom, it is *if I stop, the city falls.* Above it: *if my hand is on the weapon, the hand is at least a good hand.* Above that: *love makes the weapon merciful — I will not pretend I do not love the people I lose.*
- **Ghost:** Twelve years ago. A small private dinner at the Iron-Lily House. The Petitioner-we-know, then twenty-five, two cases in, sat across from him and asked, with the directness Saerin had taught him to ask, *Teacher, what if the right names are sometimes on the list because someone wants them there?* Saerin set down his cup. He smiled. He said, *Tomas, that is the question I built the Petitioner's Hall to ask.* Veren took the answer as approval of his rigor. Saerin took the question as the moment he understood his student was the kind of mind who would, in the wrong year, see him. The Architect's twelve years of careful proximity to Veren since have been the working-out of that dinner.

## Competence / Contradiction / Flaw

- **Competence:** Fifty years of unmatched institutional knowledge of the Ceremony's mechanism — the parish-clerk network, the Examining Magistrate's bench-customs, the Master Teller's seal-routine, the bonded clearing-house family-account architecture, the Crooked Rule's anonymity rules, the Charter's procedural seams. He can move a name into the top seven through Sailmaker's clerk-clusters without the parish committee fully understanding what they did. He can engineer a slot-rule trap on a Petitioner using the Petitioner's own filing habits as the load-bearing tool. He understands the system better than the system understands itself, because he wrote half of it.
- **Contradiction:** A man who loves the public lobby. He wrote the no-enjoinment clause into the Charter fifty years ago and the Ceremony-morning extension into it thirty-five years ago; he did so on principle; he has not changed his mind; the climax uses the clause he wrote to break the instrument he set. He could, in theory, have closed the lobby thirty years ago when he became powerful enough to push the amendment. He did not. He believes the public should never be silenced, even when the public is wrong. This is not a contradiction the book resolves — it is the moral architecture of his betrayal. He genuinely believes both things. The reader feels the pull.
- **Flaw:** He has never, in fifty years, accepted that a single sufficiently competent young person could see him. He plans against magistrates, against committees, against journalists. He plans loosely against his protégé because he loves him and trusts him. He does not plan against Erys at all — she is, in his frame, the cost, not the actor. Both protagonists, by the climax, are doing things he did not model.

## Voice

(See the two-voice contract above. This card is the parameters the drafter consults during scrub.)

- **vocabulary (on-page):** *honorable, the spirit of the Charter, the public lobby, integrity, the Bench, my dear, my boy, Tomas, child, the morning.* He calls Erys *child* once and never again.
- **vocabulary (interlude):** *seam, thread, the line, the cost, the Charter, the second slot, the Sailmaker's roll, fourteen years, three weeks, nine days, the girl whose mother kept the ledger, the man at the green door, the woman who brings the tea, the wrong tincture* (this last word once, in the second interlude, never explained; the careful reader will hold it).
- **sentence length:** on-page medium-long with short closes; interlude shorter and crisper. Both modes use periodic structure with declarative resolution.
- **avoids talking about:** his wife (mentioned once on-page, in a single line on Day 6, without elaboration). The child who did not survive (never on-page). Aldovan Rell (never on-page in book 1). Lirien Maris (never on-page in book 1; in the second interlude he thinks of her mouth once and the reader does not know whose mouth he means until much later).
- **speech tics:** addresses his protégé as *Tomas* only twice in the book — once in real tenderness on Day 4, once at the climax in a way that retrospectively makes the first instance unbearable. Says *the morning* to mean Frostmonth 8 (the day she dies), a habituated phrasing that on first read is fatherly poetics and on re-read is foreknowledge.

## Physical

(In on-page scenes; never in interludes.)

Seventy-three. Tall (six foot two) and standing straight, a little surprisingly for his age. White hair kept short and neat; a close-trimmed beard, white. Eyes pale grey, set deep, the eyebrows still nearly black. A long face, lined around the mouth from years of slight, controlled smiling. Long-fingered hands, always dry-warm, with no rings. Wears a charcoal coat indoors as well as out (the Iron-Lily House runs cold and he likes it that way), a cream linen shirt with a high collar, no tie. A small brass watch on a thin chain in his waistcoat pocket — he checks it once per visit, not from impatience but from habit. Walks with a silver-headed cane he does not need; the cane is old, his father-in-law's, and he carries it as a gesture toward the house he married into. The garden gate of the Iron-Lily House has wrought-iron lilies cradled in an open palm worked into the ironwork. (The sigil. The drafter and continuity-checker handle the three-time plant.)

## Arc beats

- **Planted (ch ~3, first anonymous interlude):** He is already in motion. The bait is filed; the option is purchased; the second-slot citizen is already named on the sealed list. The interlude shows him receiving leaked parish totals from the Hall of Tallies and confirming the work has landed. The reader meets a voice without a face. The voice is *patient, procedurally elegant, quietly sorrowful about the cost.* He thinks once, in a single sentence, about the girl whose mouth he has not yet seen.
- **Tested (ch ~12, second interlude):** The Day 4 night attempt. He learns of it from the woman who brings the tea, carrying morning-market gossip. The attempt was not his order. He is, on the page, calm; the reader feels the temperature drop. The interlude shows him sending — through two layers of intermediation, by the routes he uses — a message to the parish-committee operator at the Sailmaker's Voting House that closes a door he had no intention of leaving open. He does this without naming the operator and without writing anything that could be traced. The reader sees the work as elegance. The reader does not yet know what the work is preventing.
- **Tested again (ch ~22, third interlude):** Erys has visited the Crooked Rule. He learns of it the same day, through Madam Vell's coded receipt of his earlier exercise. He understands, before Veren does, that Vell is going to disclose. Under the Crooked Rule's anonymity-void rule his own act has endangered the parlour, and the rule permits her to give up his name; she has offered Erys a sacrifice price first, which Erys may or may not pay, but either way Vell's controlled disclosure is now a matter of timing rather than possibility. He decides not to act. The decision is the test. He could, in this interlude, send a message that would close Vell as he closed the parish-committee operator. He does not. The drafter writes this restraint as both moral (he will not, on a girl who he is already taking everything from, take Madam Vell's life as well) and tactical (Vell's death would do more to confirm the conspiracy than Vell's disclosure will; the disclosure travels through one Petitioner and the Council, the death would travel through every broadsheet in the city). The reader cannot yet tell which weight is heavier in him. Both are true.
- **Resolved (ch ~28, the morning of Frostmonth 8):** Erys walks into Argent Square already knowing his name — Madam Vell's controlled disclosure on Day 6 or Day 7 has put it in her mouth. The Petitioner walks beside her. The Architect is in the front row. She finds him. He meets her eyes. He smiles at her with love — the same smile he gave her on Day 4 with the tea between them. She has already named him to the city moments before from the Plinth, in language she has been gathering for six days; the smile is not the reveal of his identity but his acknowledgment of her name in his mouth. *Yes, child. I am the man.* The doubleness lands without dialogue: the love was real and the betrayal was real, and the smile says both at once. He does not look away. He listens through her speech. He listens through Veren's speech. He does not stop them. He has, by the time the third bell rings, already conceded internally that the instrument has broken; he allows himself, in the negative half of his arc, to keep believing it should not have broken. He stays for the Chief Magistrate's civic-order ruling. He walks back across the Old Stone Bridge in the rain. He does not weep. He has a long evening's planning ahead of him for the next year. The book ends with him in his library, the brass glass on the tripod, the Petitioner's window lit. (Or with him simply absent from the resolution — the plotter sets the final placement.)

## Knowledge (at start of book)

- The full institutional architecture of the Ceremony, the Petitioner's Charter, the parish-clerk network, the Sailmaker's Voting House's committee structure, and the Crooked Rule's anonymity rules.
- The Lirien Maris ledger he took, copied, and returned with falsified notes fourteen years ago. The original is in the locked drawer of the Iron-Lily House library. He has not read it in seven years.
- The wrong-tincture order he placed with the Pearl Quarter doctor in the fever-cycle fourteen years ago. The doctor is dead now (genuinely, of age, not arranged).
- Veris Calden's case in full procedural detail, and the bench-customs of the magistrate who ruled against Veren. He did not order the ruling. He knows why it ruled.
- The Saern Korin / wider-net architecture (Aldovan Rell's design, his own student-of-a-student in Saern Korin, the eight-person inner circle across the Compact). He communicates with the inner circle through coded broadsheet placements and an annual letter. Two such placements appear in book 1 as iceberg; the plotter sets the chapters.
- Erys's roll-number, parish, ward address, godparent's death-date, father's death-date, apprenticeship date, and the line in her parish-charity file that reads *unable to read*. He has known the line for fourteen years. He has not seen her face.
