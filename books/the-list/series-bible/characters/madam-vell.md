# Madam Vell

role: ally-by-self-interest (shapeshifter, contrast foil to Veren)
pov: no
arc: flat
first appearance: ch ~14 (the Crooked Rule scene)

## Naming convention

- Known to the city as **Madam Vell** — mononym, parlour-keeper register. The honorific *Madam* is colloquial deference, not aristocratic; parlour-keepers in Vael Korin are addressed this way.
- Personal name (in her parish file, never said aloud on-page): **Areth Vell-Sailer**. The Sailer surname links her to the same Sailmaker's parish that Erys's father came from — quay-folk register; the character-designer plants this as a quiet shared-origin echo. Erys does not register the name in the parlour scene because Vell never gives it.
- Brass Ward folk who know her by reputation only call her *the Crooked Rule*.

## Want / Need

- **Want (external):** Keep the Crooked Rule operating through Frostmonth and into next year's Naming. She has eight house-staff, a parish-charity sister with two children she supports, and a long-cultivated set of clients whose continued business depends on her continued discretion. The book's pressure on her is that Saerin's act has drawn enough Council attention to the parlour that her operation is now exposed; her continued business *requires* the heat to move off the Crooked Rule and onto a named figure outside it.
- **Need (internal):** Acknowledge — internally, never aloud — that she sold the second-slot option to Saerin three weeks ago in the knowledge that the buyer was acting against a specific named citizen, not in the general way her clients usually buy slots. The Crooked Rule's anonymity rule is the moral architecture of her trade and she has lived inside it for thirty years; the Saerin transaction was the first time she sold knowing the buyer's target by inference, and she sold anyway because the cleared-marks were Pearl Quarter. She does not, in book 1, accept this. (Flat arc; she ends the book having shifted the heat and kept the Crooked Rule open. Book 2 may break the lie. The plotter and the book-2 designer can take it.)

## Wound / Lie / Ghost

- **Wound:** Thirty years ago, as a junior parish-clerk at the Sailmaker's Voting House, she was the clerk who wrote the bloc-vote ballots that put a small-shop tea-merchant onto a Ceremony list. The merchant drew the wheel. She was twenty-two. She quit the parish two months later and bought into the Crooked Rule with what she had saved.
- **Lie:** *If the trade is honest about being a trade, the buyer carries the moral weight, not the seller.* The Crooked Rule's anonymity rules are her version of clean hands — she does not ask who, she does not ask why, she handles only the procedural seam. She has lived this lie productively for thirty years and the Saerin transaction was the first time the lie failed and she did the work anyway.
- **Ghost:** Frostmonth 8, thirty years ago. She stood in the back of the crowd in Argent Square and watched the tea-merchant on the trestle. She did not look away. She has not, since then, attended a Ceremony. (Book 1 plants this as a single line in the parlour scene: when Erys asks her if she will be in the square on Frostmonth 8, she says *I have not been since the year I was twenty-two.*)

## Competence / Contradiction / Flaw

- **Competence:** A parlour-keeper of thirty years' standing in the city's most precise vote-brokerage. She can place a parish-clerk-cluster of any size short of the top three slots and can move cleared marks through six bonded clearing-houses without leaving a trail. She runs eight staff and a working gambling license simultaneously without the watch ever finding cause.
- **Contradiction:** A vote-broker who has not voted herself since the year she quit the parish. The Sailmaker's parish-clerk would mark her down as *failed to register* three years running, except that her own former clerk-network protects her roll-status quietly. She has the parish standing she sells; she does not exercise it.
- **Flaw:** She is loyal to her own continued operation above any client. Saerin paid her in cleared marks she could not refuse; she will, when his act endangers the parlour, give up his name under the house anonymity-void rule. She is not greedy. She is not cruel. She is a businesswoman whose business has cost her her sleep for thirty years.

## Voice
- **vocabulary:** Quay register with parlour overlay — *aye* (Sailmaker's parish flatness, like Erys) softened into Brass Ward *yes* in front of clients, but the *aye* returns when she is settled. Says *the trade*, *the seam*, *the rule*, *the rule of the house* with practiced familiarity. Does not say *bribe*, *fraud*, *manipulation*; her trade is *option*, *placement*, *clearing*, *receipt*.
- **sentence length:** Short to medium, periodic. Never rambles. Closes on the verb. ("I do not sell. I clear.")
- **avoids talking about:** The tea-merchant. The year she was twenty-two. The Sailer surname. Her sister and her sister's children.
- **speech tics:** Says *the rule of the house is* before stating any condition. ("The rule of the house is that anonymity holds unless the buyer endangers the house.") The phrase is liturgy. Erys hears it three times in the parlour scene.

## Physical

Fifty-three. Heavy-set, short. Grey hair piled and pinned with two plain combs of horn. Skin weathered the way thirty years of late nights and bad ventilation weather a parlour-keeper. A small brass pen at her collar, working. A brooch on her left lapel — wrought-iron, shaped like an open palm enclosing a stylized lily, the same sigil as the Iron-Lily House's garden gate and Veren's door-knocker. (The brooch was her mother's; her mother served the long-dead Pearl Quarter shipping family whose bonded account Saerin used; the brooch is the third sigil sighting and the careful reader can match it to the bonded-trust seal at the financial dead-end. Vell wears it without comment.) Walks heavily, slowly, on legs that ache by the eighth bell. Sits behind a desk in the back-room office, not in front of it; clients come around to her side.

## Arc beats

- **Planted (ch ~14):** Erys arrives at the Crooked Rule in the late afternoon of Day 5, having traced — through Veren's parish-bell-keeper contacts — the existence of a standing option on the second-place slot. Vell receives her in the back-room office. The brooch is described once, without comment, in the language the drafter will echo at the bonded-account dead-end.
- **Tested (same scene):** Vell offers the sale. The price is **a controlled deflection**: Erys must, in her speaking-hour at the Plinth on the morning of Day 6, publicly name Tomas Veren as the source from whom she learned the buyer's identity — severing him publicly, breaking their alliance on the record, and deflecting Council inquiry onto a named Brass Ward Petitioner rather than back toward the parlour. The cost is plausible because Petitioners are exactly the kind of citizen who would have access to a buyer's name; the cost is coherent for Vell because a Brass Ward Petitioner as the named source closes the trail before it reaches the Crooked Rule. Vell explains the price in the language of the trade: *the rule of the house is that we move the heat onto a name the heat can land on.* She does not say it cruelly. She says it as a businesswoman who has done the arithmetic.
- **Resolved (multi-scene):** Erys refuses the price in the parlour. (The plotter sets exactly how — the refusal scene is the protagonist's arc beat, not Vell's.) Vell does not press; the offer stands until the sixth bell of Day 6, when the speaking-hour passes. Vell tells her *if you change your mind, come before the bell.* Erys does not. THEN, late in the book (Day 6 evening or Day 7 morning — the plotter sets), the parlour is exposed by other vectors: Veren's bonded-account trail has surfaced enough by now that the Crooked Rule's exposure is happening regardless of Vell's silence. At that point Vell makes a controlled disclosure on her own initiative — she gives Erys and Veren **Saerin Korran's name as the buyer** of the standing option, framed under the Crooked Rule's anonymity-void rule (*the buyer endangered the parlour; the rule of the house permits me*). She does NOT give the bonded-trust dead-end family name (that stays iceberg for book 2). The disclosure travels through Veren to the Plinth on the morning of Frostmonth 8. The controlled disclosure was always the back-up plan; the sacrifice price was Vell's preferred outcome.

## Knowledge (at start of book)

- That Saerin Korran of the Iron-Lily House purchased a standing option on the second-place slot three weeks before the Reading, paid in cleared marks from a long-dead Pearl Quarter shipping family's bonded account, and exercised the option during the Silent Days.
- That the buyer's target was a specific named citizen, inferred — but not stated by the buyer — from the parish-clerk-cluster signature that placed the second-slot citizen.
- The bonded-account trail's true terminus (the dead family seal that matches her brooch). She will NOT give this in book 1; that family connection — to her mother's old patron and to her brooch's origin — stays iceberg for book 2. She gives only the bonded-account trail's *near* terminus (the clearing-house) and Saerin's name as the buyer; she lets Veren and Erys hit the dead-end at the family seal themselves.
- The full client list of the Crooked Rule over thirty years. None of this surfaces in book 1.
- She does NOT know that Saerin arranged Lirien Maris's death. She does not know Erys's mother's name.
