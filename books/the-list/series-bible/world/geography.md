# Geography

## The Country

### The Korresand Compact

type: confederated republic
language: Korresandi (the common tongue) plus regional dialects
calendar: civic, twelve months of thirty days plus a five-day intercalary festival at year's end; the year hinges on the autumn harvest tally
currency: silver marks (1 mark = 100 pennies); high-denomination drafts handled through bonded clearing-houses

A flat coastal republic of nine charter cities and the agricultural cantons that feed them. No monarchy in seventy years; the last hereditary Stadholder abdicated under pressure during the so-called Quiet Reform. The Compact is governed by a rotating Council of Charters that meets twice a year in Vael Korin. In practice each city runs itself, sends taxes inward, and resents the others.

Vael Korin is the largest city by a factor of three and the only one with a deepwater harbor; it dominates the Compact economically and culturally without admitting that it does. The other charter cities — Brael, Anveris, Saern Korin, Old Korin, Tirreth, Hofdal, Nerren, and Mossbridge — each hold their own annual Ceremonies. The Compact's founding charter requires it. (The reader will not need this fact in book 1. It is here so the bible holds.)

The country is bounded to the west by the Auberen Sea, to the north by the Brittle Mountains (a low range, more border than barrier), to the east by the marsh-country of the Long Fens, and to the south by the river border with the Sereth Kingdoms, with whom the Compact has been at uneasy peace for forty years.

## The City: Vael Korin

type: charter city, seat of the Council
population: ~400,000
character: a working harbor under a marble crown — broad-shouldered, smoke-streaked, secretly proud of its civic rituals; a city that believes itself to be the moral center of the Compact and will not be told otherwise

Vael Korin sits where the River Korras meets the sea, on a flat alluvial plain rising slowly to a single low hill in the northeast. The hill (Cloister Hill) holds the old money. The plain holds everyone else. The harbor and the river divide the city into four districts joined by five bridges: three iron, two stone, one of them — the Old Stone Bridge — load-bearing in the book's geography.

The city is gaslit on the main thoroughfares (iron poles, hand-lit at dusk by lamplighters working in pairs), unlit on the side streets in the lower districts. An experimental electric tramway runs the central artery from the harbor up to the Council Hall; outside that line, travel is by hansom cab, by foot, by the river ferries, or — for the rich — by private brougham. Telegraph is universal in commercial use; the telephone is experimental and exists only in three or four private homes and the central police bureau.

Broadsheets are the dominant medium. Six daily papers compete in Vael Korin. The most respected is *The Argent Watch* (centrist-liberal, prints the official Ceremony tallies). The most read is *The Common Hour* (penny-broadsheet, sensational, prints the methods drawn each year on the front page in red ink). Both will appear in the book.

### Districts

#### The Harbor & Low Quays
type: working district
class: low-tier (Erys's tier)
character: tar, fish-smoke, salt, the constant noise of stevedores; tenements packed five families to a building; bakeries and chandlers and rope-walks at street level, sleeping rooms above

Where Erys lives and where she works. The bakery she apprentices to (Master Holbren's, on Cooper's Lane) is six blocks from the quayside. Her room is above the oven — hot in summer, hot in winter. The Low Quays vote in the Sailmaker's Parish Voting House, the largest in the city. It tallies more ballots than any other parish and so its votes weigh disproportionately in the public imagination, though the count itself is one-citizen-one-ballot.

The Low Quays at night are unlit on the alley grid. Watchmen pass each corner once an hour, twice on holy nights, and almost never on the night of Day 4 after a Reading (the night between Day 4 and Day 5) — the "watchmen's blind night" — by long unofficial custom, because the watch will not be seen interfering with private vengeance against the named. The blind night is publicly denied and privately justified as letting the city's rage spend itself without admitting lawful sanction. (This will matter for the night attempt on Erys.)

#### The Brass Ward
type: middling commercial district
class: mid-tier (the Petitioner's tier, professionally; he keeps his office here)
character: solicitors' chambers, printing houses, the Petitioner's Hall, the Hall of Tallies, three coffee-houses where the city's lawyers and journalists gossip; gaslit through the night

The civic district. The Hall of Tallies (where votes are counted) and the Petitioner's Hall (where Petitions are filed and heard) face each other across Counter Square, a deliberate architectural reminder: the city counts, the city objects. The Petitioner's office is a narrow three-story building two streets east of Counter Square, behind a green door with a brass knocker shaped like an open palm.

#### Argent Square & the Council Quarter
type: civic monumental
class: cross-class (everyone comes here for the Reading and the Ceremony)
character: marble, granite, the city's set-pieces happen here

The vast public square at the city's heart. The Reading of the List happens here at dawn on Frostmonth 1; the Ceremony happens here at dawn on Frostmonth 8, seven days later. The square holds approximately twenty thousand standing; for the Reading and the Ceremony it overflows into the four streets that feed it. The Council Hall stands on the north side, the Tellers' Tower on the east, and on the west the small stone Petitioners' Plinth from which a Petitioner makes his public address. On the south, behind a low railing, is the bare flagstone plot where the Ceremony itself is conducted: no permanent gallows, no permanent block — the apparatus for each method is brought out, used, and taken away.

#### Cloister Hill & the Pearl Quarter
type: old-money residential
class: high-tier (the Architect's tier)
character: walled gardens, plane trees, private libraries, the houses of senior advocates and retired judges and inherited shipping money; lit by the city's first electric streetlamps, installed twelve years ago as a private subscription

The hill sits to the northeast of the city, separated from the rest of Vael Korin by the Korras River and the Old Stone Bridge. Crossing the bridge is psychologically as much as physically a passage; the air gets cleaner, the streets quieter. The Architect's house — the **Iron-Lily House**, named for the wrought-iron lilies set into its garden gate — stands on Wellspring Road, near the crown of the hill. The Pearl Quarter is laid out on a long shallow ridge with sight-lines down toward the Brass Ward and Argent Square; from the Architect's garden, with a good glass, he can see the lit windows of the Petitioner's office across the river. This is not a coincidence of geography. He chose the house for the view.

## Places That Appear in Book 1

### Argent Square
The civic plaza. Site of the Reading (Day 0) and the Ceremony (Day 7). Cobbled, six gas-lamp pillars at the corners, a low marble curb around the central flagstone plot. The Tellers' Tower clock chimes the hour across it.

### The Hall of Tallies
type: civic building
where: Counter Square, Brass Ward
A three-story stone building with a long counting hall on the ground floor. The Tellers — twenty-four sworn officers in grey robes — count ballots here for the ten days following the Naming, in pairs, watched by witnesses from each parish. Hourly aggregate totals are posted by parish and ballot volume only; candidate names remain sealed until the Reading. The final list is sealed at midnight on Autumnmoon 21 and not unsealed until dawn on Frostmonth 1 in Argent Square. The Master Teller is by long tradition deaf-mute and chosen for life; the sealing of the list is his sole official act.

### The Petitioner's Hall
type: civic building
where: Counter Square, Brass Ward, facing the Hall of Tallies
A smaller two-story building of yellow stone with a high-ceilinged hearing room on the ground floor. The Petitioner's Bench (where the Examining Magistrate sits to hear filings) is raised one step. There are sixty seats for the public. Petitioner filings are heard in public; the magistrate's ruling is final and not appealable. Erys's filing is rejected here on Day 3.

### Master Holbren's bakery (Cooper's Lane, Low Quays)
type: residence + workplace
Erys's apprenticeship. Two ovens, a flour-store, a front counter, a back stair to her room.

### The Petitioner's Office (Green Door, Brass Ward)
type: private chambers
Three stories. Ground floor a waiting room with no chairs. First floor the workroom: a long oak table, paper stacked in deliberate towers, two windows facing the river. Second floor private. The Architect visits here in the book.

### The Iron-Lily House (Wellspring Road, Pearl Quarter)
type: private residence
The Architect's home. Three stories of grey stone, a walled side-garden with a small stone bench by the back wall, a library window overlooking the river toward the Petitioner's office. One of the city's private telephone lines terminates in the library, officially for Council emergencies.

### The Crooked Rule (vote-buyer's parlour)
type: back-room establishment
where: an unmarked door on Penny-Match Lane, between the Low Quays and the Brass Ward
A gambling parlour on its license, a vote-broker's office in fact. Run by a woman called Madam Vell. The book's vote-buyer's parlour scene takes place here. The Crooked Rule deals in two markets: ballots-for-sale (citizens who will vote as instructed for a fee, picked up at the Sailmaker's Voting House during the Ten Days of Naming) and Petitioner-slot manipulations (the rarer, more expensive instrument; the Architect bought a standing option from Madam Vell here, three weeks before the Reading, and exercised it after parish totals leaked during the Silent Days).

### The Old Stone Bridge
The crossing between the Low Quays and the path up to Cloister Hill. Erys crosses it twice in the book, the second time at dawn on the morning of the Ceremony.

### Lamp Alley
type: street
A narrow alley running parallel to the Low Quays' main thoroughfare, named for the single gas-lamp at its mouth and the unbroken dark for the next two hundred yards. Site of the night attempt on Erys's life on the night of Day 4 (the night between Day 4 and Day 5) — the watchmen's blind night. The watch does not patrol Lamp Alley on that night, by long unofficial custom.

## Travel times (for staging)

The drafter and plotter will need these for the Day 3-4 chain of scenes (office, Crooked Rule, home, Argent Square) and for the climax.

- Low Quays to Brass Ward: 25 minutes on foot.
- Brass Ward to Argent Square: 10 minutes on foot.
- Brass Ward to Crooked Rule: 12 minutes on foot.
- Low Quays to Cloister Hill: 45 minutes on foot, via the Old Stone Bridge.
- Electric tramway end-to-end (harbor to Council Hall): about 18 minutes when running; the line stops at the eleventh bell each night and runs the next morning from the fifth.

## The Ring of Context (everything beyond)

The Compact's other eight charter cities each hold their own Ceremony in the same week, on staggered days, so that the Council's couriers can carry the lists between them. (This is iceberg material — not surfaced in book 1 except as a single passing reference. It is the book-2 hook.) The Sereth Kingdoms to the south do not hold Ceremonies and consider the practice barbaric; the Compact considers the Sereth practice of private trial-by-combat barbaric in return.
