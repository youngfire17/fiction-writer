# History

The book needs three layers of past: living memory (the Architect's lifetime and the founding of the modern Petitioner system), recent history (the founding of the Ceremony itself), and old history (the Year of Knives that preceded the Ceremony's founding). Below that, only the deep iceberg matters — see `iceberg.md`.

The single most important historical fact for the drafter: **the Ceremony is roughly 100 years old; the modern Petitioner system is roughly 50 years old, founded by the Architect.** Everything else in this file supports or complicates that core fact.

## Layer 1 — Old History (the founding, ~100 years ago)

### The Year of Knives (the year before the Ceremony, ~101 years ago)

For two generations before the founding, Vael Korin and the Compact had been in slow civil dissolution. The old Stadholder's authority had failed. The judiciary was corrupt. Private feuds — between shipping families, between parishes, between rival guilds on the quays — had become normal, and the watch could not or would not interfere. In the year now called the Year of Knives, the city's coroners' rolls recorded over four thousand killings in twelve months, in a city then of roughly two hundred thousand. One in fifty Korresandi died at the end of a neighbor's blade. The grain trade stopped. The harbor stopped. The Compact nearly fell apart.

The Year of Knives is not a single event but a year that *passed* — and the cultural memory of it is the deepest constitutive fact of Korresandi civic life. To this day, the worst Korresandi insult is to call someone *a knife*. The Ceremony was founded explicitly to answer the Year of Knives: to relocate the impulse toward vengeance out of private hands and into a public, witnessed, anonymous, bureaucratic procedure. *We will not kill each other. We will, instead, decide together.*

### The Founding of the Ceremony (Frostmonth 8, ~100 years ago)

In the autumn following the Year of Knives, an emergency session of what is now the Council of Charters — then an improvised assembly of solicitors, parish bell-keepers, harbor-masters, and one surviving Stadholder's-deputy — drafted what was then called the **Compact of Stones**, named for the seven small stones (later replaced by the iron lots) that were inscribed with the seven oldest methods of public execution.

The Compact of Stones laid down:
1. That each charter city would, once a year, hold a Ceremony in which seven of its citizens would die.
2. That the seven would be named by anonymous ballot of all citizens, counted publicly, ranked by vote, and read aloud at dawn in the public square.
3. That the method of each execution would be drawn by lot from the seven oldest forms, so that no one could plan the death in advance and no one could choose it.
4. That seven days would pass between the naming and the executions, so the city could grieve, object, or, in the original phrase, *speak the unspoken*.
5. That no private vengeance could be lawfully sought against any citizen for any cause; the Ceremony was the city's only sanctioned vengeance, and the city's only one.

The Compact of Stones was passed in a single session, in an exhausted city. It was supposed to be a temporary measure. It has been renewed annually for one hundred years.

The first Ceremony, Frostmonth 8 of the founding year, executed seven citizens whose names are still inscribed on a small bronze plaque in the Hall of Tallies. The first man drew the rope, the second the blade, the third the wheel, the fourth the cup, the fifth the stone, the sixth the cold, the seventh the fire. (The ordering by chance was thought, that first year, to be itself a sign.) The number of killings in Vael Korin the following year fell from four thousand to under three hundred.

The Korresandi remember this number. It is the moral foundation of the entire institution. The Ceremony *worked*. That is the fact every defender returns to.

## Layer 2 — Recent History (50-100 years ago)

### The Long Quiet (~100-60 years ago)

For the first forty years of the Ceremony's life, the institution ran roughly as designed. Annual ballots, annual Readings, annual Ceremonies. Private vengeance never returned to its Year-of-Knives levels. The Compact stabilized. The judiciary reformed. The Stadholder's office withered and was finally vacated in the so-called Quiet Reform seventy years ago. The Council of Charters became the standing government.

In this period the "Petitioner" role existed but was vestigial — a procedural relic of the Compact of Stones, which had included a one-line provision that "any citizen may speak for any named, with proof of error, at the magistrate's hearing." For forty years almost no one did. The few who tried filed in the wrong building, were not heard, and went home humiliated. Three names in forty years were commuted by Petitioner action; all three were the result of clerical error (a wrong parish entry, a same-name confusion).

### The Reform Years (~60-50 years ago)

A generation after the founding, the Ceremony's death-rate had begun to drift upward. The original founders had assumed the list would name the city's genuine worst — repeat offenders, known killers, the violent and the cruel — and the early lists had largely done so. But by the third generation, the seven names each year were drifting toward the *resented* rather than the *dangerous*. Unpopular landlords. A grain-broker who had cornered the rye-market the previous winter. A magistrate who had ruled against a parish in a property dispute. The lists had become, quietly, a way for the city's poor to punish a particular kind of injustice; and a way for any sufficiently organized faction to remove its enemies.

This is the period in which the modern Petitioner system was founded.

### The Founding of the Petitioner System (~50 years ago)

The Architect — then in his early twenties, a public defender working out of the Brass Ward, the son of a quay-folk family that had risen through scholarship to the middling tier — took up the case of a Reapmonth grain-broker named (the records have his given name; not surfaced on-page) who had been placed third on the previous Ceremony's list almost certainly because he had foreclosed on a Sailmaker's parish bakery the spring before. The broker was a hard man and not a sympathetic one. The Architect filed an objection at the Petitioner's Bench, which then sat in a back room of the Hall of Tallies. He spoke for the broker for an hour. He proved that the broker's foreclosure had been legally clean and that the *parish committee* had organized the vote against him through Sailmaker's Voting House — that, in other words, the list was no longer naming the dangerous; it was naming the inconvenient.

The Examining Magistrate ruled against him. The broker was executed at the Ceremony. The Architect attended.

In the year following, the Architect drafted what is now called the **Petitioner's Charter** — a thirty-page document of procedural reform that gave the Petitioner's office its own building, its own bench, its own Examining Magistrate, its own publicly-printed filings, and its own irrevocable, non-appealable, but *public* hearings. He took the draft to the Council of Charters; he was a public defender of twenty-three years old; the Council laughed and then, six months later, after a parish riot over a particularly cynical list, signed it.

The Petitioner's Hall was built two years after that, across Counter Square from the Hall of Tallies. The first Examining Magistrate took his bench fifty years ago this Frostmonth. The first three Petitioners filed in that first year; one succeeded.

The Architect was named the first Honorary Petitioner — a lifetime title that does not exist in the Charter, but that the Council awarded him by acclamation at the building's dedication. He has worn it for fifty years.

This is the public history. The Korresandi tell it to their children. The Petitioner-we-know tells it to himself.

### The Save-Rate (~50 years to now)

Since the Charter, between zero and three of the seven named have been commuted by Petitioner action each year. The average is just under one. The Architect personally filed eleven successful Petitions in his thirty active years (he retired from active filing thirty years ago). His record is unmatched. The Petitioner-we-know is the only Petitioner since the Architect to file in the high single digits — he has saved eight names in twelve years of active practice, including four in his first three years, the streak that made his reputation.

### What's been deliberately erased

In the public record, the Petitioner system was founded as a response to drift — to the lists naming the inconvenient rather than the dangerous. The drafter must hold a second fact alongside it: the Architect also founded the Petitioner system to give himself permanent, lifelong, unchallengeable access to the Ceremony's apparatus from inside. The official histories do not record this. When asked, the Architect has always said the same thing: "I wanted to make sure the right names were on the list." The public hears this as *I wanted to keep wrong names off it*. He means the literal sentence.

This is iceberg material. It informs every line he speaks but is never confirmed on-page in book 1.

## Layer 3 — Living Memory (last 50 years, the Architect's career)

The drafter only needs a handful of dates from this layer. Listing them so they don't drift.

- **~50 years ago:** Architect founds the Petitioner system. Becomes Honorary Petitioner.
- **~45 years ago:** Architect marries into the Pearl Quarter (the Iron-Lily House family, an old shipping name now functionally extinct in the male line). Takes the house-name.
- **~35 years ago:** Architect's wife dies in childbirth; the child does not survive. He does not remarry. He lives alone in the Iron-Lily House for the next thirty-five years with one house-keeper.
- **~30 years ago:** Architect files his last personal Petition. Succeeds. Retires from active filing at the height of his reputation.
- **~25 years ago:** The Architect's first known act of off-book intervention. (Iceberg — see `iceberg.md` for the full chain. The public record shows nothing.)
- **~14 years ago:** The Architect arranges, through intermediaries, the death of a woman named **Lirien Maris** — a parish-charity records-keeper at the Sailmaker's parish who had begun to notice patterns in the Ceremony's tallies that should not have been there. Lirien Maris was Erys's mother. Erys was three. The death was recorded as fever. (Iceberg in book 1; the reader feels its shape but does not learn the name on-page until late.)
- **~9 years ago:** Erys's godmother dies in the early summer of a long fever-cycle that runs through the city for two years.
- **~7 years ago:** Erys's father, a quay-stevedore named Holm Sailer, dies in the later peak of the same fever-cycle. The fever is real and killed many; the Architect did not arrange this. Erys, ten, enters the parish charity-house.
- **~15 years ago:** The Petitioner-we-know — then twenty-two, just out of the public defenders — meets the Architect at a Brass Ward coffee-house. The Architect takes him on as a private student. Trains him for three years.
- **~12 years ago:** The Petitioner-we-know files his first Petition. Succeeds. Files three more in the next two years; succeeds in all three. The Architect attends every hearing in the front row.
- **~5 years ago:** The Petitioner-we-know files what becomes his most famous case — a Petition for a Sailmaker's parish printer accused of inciting riot. He saves the printer. The case makes him a public figure. The Architect, after the ruling, walks him home through the Brass Ward in the rain. They do not speak. The Petitioner has not forgotten this.
- **~3 years ago:** The Petitioner-we-know files the case he loses. (Iceberg — book 2.)
- **~6 weeks ago:** The Architect, at his usual coffee-house, mentions to the Petitioner that he has been "thinking about the Sailmaker's rolls again." It is the kind of remark the Architect has made for decades. The Petitioner does not register it.
- **~3 weeks ago:** Two events on the same day. (a) The Architect, through two layers of intermediation, delivers a fabricated case file to the Petitioner-we-know via routes the Petitioner trusts; the Petitioner files an Intent to Represent for the file's subject — a Brass Ward citizen — at the Petitioner's Hall, by his own hand, in the ordinary course of his practice. (b) The Architect purchases a standing option on the second-place slot from Madam Vell at the Crooked Rule, paid in cleared marks from a bonded account in the name of a long-dead Pearl Quarter shipping family. The option is exercised after the Sealing, during the Silent Days, once parish totals leaked from the Hall of Tallies confirm the clerk-cluster manipulation has placed his bait-citizen in the second slot.
- **~10 days ago (Autumnmoon 21):** The Master Teller seals the list. Erys's name is at the top by a margin no one in the Hall of Tallies can immediately explain.
- **~9 days ago:** The Petitioner-we-know — who never reads the public list before the Reading, by long personal discipline — sees a rumor-column item in *The Common Hour* about unusual Sailmaker's totals around Erys Maris's roll-number, and registers the name. He does not know her. He does not yet select her; the column is rumor, not identification, and the list is sealed. (The Architect has known the Petitioner's reading habits for thirty years; the column placement is one of several seeds he has put in the Petitioner's path. He is not relying on any single seed.)
- **Day 0 (Frostmonth 1):** The Reading. The book begins.

## What everyone knows that's wrong

- The Ceremony is fair because the ballots are anonymous. *(Anonymity holds at the parish level; the Sailmaker's parish-clerk-written ballots — about a third of the parish's total — can be coordinated through the parish committee for the right price, and have been.)*
- The Petitioner system is incorruptible because filings are public and rulings are final. *(Procedure is incorruptible; the slot is not. A Petitioner can be baited into filing for the wrong client three weeks before the Reading, and the slot rule then binds him against the right client. The Crooked Rule has brokered this kind of bait for decades.)*
- The Architect is the system's moral conscience. *(He is the system's moral conscience. He is also its longest-running corruption. Treat this as a single load-bearing fact about him, not as a contradiction to be resolved.)*
- The Ceremony has been right enough, often enough, for a hundred years that it can be trusted. *(The Ceremony has been right enough, often enough, that the city has agreed to stop asking. That is not the same thing.)*

These are the four loud lies the book is going to unmake.
