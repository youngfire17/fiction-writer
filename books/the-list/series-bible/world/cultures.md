# Cultures

The book is set in one city, in one country, in one civic tradition. There is one culture to design — Korresandi — and within it the class differences that shape every page.

## Korresandi Civic Culture

### What Korresandi value
- **Civic participation.** Voting is not a right but a duty. A citizen who fails to cast a ballot in three consecutive years can be stripped of charter standing and lose access to the parish hospitals and the bonded clearing-houses. The poor especially vote — because voting is the cheapest proof of citizenship, and citizenship is the difference between a person and a foreigner-without-papers on the docks.
- **Public reason.** Arguments must be made in public, in writing or in the open hearing room, with witnesses. A private agreement carries no legal weight. The phrase a Korresandi solicitor uses to dismiss a private claim is *"unspoken in the square."*
- **The list.** Not the Ceremony's list specifically — the *idea* of the list. The Korresandi maintain public lists of everything: the harbor manifest, the tax rolls, the Tellers' rolls, the Petitioner's previous filings, the births and deaths of every parish. To be on a list is to exist. To be off all of them is to be no one.
- **Stoicism on the personal scale.** Public grief is acceptable; private complaint is not. A widow weeps at the funeral; she does not weep at the market. This will shape Erys's interiority: she has been raised not to make her fear visible.

### What Korresandi fear
- **Private vengeance.** The cultural memory of the Year of Knives (see history) is still active. A neighbor who takes private revenge — for an insult, a wronging, an old grudge — is regarded with more horror than the crime that prompted the revenge. The Ceremony exists to absorb that impulse into the civic body. Korresandi look at the Sereth practice of trial-by-combat and shudder.
- **Being unlisted.** To die without a name on a roll is the secular equivalent of being damned. Charity in Vael Korin overwhelmingly funds the parish lists of the indigent dead — so that no one ends up *unspoken in the square*.
- **The wrong name on the list.** This is the deepest fear and the one the Ceremony's whole apparatus is built to defuse. The Korresandi will tell you, with confidence, that the system is too well-built for that to happen. The Petitioner system, they will say, exists precisely so that it cannot. This is what the book breaks.

### How power is held
- **By election, by tally, by appointment.** The Council of Charters is elected by parish vote every three years. The Examining Magistrates are appointed by the Council for nine-year terms, renewable once by Council vote at the end of the first term. The Tellers are elected for life from candidates the parishes propose; the Master Teller serves until death. The Petitioners are not officials at all — they are private citizens who file in the public interest; the office of Petitioner has no salary, no oath, and no formal selection. Anyone may file. Most do not, because the work is unpaid and the loss is public.
- **Quiet power lives in the Pearl Quarter.** Old shipping money, the senior solicitors, the retired magistrates. They do not hold office. They hold dinners. The book's Architect is the keystone of this network.

### How the economy actually works
- The harbor. Eight of ten Korresandi marks pass through Vael Korin's harbor in some form. Grain from the cantons goes out; spices, copper, citrus, and Sereth wine come in. The shipping clearing-houses on the Brass Ward broker the trade; the banking-houses (private, family-held, mostly Pearl Quarter) carry the debt.
- Day-labor on the quays. Bakeries, cooperages, sail-lofts, rope-walks, fish-smokeries, chandleries — all the small trades that feed the harbor's people and outfit its ships. Erys's tier.
- A small but visible professional class in the Brass Ward: solicitors, journalists, clerks, doctors, schoolmasters, the lower magistrature, the Petitioners. The Petitioner-we-know is from this tier originally; his rise to the highest reputation in the Petitioner's Hall has not changed his rent or his neighborhood.

## Class Structure

The book lives at three tiers. Naming them here so the drafter has the words ready.

### Low Tier (the Quay-folk)
- About 60% of the city. Stevedores, bakers, sailmakers, rope-walkers, fishwives, laundresses, lamplighters, the watch's foot-patrols, journeymen of the small trades.
- Live in tenements above their workplaces or in shared rooming-houses. One bath a week at the parish bath-house, by ticket. Bread, fish, root-vegetables, beer cut with water; meat on the feast-day at year's end.
- Vote at the Sailmaker's Voting House (the Low Quays' parish) and at three smaller parish houses. Vote heavily — the highest turnout of any tier. This is the tier that *believes in the Ceremony most*: it is the one civic instrument that does not require literacy, money, or a name in the Brass Ward to operate. A laundress's ballot weighs the same as a banker's.
- This is Erys's tier. She is seventeen, a baker's apprentice, with no living parents and no siblings on the books. She has a parish-list entry and a Sailmaker's voter-roll number. She has cast a ballot in two of the last three Ceremonies — she could not bring herself to name anyone, so both times she folded a blank.

### Middling Tier (the Brass-Ward folk)
- About 30% of the city. Solicitors, clerks, junior magistrates, printers, journalists, doctors, the better shopkeepers, schoolmasters, the Petitioners.
- Live in narrow row-houses, two or three rooms. Educated to age sixteen at minimum, often older. Read at least one daily broadsheet; many read three. Take coffee at the Brass Ward coffee-houses; argue. Their morning is paper-and-talk.
- The Petitioner-we-know belongs to this tier. He rents two of the three floors of the Green Door house; the ground floor and his workroom are his office, the second floor his living quarters.
- This tier is the Ceremony's *managers*. They count the ballots, they hear the Petitions, they print the lists. Most of them have a private, faintly embarrassed pride in the institution and a public, fluent set of arguments for its necessity.

### High Tier (the Pearl Quarter folk)
- About 10% of the city. Inherited shipping money, senior solicitors, retired magistrates, the senior partners of the banking-houses, three or four old-name shipping families that span centuries.
- Live behind walled gardens on Cloister Hill. Servants, libraries, private gas (now electric) lighting, private carriages. Dine in. Speak in lowered voices.
- This tier votes lightly, almost theatrically — a handful of ballots cast at the small Cloister Parish Voting House each year, almost always for a name already on every other ballot, so the act registers as agreement without exposure.
- The Architect belongs here, but as an irregular: he was born middling, made his name as a Petitioner, married into the Pearl Quarter at thirty, was widowed at forty, and has lived alone in the Iron-Lily House since. He is one of two or three people in Vael Korin who is regarded with deep respect by all three tiers simultaneously. The quay-folk consider him "one of theirs who made it"; the middling tier considers him their elder statesman; the Pearl Quarter considers him a peer.

## The Relationship Between Citizens and the Ceremony

For most Korresandi, the Ceremony is the year's gravitational center. The civic calendar runs:

- **Late summer:** the parish charities collect the names of the indigent dead, so the rolls are complete before voting opens.
- **Autumnmoon 1-10:** the Ten Days of Naming. Voting Houses open at dawn, close at the eighth bell. A citizen may cast one ballot per day; only the last is counted (to allow change of mind). The ballot is a small square of grey paper, folded twice; the citizen writes a name (or marks an X and tells the parish clerk a name, which the clerk writes; about a third of low-tier ballots are clerk-written). Each folded ballot is sealed inside a numbered outer sleeve issued against the voter-roll on the day of casting. When the parish's Tally-Drum is opened at the Hall of Tallies, clerks void any voter's earlier sleeves against the roll before the unopened inner ballots are separated from their sleeves and counted. Anonymity is preserved because the inner ballots are mixed before opening; the change-of-mind rule is preserved because the sleeves carry the date.
- **Autumnmoon 11-20:** the Days of Counting. Tellers count in pairs at the Hall of Tallies, watched by parish witnesses. Hourly totals posted on the slate wall.
- **Autumnmoon 21 midnight:** the Master Teller seals the final list.
- **Frostmonth 1 dawn:** the Reading of the List. The Chief Magistrate reads the seven names aloud in Argent Square, in vote-rank order. The Days of Petition begin.
- **Frostmonth 1-7:** the Days of Petition. Each named citizen may have one Petitioner file on their behalf. Filings are made at the Petitioner's Hall and heard publicly. The Examining Magistrate rules; the ruling is final.
- **Frostmonth 8 dawn:** the Ceremony. The seven named, in vote-rank order, draw their methods from the Bowl of Methods. Executions proceed across the day. The Plaza is open to all; attendance is heavy but not mandatory.

Erys's seventeen years have included seventeen Readings and seventeen Ceremonies. She has attended the Reading every year since she was twelve (parish custom — apprentices are expected). She has attended the Ceremony itself twice, both times because Master Holbren brought her, both times she did not watch the actual deaths and Holbren did not require it of her. This is the cultural texture: the Ceremony is normal. Citizens disagree about whether it is *good*, but almost no one alive can imagine a city without it.

### What the poor especially believe
That the Ceremony is the one civic instrument in which their voice weighs equal to the bankers'. A penny-broker on the quay cannot afford a solicitor, cannot afford a bribed magistrate, cannot afford anything in the Brass Ward — but he can fold a grey paper and put it in the iron drum, and that paper is counted the same as any. This belief is so deep that the most vocal defenders of the Ceremony in the broadsheets are not the high tier (who treat it with discretion) but the low tier's spokesmen. The Architect knows this. He has spent fifty years cultivating it.

## Daily Life: A Morning at the Bottom and a Morning at the Top

### Erys's morning (Low Quays, before the book begins)
Fourth bell. Up in the dark. Wash from the basin (cold). Down the back stair into the bakery. The first oven is already lit; Holbren has been up an hour. Mix the day's dough — flour from the bin, salt from the crock, the leaven from yesterday's scrap. Shape the loaves. Slide them in. Sweep the floor while they bake. The first customers come at the sixth bell, smelling of fish and tar; she sells bread for pennies, makes change in pennies, ties her apron tighter. Lunch is the heel of a yesterday-loaf and a cup of small beer. The afternoon is the second bake. She closes the front at the ninth bell, sweeps again, climbs the stair to her room, eats whatever Holbren has left on the back-room table, and is asleep by the tenth.

She has never met anyone from the Pearl Quarter. She has never crossed the Old Stone Bridge.

### The Architect's morning (Iron-Lily House)
Seventh bell. The house-keeper brings tea to the library. He reads three broadsheets, makes annotations in a small black-bound notebook, writes two or three letters in his careful hand. By the ninth bell he is in the garden, walking the path along the back wall, where the stone bench faces north — and where a man with a brass glass on a tripod can see, between two plane-trees on the slope below, the lit windows of the Petitioner's office in the Brass Ward. He does this every morning. It is the routine of a man who loves his former student and worries about him.

The reader will read this scene before the reveal and feel its warmth. After the reveal, the same scene is the watcher in the garden, checking on his target's keeper.

## Names, Calendar, Food, Kinship

- **Names:** Korresandi names are typically two-syllable, soft consonants, with a regional suffix: *-en* (Holbren, Veren) for the harbor parishes, *-is* (Erys, Maris) for the older quay families, *-an* (Aldovan, Korran) for the Brass Ward and Pearl Quarter. Surnames are usually trade-based at the low tier (Cooper, Sailer, Holm), patronymic at the middling tier (Aldovan = "of Aldo"), and house-named at the high tier (the family is "of the Iron-Lily House," not a surname in the modern sense). The Architect's house-name is set in the geography file; his personal name will be set by the character-designer.
- **Calendar:** twelve months of thirty days plus five intercalary days at year's end. Months: Firstlight, Greenmonth, Highsun, Reapmonth, Autumnmoon, Frostmonth, Hollowmonth, Snowmonth, Latefrost, Thawmonth, Floodmonth, Brightmonth. The book is set in Frostmonth 1-8. Days of the week: Marketday, Quayday, Postday, Hearthday, Bellday, Restday, Tellingday (the seventh, when the parish bell tolls seven and tallies are read in the parish halls — the weekly civic ritual that prefigures the annual one).
- **Food:** the staple is dark rye bread and salted fish. The feast is roast goose at year's end. The taboo is red meat at funerals (a hold-over from the Year of Knives — meat for the dead's family was once a sign of complicity in their death). The despised food, by quay-folk, is anything sweetened with refined sugar (a Pearl Quarter affectation); by the Pearl Quarter, anything sweetened with molasses (a quay affectation). Erys eats rye, fish, root vegetables, and beer-cut-with-water. The Architect drinks tea with a single sugar lump.
- **Kinship:** nuclear families dominant at all tiers, with strong parish-godparent custom (every Korresandi child is godparented at the parish bell on the seventh day; the godparent is named on the parish list and is expected to step in if the parents die before the child is sixteen). Her mother died when Erys was three; her godparent died when Erys was eight; her father died two years later, in the later peak of the same long fever-cycle. She was raised by the parish charity-house from ten until twelve, then apprenticed to Holbren. She is "unparished" in the technical sense — no living blood kin, no living godparent — which is one of the bureaucratic facts that made her easy to put at the top of the list.

## Religion

The Korresandi are nominally observant of the Three-and-One — a faith in three impersonal civic virtues (Order, Witness, Mercy) united in one impersonal Source. The faith has no clergy in the priestly sense, only parish bell-keepers who toll the hours and the seven-bell on Tellingday. Doctrine is thin. Ritual is thick. The Ceremony itself is not a religious rite — it is explicitly civic, not sacred — but the parish bell tolls during it, and most citizens cross themselves with two fingers at each method drawn from the Bowl.

The Architect was raised devout. The Petitioner-we-know is a lapsed observer who still crosses himself at the Bowl without thinking. Erys was raised by the parish charity-house and so is observant by reflex rather than conviction.

## Avoiding Monoculture

Other charter cities hold variant Ceremonies. (Detail kept in `iceberg.md` as book-2 scaffolding; book 1 does not surface the variants on-page beyond one passing broadsheet line.)

Within Vael Korin, the parishes have visible cultural difference — the Sailmaker's parish speaks with a flatter accent and uses *aye* where the Brass Ward uses *yes*; the Cloister parish's voter-roll is read in the older, more formal Korresandi, with the *-an* suffix pronounced long. These are seasoning, not substance. The drafter should let them appear once or twice in dialogue.
