# The Ceremony and the Petitioner System

This is the book's load-bearing world fact. Every other file in this folder supports this one. The drafter must reach for this file before drafting any procedural scene.

## The Ceremony

### One-line definition
Once a year, the citizens of Vael Korin vote by anonymous parish ballot on the seven citizens who most deserve to die; seven days after the names are read aloud at dawn in Argent Square, those seven citizens die in the same square by methods drawn by lot.

### The annual cycle (Korresandi civic calendar)

- **Autumnmoon 1-10 — the Ten Days of Naming.** Voting Houses open dawn to eighth bell. One ballot per citizen per day; only the last is counted. A ballot is a small square of grey paper, folded twice, with a name written on it. A citizen who cannot write may dictate to the parish clerk, who writes the ballot in front of the citizen and folds it. Roughly a third of low-tier ballots are clerk-written.
- **Autumnmoon 11-20 — the Days of Counting.** Tellers count in pairs at the Hall of Tallies, watched by witnesses from each parish (one witness per parish, present at the count, sworn). Hourly running totals posted on the slate wall in the public counting hall. Anyone may enter and watch.
- **Autumnmoon 21, midnight — the Sealing.** The Master Teller (deaf-mute, lifelong, the office's only ceremonial holdover from the founding) seals the final list with a wax-and-rope seal in front of three witnesses. The sealed list is locked in the bronze list-box at the foot of the Tellers' Tower in Argent Square.
- **Autumnmoon 22 to Frostmonth 1 — the Silent Days.** No civic announcements. The list is sealed. Parish bells toll the quarter-hour but not the seven on Tellingday. The city quiets.
- **Frostmonth 1, dawn — the Reading of the List.** The Chief Magistrate, in front of a public crowd in Argent Square, breaks the seal and reads the seven names aloud in vote-rank order, slowly, with the vote-count after each name. The reading takes roughly five minutes. The crowd is silent during the reading by long custom; sound is permitted only after the seventh name is read.
- **Frostmonth 1-7 — the Days of Petition.** Petitioner filings made at the Petitioner's Hall, public hearings each day at the second, fourth, and sixth bells. (See "The Petitioner System" below.)
- **Frostmonth 8, dawn — the Ceremony begins.** The seven named are brought from the Holding House to the south side of Argent Square in vote-rank order. From dawn until the third bell, the Petitioners' Plinth remains open; the named, by procedural right under the Charter, may speak from the Plinth before the Bowl. At the third bell, the Plinth closes; the Bowl of Methods is brought out onto the central flagstones; the seven named, still in vote-rank order, draw their methods. Executions begin with the first-named immediately after their draw and are conducted across the day, finishing before sunset.

### The methods (the seven from the Bowl)

The seven methods are fixed by the Compact of Stones and have not changed in a hundred years. The Bowl is a shallow bronze vessel; the seven iron lots are flat discs the size of a coin, each stamped with one method-glyph. The condemned draws their own lot — except in cases of physical incapacity, when the Master Teller draws.

In order of the Bowl's original arrangement (which is itself ceremonial, not predictive):
1. **The Rope.** A simple drop-gallows, assembled at the south flagstones. Quickest of the seven. The crowd-preferred outcome — there is, year over year, an informal sigh of relief when an early draw shows the rope.
2. **The Blade.** A single beheading-stroke on a low block. Considered honorable; old quay-folk slang calls it "the soldier's draw."
3. **The Wheel.** The body bound to a wagon-wheel laid flat on a stone trestle; the executioner breaks four major bones with a heavy iron bar and then concludes with a stroke to the throat. The slowest of the unambiguously-fatal methods and the one most often associated, in the broadsheets, with the worst of the seven names.
4. **The Cup.** A measured dose of black hellebore in wine. The condemned drinks; the body fails over about a quarter-hour. The only method in which the condemned is seated and speaks last words audibly to the crowd.
5. **The Stone.** Drowning in a tank of river-water with two iron weights at the ankles. The tank is wheeled out from the Tellers' Tower in the morning and wheeled back at noon. The body floats by the time the wheel is finished and is removed in front of the crowd.
6. **The Cold.** The condemned bound and lowered into a copper tub of iced water; the cold takes its time. The longest of the seven by elapsed minutes. Considered the cruelest by the Quay-folk and the most "scientific" by the Pearl Quarter, in roughly equal measure.
7. **The Fire.** A pyre built on the south flagstones, lit at the eighth bell of the day. The last of the seven by tradition, regardless of vote-rank, if it is drawn. The crowd disperses during the burn; it is the one method most citizens do not stay to watch.

The order of drawing follows the vote-rank; the order of execution follows the *draw* — fire always last, regardless. The other six are executed in the order they were drawn.

### Why the bowl-draw is the moral pillar

The lot system exists for one reason: to prevent anyone — accuser, conspirator, magistrate, or citizen — from choosing the death. The Compact of Stones is explicit. The naming is collective; the method is chance. To choose a method for a named citizen, even by proxy, would be the corruption the Compact was built to prevent. (The careful reader, after the reveal, will note that the Architect has chosen everything *except* the method. He has not violated the bowl. He has worked entirely around it. This is in character; he is the system's most rigorous believer.)

## The Petitioner System

### One-line definition
Between the Reading and the Ceremony, each named citizen may have one Petitioner — a private citizen who files publicly, on their behalf, to commute the verdict on grounds of error.

### How a filing works

1. **Selection.** The named citizen names their Petitioner verbally at the Petitioner's Hall, before the Examining Magistrate, in person. Most named citizens name their own family solicitor, a parish friend, or — increasingly, over the last twenty years — one of the small number of professional Petitioners (citizens like the Petitioner-we-know who specialize in filings, take no fee, and work out of small private offices in the Brass Ward). The named citizen may also accept a Petitioner who has already filed an *Intent to Represent* on their behalf — a one-page document filed by the Petitioner in advance of the Reading, declaring intent to take the case if the named citizen consents.
2. **The seventy-two-hour window.** A named citizen must accept or file a Petitioner by the sixth bell of Day 3 — the Charter's measure of seventy-two hours from the Reading, set to a fixed bell rather than a strict clock so each named has a clean final filing day. Later filings are procedurally lapsed. The Examining Magistrate has no discretion to extend the window. In practice, a named citizen who arrives at the Petitioner's Hall on the morning of Day 3 has until that day's sixth-bell hearing to file or accept.
3. **Binding consent.** A Petitioner is bound only by their own signed Intent or by in-person acceptance before the Bench. A relative's request, a parish-bell-keeper's referral, or a verbal nomination from any third party cannot bind a Petitioner without their own signature or in-person acceptance.
4. **The Intent to Represent.** The procedural device that the Petitioner-we-know's reputation runs on. A practicing Petitioner who studies the parish rolls and the rumor-flow of the Brass Ward can predict, with some skill, who is likely to be named — and can file an Intent in advance, so that on the morning of the Reading they are the first Petitioner available for the named. The Architect designed this device fifty years ago. He understood, when he designed it, that it was also the mechanism by which a Petitioner could be *taken out of play* before they ever met their client.
5. **The slot rule.** Each named citizen has exactly one Petitioner slot. If two Petitioners file Intents to Represent for the same named citizen, only the first-filed Intent stands. If a Petitioner files Intents to Represent for two different named citizens, they must, at the Reading, declare which one they will pursue; the other Intent is voided. **A Petitioner cannot, while their Intent is active for one named, file or accept any other case.** This is the slot rule.
6. **The second-slot vulnerability.** Because a Petitioner with an *already-filed, active* Intent for one named cannot file for any other named, a sufficiently patient conspiracy can take a target Petitioner out of play by inducing them — honestly, by their own hand — to file an Intent for someone else *before the Reading*. The induction may be a more sympathetic case, a parish friend's plea, or — as in book 1 — a fabricated case engineered to bait the Petitioner's known instincts.

   **How the Architect baited the Petitioner-we-know.** Three weeks before the Reading, the Architect provided the Petitioner-we-know — through two layers of intermediation, by routes the Petitioner trusted — with a fabricated case file: documentary evidence of a Brass Ward citizen who, on the basis of the planted evidence, the Petitioner found urgent and worthy. The evidence was false but compelling. The Petitioner filed his own Intent to Represent for that citizen, under his own hand, at the Petitioner's Hall, in the ordinary course of his practice. He did not know the case was a trap.

   **How the Architect placed that citizen second on the list.** During the Ten Days of Naming, the Architect — through the parish-clerk network at the Sailmaker's Voting House — engineered the clerk-written ballot clusters that put his chosen citizen in the second-place slot. (Roughly a third of low-tier ballots are clerk-written; clerk-clusters of the right size can move a name into the top seven without anyone outside the parish committee noticing.) After the Silent Days began and leaked parish totals identified the second-place named, the Architect did nothing further to make the trap work. The trap was already set. He did, separately, exercise a standing option he had purchased from Madam Vell at the Crooked Rule three weeks earlier — not because the trap needed it, but to bind her silence under the Crooked Rule's anonymity rules. That was the final seam.

   **The result on the morning of the Reading.** When Erys's name is read at the top of the list, the Petitioner-we-know moves immediately to file an Intent for her. He cannot. His prior Intent for the second-place named is active and binding under the slot rule. The Examining Magistrate has no discretion to waive it. The Petitioner-we-know is, by his own hand, bound to the manufactured case.

   The Architect did not bribe a stranger. He did not pay off a solicitor. He handed his own protégé the bait and watched him bite.

**Erys arrives at the Petitioner's Hall on the morning of Day 3 to confirm her Petitioner of choice. She is told the Petitioner-we-know is bound to another case. She is handed the public roll of practicing Petitioners — a printed list of forty names. The list is informational only; the Examining Magistrate has no power to compel any Petitioner to accept a case. None of the listed Petitioners are at the Hall, and none have filed an Intent for her, which means none have prepared. During the afternoon Erys sends messengers to the three most-decorated names on the roll. Two refuse the case by reply within the hour; the third arrives at the Hall at the fifth bell, reviews her file, and refuses on the bench, citing the volume of her record-breaking vote-margin and the impossibility of mounting a credible parish-fraud case in the time remaining. The sixth bell strikes. The seventy-two-hour window closes. Her right to a Petitioner is, by procedural default, lapsed. Her filing is rejected.**

The room is full. The Examining Magistrate is sympathetic but bound by procedure. The Petitioner-we-know is at that moment four blocks away at the Petitioner's Hall annex, beginning to realize what has happened.

### The hearing

If a Petitioner does succeed in filing on behalf of a named citizen, a public hearing is scheduled within forty-eight hours. The Petitioner has one hour of unbroken speech to make their case. They must produce evidence of error — *not* of innocence in the moral sense, but of error in the procedural sense: that the named citizen was placed on the list by fraud (parish-clerk ballot manipulation, duplicate ballots, miscounting), by mistaken identity (a same-name confusion), by procedurally-improper organization (a parish committee organizing a bloc vote — technically not illegal but, since the Architect's Charter, grounds for commutation), or by demonstrable factual error about the alleged offense that motivated the votes against them. The Examining Magistrate rules at the end of the hour. The ruling is final.

Petitions succeed on roughly one-of-five files. The Architect's lifetime rate was higher (he chose his cases). The Petitioner-we-know's rate is higher still (he chooses his cases more carefully, and refuses any case where he cannot find an error in the first day of work).

### The Public Lobby

Between the filing and the hearing, the Petitioner may speak publicly. This is the second pillar of the Charter — the *public lobby*. A Petitioner may publish in the broadsheets, may address the parish bell-keepers at Tellingday, may speak from the Petitioners' Plinth on the west side of Argent Square at any of the daily speaking-hours. The public cannot vote a name off the list — the list is sealed — but a sufficient public outcry against a verdict can pressure the Examining Magistrate's ruling. (No magistrate will admit this. Every magistrate is aware of it.)

**Speaking-hours.** The Plinth is open to any citizen at the second, fourth, and sixth bells each day during the Days of Petition. On Frostmonth 8 — Ceremony morning — the final speaking-hour runs from dawn until the third bell, before the Bowl draw. By procedural right under the Charter, the seven named themselves may speak from the Plinth during this hour, under escort from the Holding House. After the third bell the Plinth closes; the Bowl is brought out onto the south flagstones; the named draw in vote-rank order; executions begin immediately after each draw.

**Press throughput.** Six penny broadsheets print dawn extras during Ceremony week, and criers carry Plinth speeches into the four feeder streets within minutes. A speech given at dawn from the Plinth can be in print and on the streets across the city before the third bell, and a city-scale rejection can be mobilized inside a single morning if the speech lands.

**Press protections.** No Council office can enjoin a broadsheet before publication during the Days of Petition or during the Frostmonth 8 speaking-hour itself (dawn to the third bell); libel is punished only afterward. This was a deliberate provision of the Architect's Charter fifty years ago, intended to prevent any magistrate or senior official from suppressing a Petitioner's public argument while the case was live — and the extension of the no-enjoinment to Ceremony morning specifically (added by Charter amendment thirty-five years ago) was the Architect's own work, on the principle that the city's last word must not be silenced before the lot is drawn. It now protects Erys's story against his own influence.

**The civic-order suspension.** If, on Ceremony morning, the Chief Magistrate declares the square unable to witness in civic order — i.e., if the crowd's protest is sufficiently large and sustained that the Council's own jurists cannot pretend the witness is freely given — the day's Ceremony is suspended until the Council reconvenes to rule on the procedural objections. This power has existed since the Compact of Stones and has never been used in living memory. The climax of book 1 is the first use.

The book uses the public lobby as the engine of its climax. Erys cannot file (her Petitioner slot has lapsed). The Petitioner-we-know is bound to another case. But neither the slot rule nor the binding silences either of them in the public lobby. They cannot file a Petition. They can still talk to the city.

This is the field-of-battle the protagonists choose for the climax: not the Petitioner's Hall, where they cannot win, but Argent Square at dawn on the morning of the Ceremony, where the only remaining instruments are the Plinth, the broadsheets, the crowd, and the Chief Magistrate's civic-order ruling.

## What the System Cannot Do (the constraint catalog — for the drafter and plotter)

The world's "magic system" is the Ceremony, and per Sanderson's three laws it must constrain plot, not solve it. The drafter and plotter may not violate the following:

1. **The list cannot be unsealed.** Once the Master Teller has sealed it on Autumnmoon 21, no procedural mechanism in the world of book 1 can open it before dawn on Frostmonth 1. The drafter may not invent a reveal that re-opens the list.
2. **The Examining Magistrate's ruling is final.** No appeal. No higher court. The Compact of Stones forbids appeal explicitly. If Erys's filing is rejected on Day 3 it is rejected for the year.
3. **The method is drawn by lot.** No one — not the Architect, not the Petitioner, not Erys, not the Examining Magistrate — can choose, modify, or substitute the method. The drafter may not contrive a scene in which the lot is rigged. The Bowl is searched by three sworn witnesses at the eighth bell of the morning. The lots are weighed. The Architect knows this. He has never tried to corrupt the Bowl. He doesn't have to.
4. **The named citizen cannot withdraw their own name.** Once read, the name is on the list until the Ceremony or commutation.
5. **The slot rule binds.** A Petitioner with an active Intent cannot represent another named, and the Examining Magistrate has no discretion to waive this. The drafter may not invent a procedural exception. (This is the source of the trap; it is also the source of the trap's mechanical integrity.)
6. **The public lobby is unregulated.** A Petitioner barred from filing is not barred from speaking. A named citizen herself is not barred from speaking. Anyone may speak from the Petitioners' Plinth at speaking-hours. This is the loophole the climax uses. It is not a cheat; it has been in the Charter for fifty years; the Architect built it himself, on principle, because he believed at twenty-three that the public should never be silenced and he has not changed his mind in fifty years even as it now threatens him. *This is the moral architecture of his betrayal.*
7. **No private vengeance is lawful, including against the named.** The night attempt on Erys's life on the night of Day 4 (the night between Day 4 and Day 5) is — by long custom and the watch's blind-night habit — a *practical* possibility, but it is not legal. Anyone who killed Erys outside the Ceremony would themselves be killed at the next Ceremony, almost certainly with the rope on first-name draw. The conspirator who tries it is operating outside the Architect's chain — a parish-committee operator with their own reason to want Erys dead before the Ceremony reveals something. (Iceberg details are in `iceberg.md`; the world's procedural rules support this exact reading.)

## Set-Piece Compatibility (re-confirmed)

The world as designed supports every set-piece the intent file calls for:

- **The Reading at dawn in Argent Square** — supported by geography (Argent Square, ~20k capacity), procedure (the Sealing-to-Reading flow), and culture (mandatory civic ritual).
- **The Petitioner's Hall hearing where Erys's filing is rejected** — supported by the slot rule, the seventy-two-hour Intent window, the Architect's engineered manufactured-case bait on the Petitioner-we-know, the parish-clerk-cluster manipulation that placed the bait's subject in the second slot, and the procedural inflexibility of the Examining Magistrate.
- **The night attempt on Erys's life** — supported by the unlit alleys of the Low Quays, the watch's blind-night custom on the night of Day 4 (the night between Day 4 and Day 5), and the procedural availability of a conspirator outside the Architect's chain.
- **The vote-buyer's parlour** — supported by the Crooked Rule, Madam Vell, the ballot-and-slot markets, and the cultural cover of the parlour's gambling license.
- **The Ceremony at the climax** — supported by the Bowl-and-Lot procedure, the seven methods, the order-of-draw and order-of-execution rules, and the geography of the south flagstones.
- **The Architect watching the Petitioner's window from his garden** — supported by the Pearl Quarter ridge-line, the line-of-sight from the Iron-Lily House's back garden, the Architect's habituated morning routine, and the cultural plausibility of a revered elder's "morning walk" being unremarkable.
- **The climax in the public lobby** — supported by the Charter's public-lobby provision, the Petitioners' Plinth in Argent Square, the daily speaking-hours, and the cultural seriousness with which the city takes a Petitioner-grade voice in the square.

## A note on the Architect's design integrity

Write the Architect as sincere in his belief that the Petitioner system gives the named a real chance against the list, and active in his use of that same system to select which of the named the chance extends to. He built the first, and he has worked the second for fifty years. The book is the first year the second has been visible enough to be caught.

When the drafter writes a scene involving the Architect explaining the Petitioner system to Erys (and there will be such a scene; the intent file calls for it on Day 4 or 5), the Architect should speak his real reasons. He does not lie about the procedure. He explains the slot rule honestly. He explains the Intent to Represent honestly. He explains the public lobby with particular love — he believes in it. He omits only the one fact that would link him to the manufactured-case bait and the clerk-cluster manipulation, and even there he does not lie; he simply does not surface it. The careful reader, on re-read, will notice that he leaves nothing important out except *himself*.
