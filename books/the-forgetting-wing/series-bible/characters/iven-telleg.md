# Iven Telleg

role: victim / inciting body
pov: no (deceased before page one)
arc: negative (completed off-page; he died because he had begun to change)
first-appearance: chapter 1 (corpse); chapter 9 (in case file); chapter 16 (in his recovered date-journal)
aliases: the body in the alley (ch 1-4); Citizen Telleg (Wing file); Iven (his sister Halse, never on-page; his journal entries)

## Want (external goal at moment of death)

To get one date-journal page — the one he wrote two nights before he died — out of the Wing's building and into the hands of someone in the Marrow Quarter who could read it without being seen reading it. He believed if the page reached a specific Rememberer cell (he was wrong about which cell; he had the wrong address) it would be preserved long enough to matter. He never made the drop.

## Need (internal truth)

To accept that he had been complicit, for eleven years, in a category of work that was not what he had been told it was — and that the appropriate response was not a private journal but a public testimony he was not brave enough to give. The book's tragedy of him is that he started to do the right thing in the smallest possible way and was killed for it anyway.

## Wound

Year three of his Wing employment, age twenty-four. He built a W-I cartridge for a Vell-Outer citizen whose flagged content was logged as *witness, unauthorized facility* — Iven did not ask which facility. The citizen died on the table during the procedure. The death was logged as cascade-cardiac. Iven attended the Sequence Memorial that year and lit a candle and did not say what he had built. He has carried that night for eight years. It is the night he began to keep notes — not in writing, at first. In what he could afterward call a habit of remembering on purpose.

## Lie they believe

*I will know when it is time to stop.* For eleven years he believed his conscience was a reliable instrument that would tell him when his work had crossed a line he could not return from. The lie permitted him to continue building cartridges that erased witnesses to off-the-books deaths because none of them, individually, had quite been the one that crossed the line. The lie was tested by a single cartridge in his last six months — a Wing-Internal cartridge ordered for a fellow technician, a friend of his named Kessen, who had asked the wrong question about the Cold Archive. Iven built that cartridge. Kessen survived but lost the year. That was the line. Iven began the journal a week later. Two months after that he was dead.

## Ghost

A Wednesday evening, twelve months before his death, on Floor Minus Eight. Iven was logging a routine cartridge into the Cold Archive when he saw, on the inventory terminal, a citizen's name he recognized — the mother of a Cant schoolfriend from his childhood. The cartridge category was *W-I, retroactive, year three.* He realized, standing there, that the woman had not died in a Plate Fever winter as the obituary said. She had been threaded out of being a witness to something, and the thread had cascaded. He logged the cartridge. He went home. He cooked a meal he did not eat. He did not tell anyone. He began, that night, to count the W-I cartridges he had built and to memorize the citizen names. He had built forty-one. He could name thirty-nine.

## Competence

- **Cartridge fabrication.** Eleven years a Cartographer of Recall, two years a senior Cartographer with W-I clearance. He could read a biometric index faster than anyone in his theater. He built cartridges that were, technically, the most precise in his cohort — which is part of why he was promoted into W-I.
- **Memory of citizen names and dates.** He had committed thirty-nine of forty-one W-I subjects to private memory. This is the load-bearing competence the plot uses: the journal he was murdered to suppress contains those thirty-nine names, fragmentary but recoverable.
- **Quiet.** He could spend a full Wing shift without speaking and have it read as professional. He has been doing it for eight years.

## Contradiction

He bred fancy mice. Three generations, two cages, in his apartment in Estre-Hand. He named them after Wing protocol numbers — the joke a private one — and was unable to euthanize any of them when they got old; the apartment had retired mice eating well into their senescence. The Wing knew about the mice (every apartment is inspected annually) and let it pass. Maren will see the cages when she breaks into his apartment in ch 17 and the mice will be the moment the body in the alley becomes a person to her.

## Flaw

Conflict avoidance pitched as professionalism. He never raised an objection in eleven years. The journal was the first form of objection he ever practiced, and he practiced it alone, and he died with thirty-nine names on rice paper that no one had read. The plot exploits this: he could have, at multiple points, said something to someone — to his sister Halse, to Iren Hael (whom he respected at distance), to the Audit — and did not. His silence is what made his murder a viable solution to the problem he posed.

## Voice (as recovered through his date-journal entries — the only on-page voice he has)

- **Vocabulary:** Wing-technical, dry, with a vein of older Cant-Saxonate underneath when he relaxed. He used the word *index* for *day*. He used citizen names in full, surname-first, like an obituary writer. He did not use the polite register; his journal calls the procedure *the cut*, which is a Marrow-side word he had never used aloud in his life.
- **Sentence length default:** Short and complete. Subject-verb-object, terminal period. Occasional date entry that runs three lines and breaks into the older register: *I cooked the egg I could not eat and watched the lake darken and considered the names I have built into silence and could not finish the count.*
- **Avoids talking about:** Kessen by name (initials only: *K., today, did not recognize the corridor*). His sister. Any prediction about his own future — the journal is past-tense entirely.
- **Speech tics (in journal):** *On this day,* — opens every entry. *To no one,* — addresses an unnamed reader he is not certain will exist.
- **Subtext default:** Understatement. Catastrophes are recorded as small observations.

## Physical

Thirty-five at death. Average build, slightly soft from desk work. Brown hair beginning to grey at the temples — a feature the Wing's internal medicine had flagged as early-stage Late-Stage Cognitive Smoothing onset, which would have meant retirement within five years. Hands long-fingered, ink-stained on the right side from the journal habit. The scalp port behind his left ear is the senior Wing-issued model with the W-I clearance pin — a tiny brass dot the size of a poppy seed. The pin is what makes the body anomalous in Sael's first review: a citizen's body should not have that pin. Wing personnel files should have flagged him as Wing employee at first biometric scan; the file is being delayed by someone above Iren. At the moment of death he was wearing his off-duty clothes — wool coat, civilian trousers, no Wing insignia — and a single mouse hair on his cuff that Maren will not notice and Sael will.

The wound: a kitchen paring knife, single thrust, between the fifth and sixth ribs on the left side, angled up. Death from cardiac perforation, three to six minutes. The angle suggests a right-handed attacker shorter than Iven — which is to say, Maren-sized. (Anyone Maren-sized.)

## Arc beats (compressed; he is dead at chapter 1)

- **Planted (in the file Maren and Sael read):** ch 4-5 — He is a junior employee with no file irregularities. The story the Wing tells about him is dull. The plant: a single line in the file noting *clerical reassignment, six months prior, Floor Minus Three to Floor Minus Eight.* Nobody clerical moves down four floors.
- **Tested (when his journal surfaces):** ch 17-18 — Maren breaks into his apartment, finds the mice, finds a single rice-paper sheet folded into one of the cages' wood-shaving liners. The sheet has six initials and a date. The other thirty-three sheets are not yet recovered. Sael, in parallel, reads the file with the procedural fingerprint and realizes a senior hand has been editing it.
- **Resolved (his story closes):** ch 22 — Maren says his name aloud at the end of the climax. *Iven Telleg.* The first time anyone says it without redaction. He is not avenged in book 1. He is named.

## Knowledge (at moment of death; immutable thereafter)

- **knew:**
  - That W-I cartridges existed and were ordered by a chain of authority that did not pass through Iren Hael or any of the named field directors.
  - The names of thirty-nine W-I subjects, dates, and partial content categories.
  - That the Cold Archive retained engram data — he had seen the storage protocol himself in year nine.
  - That Kessen had asked about the Archive and been threaded for the asking.
  - That his own annual Review was due in six weeks and that he, personally, was almost certainly going to be on a W-I cartridge himself this cycle. He was building, in the journal, the case for his own future innocence — a record his future post-Review self would not be allowed to access.
- **believed (incorrectly):**
  - That the Rememberer cell in the Marrow at the Helvet Stair drop he chose was active and competent. (It had been compromised the previous autumn. The watcher at that drop reports to the Wing.)
  - That his journal habit was undetected. (It was detected six weeks before his death. Surveillance had read three of the entries via the wall-mounted cohort camera he had not realized was angled to catch his desk.)
  - That his murder, when it came, would be a clean Wing extraction — a pickup, a Review, an erasure. He did not consider that he would be used as a frame.
- **didn't know:**
  - That Maren existed as a refusal-response carrier under longitudinal monitoring.
  - The name Andren Sault as anything other than Charter-era authorial credit.
  - Who had ordered his death. (He had narrowed it to three possibilities; the actual orchestrator was not on his list.)
  - That his sister Halse, whom he had not spoken to in four years for reasons he could not fully account for, had been threaded against him on his own W-I order — built by a junior Cartographer Iven had trained.
