## Position and worldview

Halver Mire is the **Deputy Director of the Ministry of Public Cognition** — the civilian-side senior official who nominally oversees the Wing's policy interface, in practice runs the long internal play the bible's iceberg notes as item 8 (the stitch network as channel against the Chair). He is sixty-four. He has been in the Ministry forty years. He was a junior aide on the Concord Charter drafting committee and is the only living person besides Andren Sault who knows what the Charter was for.

He is not Sault's loyalist. He is Sault's rival — the one rival Sault has tolerated because removing him would require a Wing-Internal cartridge against a citizen too senior for the cover story to hold. Mire has spent twenty years building the conditions under which the Chair can be exposed and replaced. The body in Sennet's Alley is one move in that twenty-year game.

His name in this slot is a deliberate choice: **Halver** is the namesake of half the city's streets. The reader who notices, late, will register that the man hidden inside book 1's mystery is the one whose name has been on every signpost.

## Want (external goal in book 1)

Three stacked Wants, resolved in sequence by the end of the book:

1. **Remove Iven Telleg.** Telleg's date-journal was beginning to accumulate names that, if recovered, would attach to specific W-I cartridges Mire himself ordered fifteen years ago — content he needs gone before his own move against Sault. The kill is internal hygiene.
2. **Frame Maren Vasse.** Maren is a known refusal-response carrier, longitudinally monitored. Mire wants her in Wing custody where her testimony — partial, stitch-fragmented, deniable — can be staged as a Charter-era recurrence: a citizen whose unmoderated memory turned her violent. The framing is preparation for a political argument Mire wants to make to the Concord Assembly in eighteen months. He needs a contemporary example.
3. **Destabilize Sael Korrin.** Sael is being groomed — by Mire, through Iren — to become the youngest Audit officer in Wing history. Sael's discovery of small wrong things in the Sennet's Alley case is intended to inoculate him against trusting the field directorate. Mire wants Sael in the Audit by year's end. Sael does not know he is being shaped. Iren does not know he has been the shaping hand.

If all three Wants land cleanly, Mire's eighteen-month timeline against Sault advances. The book's climax denies him part of one Want (Maren is not in custody; Sael does not cross with her, which is what Mire wanted, but he also does not enter the Audit cleanly). Mire absorbs the partial loss. Book 2 is his next move.

## Need (internal truth)

To accept that he has become the thing he set out to oppose — that thirty years of incremental moves against Sault have made him a man who orders W-I cartridges as routinely as the Chair does. He will not get to this in book 1. He will not get to it in book 2. He may get to it in book 3 or never.

## Wound

Year four of his Ministry career, age twenty-six. Mire wrote a memo to Andren Sault — Sault was still publicly the Director then — arguing that the Bureau-era trials were continuing under the Wing's banner and that the Concord Charter had been written, in part, to launder them. The memo was elegant, accurate, and naive. Sault read it. Sault did not respond. Three months later Mire's older brother, a Lowmaren manufactory worker named Tessen Mire, died of what was logged as cardiac arrest during his second annual Review. Mire understood the message. He stopped writing memos. He began the long game. He has not mentioned his brother's name in any record for thirty-eight years.

## Lie they believe

*Replacing the Chair is the same as ending the Wing's worst practices.* The lie is what permits Mire to use the same instruments he intends to dismantle. He has been telling himself, for thirty years, that once Sault is removed and Mire holds the Directorate, the W-I program will be wound down within five years. The lie does not survive its own test. Mire has, in fact, expanded the W-I program twice in the last decade — small, justified expansions, each one closing a problem of his own making. He notices this, in himself, on bad nights. He has a stitch supply he uses personally. (He is the only senior Ministry official the bible records as a private stitch user.)

## Ghost

Mire at twenty-six, the night he understood what had happened to his brother. He sat in the kitchen of the apartment he and Tessen had shared until Tessen married, and he poured a glass of the cheap Lowmaren brandy Tessen used to drink, and he did not drink it. He poured it down the sink at dawn. He has not poured a drink in thirty-eight years. The brandy bottle is still in his apartment, unfilled.

## Competence

- **Institutional patience.** Thirty-eight years of moves that look like nothing. Mire has placed seventeen of his protégés in mid-level Wing positions over the last twenty years. None of them know they are protégés. None of them know the others exist.
- **The forged-routine instinct.** Mire can write a routing instruction that reads as procedural to seven sets of reviewing eyes. The Sennet's Alley case's anomalies are anomalies only because the routing is too clean. He overcorrected. (Iren will eventually notice. Sael will not.)
- **Charter literacy.** Mire is one of the three living people who knows what the original Bureau documents said. He has copies. They are not in the Cold Archive. They are in a safe-deposit box at a private bank in Hael-by-Plate under a name that has not been used since 2018.

## Contradiction

He grows fruit on a small private terrace overlooking the Plate — figs, a single old peach tree, three rows of strawberries in summer. He gives the figs to a Vell-Outer convalescent ward every September. The ward staff know him by sight and not by name. (Iren Hael's retirement house, the one he has paid the deposit on, will turn out to be three doors down from the ward. Mire chose the ward in part because it was near a property he had quietly arranged Iren to be steered toward. The intricacy is the man.)

## Flaw

He has organized his entire moral life around a deferred reckoning — the day Sault falls, the day the program winds down, the day he writes the memo he could not write at twenty-six. The deferral has become the structure. He is, by book 1, no longer pursuing the reckoning; he is pursuing the conditions for it, perpetually. The Sennet's Alley case is the latest in a long line of conditions. He cannot stop adding conditions.

## Voice (off-page; rendered only through documents and one phone call overheard at distance)

- **Vocabulary:** Charter-era formal Latinate, mixed with Lowmaren-Saxonate when alone or with very few. He uses *probative* and *evidentiary* like Iren but more precisely; he uses *Tessen* (his brother's name) only in his head. He does not use the Wing's polite register; he uses the citizen polite register in public speeches as a deliberate signaling choice.
- **Sentence length default:** Long, structured, with subordinate clauses that close cleanly. A Mire sentence in a routing instruction can be parsed as a paragraph. He prefers the semicolon.
- **Avoids talking about:** Sault. The Bureau. His brother. The figs.
- **Speech tics:** *Allow* — used in routing instructions where another official would use *approve.* *In due course* — used to defer a decision he has already made. *I will consider it* — a phrase he means literally; he does, and decides, and never returns to the speaker.
- **Subtext default:** Mire does not perform anywhere in his life. He is one of the rare characters in the cast who would defeat the Lattice not by training but because there is no significant gap between what he says and what he means. He has resolved the gap by changing what he means.

## Physical (recorded for book-2+ continuity; never on-page in book 1)

Sixty-four. Tall, very thin, with the slight tremor of a long-term low-dose stitch user. Hair white, kept Ministry-formal. A long thin scar along the line of his jaw from a childhood Lowmaren accident — not visible at distance. Hands long-fingered, precise, the nails clean. The scalp port behind his left ear is the Tier-One Ministry-issued model with the senior officials' silver inlay. He has had ten annual Reviews and one W-I cartridge; the W-I cleared a single afternoon eight years ago, the content category not in any file. He does not know which afternoon. He does not look for it.

## Arc beats (book 1; presence rather than scene work)

- **Planted:** ch 2 — The Sennet's Alley file is routed to Iren without an Audit stamp. (The omission is Mire's. Iren does not connect it.)
- **Tested:** ch 11-18 — Three procedural anomalies cross Iren's desk and are attributable, on close reading, to a senior hand. Sael's interrogation summaries appear, via the back-channel, on a terminal in Mire's office; Mire reads them at the end of each shift. The Lowmaren manufactory leak that supplies Pell is reviewed by Mire's office on a monthly cadence. None of this appears on-page; the drafter is told.
- **Resolved (book 1's piece):** ch 22-23 — A piece of Ministry stationery surfaces on Iren's desk among the file's routing slips. The initials *H.M.* are in the lower-right of a single sheet. Iren registers it. Sael does not. Maren has by this point chosen to live remembering rather than die proving; her choice removes her from Mire's immediate access. He absorbs the loss without expression. He pours a glass of water in his apartment and drinks it. He plans book 2.

## Knowledge

- **knows:**
  - That Andren Sault is alive and is the Chair.
  - That Wing-Internal exists as a category; he has personally ordered ninety-three W-I cartridges over thirty years (he can name eighty-seven of the subjects; the other six he ordered through intermediaries and chose not to be told).
  - That the Cold Archive retains all threaded data.
  - That the Truth Lattice has a back-channel.
  - That Eira Vasse was a refusal-response carrier under longitudinal monitoring and that Maren inherited the pedagogy.
  - That Sael's mother is alive in House Andren.
  - That Iren Hael's wife was W-I.
  - That the stitch leak from Lowmaren is being tolerated by his own office.
  - The names of the four other cities with Wings and the informal chain of command among the Directors.
- **believes (incorrectly):**
  - That his own erased afternoon eight years ago was a routine internal hygiene cartridge he himself ordered against himself. (He did not order it. Sault did. Mire does not know.)
  - That his long game is winnable in the eighteen-month window he has set for himself. (Sault has known about the long game for nine years and has been routing around it.)
  - That his protégés will, when the time comes, follow him rather than Sault. (Three of the seventeen are also Sault's. Mire will not learn this in book 1.)
- **doesn't know:**
  - That Sault has known about him for nine years.
  - That his own afternoon eight years ago contained a conversation with a Bureau-era colleague whose name would, if recovered, end Mire's career.
