# Sael Korrin

role: co-protagonist / antagonist-to-Maren
pov: yes
arc: negative (with positive flickers that fail to convert by book end)
first-appearance: chapter 2
aliases: Inquisitor Korrin (formal, by colleagues and witnesses); the boy (Pell, once, dismissive); Sael (by Maren, after ch 14)

## POV voice — distinguishing rules (binding)

These rules differentiate Sael on a single paragraph from Maren. The drafter and the voice-scrubber are bound by them.

1. **Thinks in lists and counts.** Sael's interiority enumerates. Possibilities arrive as numbered options. Witnesses are sorted into categories. *Three things were wrong with the body. One: the angle of the knife. Two: the absence of biometrics. Three: she had not run.* The list is the texture.
2. **Explicit measurement, once per chapter, minimum.** Every Sael chapter contains at least one numeric reading from him: a count of seconds, a distance in meters, a dose in milligrams, a number of breaths, the chimes from the Sequence clock. The pattern is binding. Do not strip on line-edit.
3. **Complete sentences as default.** Where Maren fragments, Sael completes. Even under stress his clauses close. The mask is grammar. When the grammar finally cracks (climax chapter), the crack is the event.
4. **Lattice-tap interiority.** When the Truth Lattice fires, Sael does not narrate the feeling — he names the tap. *Soft-three.* *Long suppression.* *Fabrication, two cycles.* The codes appear in italics and are not re-explained.
5. **Avoids:** the word *mother* (he does not have one, he believes); the word *afraid*; any direct admission of pain from the jaw inflammation; the word *friend*. Uses circumlocutions: *the woman in the file*, *the discomfort*, *my cohort*.

## Want (external goal)

To close the Sennet's Alley case under Wing protocol — identify the killer, recover the recovered memory, return the citizen to the program — within the standard fourteen-day window before the case is escalated to Audit. He wants to do it cleanly enough that his cohort name appears in the next year's Sequence honors.

## Need (internal truth)

To understand that competence in service of a corrupt institution is not virtue, and that the institution he has loved as a parent has been lying to him about who he is. He needs to find the seam between *the Wing as I understand it* and *the Wing as it actually is* — and he needs to do this before the back-channel on his Truth Lattice (which he does not know exists) breaks him.

He does not get there in book 1. He glimpses it and turns away.

## Wound

Age three, the day the Wing took his mother. He does not remember it; the official record says she died of complications from a cascaded Review and that he was placed in State wardship. He was raised by the Wing's ward system from three to eleven, then entered formal Inquisitor training. He has organized his entire identity around being chosen by the Wing because being chosen was the explanation he was given for why he had no one. The wound underneath: he was not chosen. He was taken. His mother is alive in House Andren in Vell-Outer, eight kilometers from his apartment, registered as a Bleeder.

## Lie they believe

Two stacked lies, and the climax tests only the upper one.

- **Upper lie (testable in book 1):** *The Wing is necessary. The cost it imposes on citizens is real and tragic, and is less than the cost of unmoderated memory. My job is moral because the alternative is worse.*
- **Lower lie (planted, not paid off in book 1; book-2 engine):** *My erased days are what the Wing told me they were — operational matters, witnessed harm, training content the Ministry judged unsuitable for me to carry.* In fact one of those days is the day in his seventh year on which he was permitted to meet his mother once and remember her face. He believes he has never met her. He has. He has been threaded to forget her three separate times.

## Ghost

The morning of his eleventh birthday, the day he was given his cohort number. The Director's deputy (not Sault, a lieutenant) shook his hand in a corridor on Floor Minus Four and said, *We saw you, child. The city saw you. You were the one we were waiting for.* Sael had cried, once, briefly, in the bathroom afterward — not because he was sad but because being seen had felt like being given a name. He has been working to be seen that cleanly again ever since. The deputy does not remember the sentence. Sael remembers it verbatim.

## Competence

- **Reading bodies through the Lattice plus six years of training.** He is the best in his cohort and one of the three best field Inquisitors in the Wing. He can run a forty-minute interrogation without water and come out with the subject's truth-frame mapped. The plot taxes this: Maren reads as anomalous to him — the Lattice fires patterns he has never seen, and he cannot tell whether she is the most controlled subject he has ever interviewed or whether the Lattice itself is wrong about her.
- **Memorization.** He has the city's transit schedule, the Wing's case-numbering system, and twelve thousand citizens' biometric thumbnails in working memory. He can place a Cant grandmother by surname-and-block within four seconds.
- **Carrothenidine tolerance management.** He has learned, without admitting it, how to schedule his doses to keep the jaw quiet without slurring at interview. The plot taxes this when his supply is interrupted in ch 17.

## Contradiction

He keeps a small folded paper crane on his desk at the Wing. He made it from a Wing pamphlet his second year of training. He does not know why he keeps it; it is the only object in his apartment that is not Wing-issued or functional. He folds another one every six months and burns the previous. The cranes are the only ritual he has that is not on his duty roster. (The reader will not be told this; the drafter may show it once.)

## Flaw

He cannot sit with an unresolved interpretation. When the Lattice produces ambiguous output he forces a reading rather than holding the ambiguity — because holding it costs him the certainty that justifies the interrogation. The case against Maren exploits this: a setup is precisely the sort of evidence that reads ambiguous to a Lattice operator, and Sael will resolve the ambiguity in the direction of guilt because the alternative is harder.

## Voice

- **Vocabulary:** Wing-internal jargon (cartridge, threading, tap-code, cohort, cordon, Lattice-fatigue, mirror-recall) used the way a senior surgeon uses surgical terms — casually, accurately, never explained. Plain Saxonate underneath when not on duty; the Wing trained the Latinate in but his Estre-Hand state-ward upbringing trained it on top of a Cant-adjacent first vocabulary, and the lower layer surfaces when he is tired. No profanity. He has never sworn aloud in his life and considers profanity a Lattice-tell.
- **Sentence length default:** Even, medium-length, complete. Bimodal toward longer when he is constructing a case in his head; sharp and clipped when he is mid-interrogation. The contrast is the rhythm. Long when alone, short when on subject.
- **Avoids talking about:** his mother (he believes he has none, so the avoidance reads to others as unflinching honesty and to himself as nothing); the carrothenidine; the back-channel suspicion he has not consciously formed yet; whether he is afraid of Maren. He will say *I find her difficult* and mean *I am afraid of her*.
- **Speech tics:** *To be precise* — used before a clarifying clause, two or three times per chapter, especially when he is about to soften something. *The count is* — used when stating a number aloud. Asks for the number of something before he asks for the meaning. Says citizen names with surname-first formality even in conversation with their relatives (*Citizen Vasse, your aunt*).
- **Subtext default:** When stressed, he becomes more procedural, not less. The mask is competence. He will list options he has already rejected as a way of avoiding the option he has not yet faced.

## Physical

Sixteen, slender, half a head taller than Maren. State-ward thinness — the wardship fed him adequately, never abundantly. Hair black, kept short on the State-employee side of the regulation, exposing the matte port behind his left ear and the slightly raised scar at the jaw-hinge where the Lattice mesh seats. The scar inflames in cold weather and he holds his jaw at an angle to favor it without realizing he does. Hands narrow, nails kept very short, no rings. He wears the Inquisitor's slate-grey field jacket buttoned to the throat in all weather; the cohort took to calling him *the closed collar* in training, briefly, before he made the joke unfunny by not reacting. Brown eyes. A pale, persistent flush along the right cheekbone — carrothenidine side-effect, attributable to many other things.

## Arc beats

- **Planted:** ch 2, 4 — His first chapter establishes the count, the list, the Lattice. The first time he applies the Lattice to Maren (ch 3 interview, his POV in ch 4) it returns a pattern he refuses to log accurately because logging it ambiguously preserves the case. The lie about his job and the flaw about ambiguity are both on the page in his first interrogation scene.
- **Tested:** ch 12-15 — The case turns. The midpoint reveals the body's placement and the missing biometrics fit a setup, not a perpetrator. The Lattice's back-channel transmits an interrogation summary that contradicts what Sael logged, and his supervisor Iren begins asking softer questions than usual. The carrothenidine supply tightens. The jaw inflammation breaks through. He continues to choose the Wing's frame because choosing otherwise is choosing himself an orphan with no institution to be the parent.
- **Resolved (in the negative direction):** ch 22-24 — Alone with Maren on the question of what to do with what they both now know, Sael does not cross with her. He has the truth — that the body was a W-I technician, that someone inside the Wing set this up — and he chooses to report it through the chain of command rather than act on it. He believes this is the moral choice. The reader sees that it is the obedient one. The cost: he loses Maren as an alliance, and the Wing — through the back-channel he does not know about — now has him flagged. He goes home and folds a crane and does not burn the last one. The seam is opening. Book 2 walks through it.

## His version of the story (antagonist depth — the book from his POV in three sentences)

A sixteen-year-old Inquisitor, the youngest his service has ever fielded, is given a case that matters: a fellow Wing employee dead in a Cant alley, killed by a citizen the Wing's biometric net cannot match, with one of her remembered days belonging to a category his training does not recognize. He works the case the way he has been taught to work cases — meticulously, kindly, without flinching from the hard interview — and as he does it he begins to see that the Wing he has loved has factions inside it, that his case is being shaped by someone above him, and that the suspect in front of him may be the only person in the city who is telling him the truth. He must decide whether the Wing is what it says it is or what its enemies say it is; he chooses to believe the institution that raised him, and he is wrong, and the book ends with him not yet able to see that he is wrong.

The reader will sometimes side with him. That is intended.

## Knowledge

Initial state at book open (continuity-checker owns updates after drafting begins):

- **knows:**
  - The full Wing case-numbering and protocol system; the Truth Lattice's documented capabilities and the official 94% accuracy figure (he privately knows the real number is 82%).
  - The names and case histories of every Inquisitor in his cohort and the three cohorts above him.
  - The published version of the Long Damage and the Concord Charter as taught at the ward school.
  - Standard interrogation kinesics, including the Cant-grandmother subset (his year-six rotation was through Cant).
  - The carrothenidine dose schedule he has stabilized on (two tablets shift-start, one mid-shift, one before sleep). He knows the dose. He does not call it dependence.
  - That his supervisor, Iren Hael, was on the same case-type rotation under a different mentor twenty years ago and recommended Sael for the Sennet's Alley assignment.
  - That his mother died of cascade complications when he was three. He knows the case file by number. He has not read it in five years.
- **believes (incorrectly):**
  - That the Truth Lattice broadcasts only to him — that the loop is closed. (The back-channel transmits to a Wing supervisor in real time. He does not know.)
  - That his three erased days were operational content as briefed. (At least one was the day he met his mother in year seven.)
  - That his mother is dead.
  - That the Wing has no individual director — that governance is committee, as the citizen literature implies. The name Andren Sault is to him a Charter-era authorial credit and a long-dead administrator.
  - That his assignment to this case is a normal merit posting. (Someone inside the Wing wants Sael specifically destabilized by it; Iren is the proximate placer, the orchestrator behind Iren is not yet on-page.)
  - That the Wing is internally uniform on procedure. (It has factions; W-I is a category the field staff are not told about.)
  - That Maren took stitch and is therefore an ordinary memory-crime suspect with an unusual constellation of evidence.
- **doesn't know:**
  - That Wing-Internal exists as a category of erasure.
  - That the body in the alley was a W-I technician.
  - That his mother is alive in House Andren in Vell-Outer.
  - That the Truth Lattice has a back-channel and that his interrogations are being audited above him in real time.
  - That the Cold Archive on Floor Minus Eight retains all threaded data.
  - That Floor Minus Eleven exists in the sense of being a working theater rather than an architectural rumor he has heard once over drinks.
  - The existence of the Chair (Andren Sault as living Director).
  - That his carrothenidine is dosed with a long-term tolerance signature the Wing's medical staff are tracking.
