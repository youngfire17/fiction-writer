# Cultures

Use for: any scene with dialogue, gesture, public ritual, professional behavior, or a character interpreting another character's social class. Load when writing crowd scenes, family scenes, or any encounter that crosses tier or district lines.

## The State and its story

The State of Halvern-Estre describes itself in its own literature as the **Concord** — the polity that, after the catastrophe of the pre-Wing decade, agreed that some forms of public knowledge are too costly to be carried by individual citizens. The Concord's official ideology is **stewarded forgetting**: the city as a community that takes turns carrying weight, with the Ministry of Public Cognition as the redistributor of cognitive burden. The image on every Wing pamphlet is a hand passing a stone to another hand. The slogan, embossed on tram seats and pharmacy receipts, is "**You are loved. The city remembers for you.**"

The Concord does not present itself as authoritarian. It presents itself as gentle, post-traumatic, and democratically legitimated. Citizens vote (the Concord Assembly is elected every four years). The Wing is described as a public health service, not a security organ. This framing is real to most citizens. The brutality of the system is invisible because it is structural rather than spectacular.

## How citizens talk about the procedure

- **Official register:** *the Review, the Consolidation, your day.*
- **Polite register:** *the rest, the trim, the kindness.* "I had my kindness in March."
- **Tense register:** *the take, the dock, the lift.* Used by people about to undergo it; rarely used after.
- **Resistance register:** *the cut, the bleach, the wash.* Marrow Quarter, stitch users, certain artists.
- **Children's rhyme** (Cant schoolyard, four-line, no one will write it down but everyone knows it):
  - *One little hand to take the day*
  - *One little hand to pass it on*
  - *One little day for the city's sake*
  - *Wake up clean, wake up gone.*
  The rhyme is technically a Class C violation (it presupposes the day was taken from a specific person) but enforcement is rare. Teachers shush it; mothers sing it.
- **Jokes:** the standard form is the date-substitution joke. "I'd remember, but it was a Tuesday." Bartenders in Lowmaren make this joke twice an hour. It is funny because saying it is almost compliance-failure but not quite.
- **Taboos:** asking what someone did last [specific date]. Showing someone a photograph of themselves on a date they cannot place. Telling a child what their parent did before they were born. The third taboo is the strongest. The city's grandparents carry a weight the city's grandchildren cannot ask about.

## Class strata and tiered Review

The Review is not the same procedure for every citizen.

- **Tier One (Hael-by-Plate, senior State, certain industrial families):** sealed-petition opt-in. The citizen may request the *date* of the taken day in advance, receive a courtesy briefing on the *category* of content (e.g. "personal grief," "professional witness"), and may refuse one Review per decade with no penalty. Lethonal-7 is administered at a reduced dose. Mirror-recall in the technician is monitored more carefully. Tier One citizens age with their memory more intact than the rest of the city.
- **Tier Two (Estre-Hand professional class, most of Vell-Outer's middle):** standard Review, no advance disclosure, but the citizen may, after the fact, apply for a "carry note" — a one-sentence summary of the content category, delivered in a sealed envelope, opened in the presence of a counselor. Most Tier Twos never apply. Applying is logged.
- **Tier Three (Cant, Lowmaren, the unregistered Marrow):** standard Review, no disclosure of any kind, no carry-note option. The citizen is told a day was taken and that the city is grateful. Maren is Tier Three. So was her mother.
- **Tier Zero (the unported, the Bleeders, certain children):** no Review. Different supervision regimes apply (see iceberg.md).

The tiers are not legally codified. Officially every citizen receives the same Review. The tiering is administrative practice, denied at the Ministerial level, known to every clinician.

## Inside the Wing

The Wing's internal culture is monastic. Long hours, internal jargon, a code of bearing. Job titles citizens know:

- **Memory Technicians** ("Threaders") — perform the procedure. Recruited from medical school, often from Lowmaren and Cant scholarship cohorts. The work is physically tolerable and psychologically corrosive (see biotech-system.md, mirror-recall). Threaders die younger. They are honored at the Sequence each year by name.
- **Inquisitors** — the field-investigations arm. Truth Lattice-fitted. Their remit is suspected memory crimes (Unauthorized Restoration, stitch possession, the small cottage industry of forgery in carry-notes). Recruited young, almost always orphans of the program or wards of the State. Trained from age eleven. Sael is the youngest field Inquisitor on record. He thinks of his colleagues as siblings; he does not have friends.
- **Field Recovery** — uniformed, blue-grey. Pickups, transports, the actual physical extraction of suspects. Recruited from the city's veteran population. Brutality is rare but tolerated.
- **Cartographers of Recall** — the cartridge fabricators, mid-level, civilian-coded. They build the day-cartridges from biometric data. They never meet the citizens whose days they extract.
- **The Audit** — internal affairs. Reviews the Reviewers. Floor Minus Two of the Wing. Wears no insignia. Other Wing staff do not initiate conversation with the Audit.

A person becomes an Inquisitor not by choosing — by being chosen at twelve, after a battery of cognitive and ethical tests run on every State ward. The screening identifies the bearings the Wing wants: high pattern recognition, low affective reactivity, conservative moral intuitions, and (this is documented internally but not in citizen-facing materials) a stable response to the Truth Lattice fitting surgery. About one in two hundred children pass. Sael passed at the top of his cohort.

A person becomes a Threader by choosing — usually from below, the Cant or Lowmaren kids whose path to medical credentials runs through Wing scholarship. They sign a twelve-year service bond. They know what they're signing.

## Counterculture

The **Rememberers** are not an organization. They are a tendency — small cells of three to seven, mostly Marrow-based, mostly people who have lost someone specific and who refuse the Concord's framing. They use stitch. They keep illegal date-journals on rice paper that dissolves in water. They photograph everything obsessively (in this they are indistinguishable from compliant citizens — the wall-density rule is a universal). What marks them is the language: they say *cut*, not *kindness*. They call the Wing the Wing without softening it. They use first names of people the State has declared net-harmful to remember.

To a mainstream citizen, a Rememberer looks like a person who is making themselves needlessly unhappy. The Concord's framing is that they are mourning something the city has agreed to let go of. The framing is effective because it is partly true. Some Rememberers are, in fact, making themselves unhappy. Some have something real to remember. The book is the difference.

## Generational difference

- **Pre-Wing adults (over 55):** remember the decade before the Wing. Remember whatever the founding event was (see history.md). Do not talk about it; the prohibition is internal as much as external. They are why the city's grandparents are quiet.
- **Founding generation (35-55):** the first generation to undergo annual Review from young adulthood. They built the etiquette. They are the most invested in the framing because they remember choosing it.
- **Maren's generation (under 25):** never knew otherwise. Reviewed since fifteen. Two or three days gone per person at this age, no comparison-state to grieve. To them, the Wing is the weather. They are also, statistically, the cohort with the highest stitch uptake. They have nothing to compare a clear year to, so a clear year feels like something owed.
