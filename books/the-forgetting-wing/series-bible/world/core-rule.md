# Core Rule

Use for: any scene set in Halvern-Estre. Load this first. Every other world choice descends from this single rule.

## The rule

In Halvern-Estre, the State legally and physically removes one full day of memory from every citizen, every year, and the citizen does not get to choose which day.

## What this means at street level

The procedure is called Annual Consolidation Review (ACR, "the Review," "your day"). It is administered by the Ministry of Public Cognition, whose operational arm — the Anterograde Welfare Division — citizens know by its building nickname, the Forgetting Wing. Citizens do not choose their day. They do not, in the standard tier, learn what day was taken. They learn only that a day was taken, somewhere in the previous twelve months, and that the State judged it net-harmful for them to keep. Most citizens never ask which one. Asking is not illegal. Asking is what people who are about to be flagged tend to do.

The day after your Review, you wake clear-headed in a recovery bay smelling of warm laundry and the resin they use to seal the scalp port. The Ministry hands you a pamphlet that says, in eleven languages, that you are loved, that the city is grateful, and that the body you are returning to has been treated and fed. There will be a small tender place behind your left ear for about a week. Your calendar will show a gentle gap: the day's appointments rolled forward, the day's messages archived to a folder you cannot open. You feel, the literature says, slightly lighter. Most people do. The Ministry's own surveys put satisfaction at ninety-one percent.

What you lose is not announced and not mourned. People who were with you that day may or may not have lost it too; if they did, the gap is symmetrical and unbothered. If they didn't — if your sister kept the day and you didn't — the etiquette is that she does not tell you. To tell someone what was taken is a Class C offense (Unauthorized Restoration). Citizens learn this etiquette as children. By the time you have your first Review at fifteen, you already know not to ask your aunt what you did at the picnic last August. The not-asking is the politeness that holds the city together.

## What is normalized

- The scalp port. Most adults have one, a coin-sized matte disc seated behind the left ear, exposed by short hair on the State-employed and hidden by long hair on private citizens. It is the city's most common piece of jewelry.
- The pamphlet. Every household has a stack of them. Children fold them into boats.
- The hum. The Wing's compressors run cold through a district-wide chiller system; certain blocks vibrate at a frequency people stop noticing by their fourth year of residence and visitors find unbearable.
- Memorial photography. Because anyone could lose a day of any year, families photograph everything: weddings, ordinary breakfasts, the dog asleep. Walls are dense with images. A house with bare walls is read as either very poor or very dangerous.
- Date journaling, the legal kind. You are allowed to write down what happened today; you are not allowed to write down what someone else told you happened on a day that was taken from you. The line is fuzzy, and is policed.

## What is lost

- The shape of the year. People do not refer to "last August" with confidence. They refer to "before my last Review" and "after my last Review." Time, for most, runs in annual rings with a hole in each.
- Apology. You cannot truly apologize for a thing you do not remember doing. You also cannot be apologized to. Whole categories of grievance fall through the floor of the calendar.
- Grief, in some cases. A widower may not remember the day his wife died. The State considers this a kindness. The widower, often, does too.
- The texture of private decision. Citizens have learned not to invest deeply in any single day. The day is provisional. The week is what counts.

## What the rule generates that the book uses

- A protagonist who is remembering what she shouldn't can never tell anyone what she remembers without committing a crime.
- An antagonist whose job is to keep this etiquette enforced — and who has been Reviewed himself, repeatedly, and has agreed to it.
- A city where the most ordinary social interaction (asking a friend how their weekend was) is freighted with the possibility of compliance failure.
- An institution that decides, every day, what a million people will not be allowed to know about themselves. The book is the story of one citizen who can audit that institution from the inside out, because the audit is happening in her own head.
