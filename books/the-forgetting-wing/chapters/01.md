# 01

The knife was in her hand before she knew her hand was a hand.

That was the first thing. The cold of the handle through the seam where the rivet sat against her thumb. The way her fingers were already shaped around it the way they shaped around it most evenings, three knuckles folded, the index laid along the spine. A paring knife. A kitchen blade. Hers — or one exactly like hers, the small clean Cant-issue blade with the worn wooden scale and the chip at the heel — and her hand had been holding it long enough that her palm had warmed the steel.

She did not move.

She had learned not to move, in the year after — in the year. She kept her eyes where they had opened to, which was a long pale brick wall an arm's length from her face, and she let the rest come in the order it wanted to come.

Wet, under her hip. Not blood, she thought. Then: how would you know. Then: cold the wrong way for blood, cold like the alley stones in fourth-watch, cold like rain that had been there a while and given up. The smell was indigo and bread.

Indigo.

Sennet's. Sennet's at the dye-works end, the dead end, the brick.

She knew where she was before she had named the alley. *Anyone would have known.* The dye-works did the wall to her cheek and the bread-wind did the rest, lifting up from Lowmaren the way it always did in the small hours when the manufactories vented warm, and there was only one alley in Cant that smelled like both. Sennet's at the closed end. Sennet's where the lamp at the mouth did not reach.

She moved her thumb. The knife came with it.

There was someone behind her.

She did not turn. She listened for breath and did not find breath, which meant the someone was either very still or — the second thought came in slow and unwilling, the way the bad thoughts came — not the kind of someone who breathed. She held the knife. She catalogued.

What she had done. What she might have done. What in the missing days she could have done with this exact blade in this exact hand if someone — if she — had been her own bad luck.

She had had her Review eleven days ago.

She had walked herself home from the Wing on the third afternoon after, the way you did, the recovery sleeve on her left wrist and the pamphlet in her coat pocket folded into a small bird because the recovery bay nurse always folded them into small birds while she waited and Maren had taken one of the birds without asking. She had eaten the supper Lo cooked. She had told Lo it had gone normally. Normal: nine hours; soft music; the laundry smell. Coming up at dawn with the small tender place behind her ear and a feeling she could not name and the calendar showing a gap on the eighth of last month. She had not asked which day.

She had not asked.

But there were three days now, in her, that were not the eighth of last month. They had been arriving since the second morning, at angles, with no roof on them. She had thought she could outwait them. She had thought.

Maren turned her head, by degrees.

The wall slid past her cheek. A streak of old indigo took the corner of her eye. She kept the knife where it was — close to her belly, point down — and she rolled her shoulder away from the brick the way her aunt had shown her you rolled away from a thing at the clinic when you wanted to look at it without it knowing you were looking. Slow as a thaw.

He was nearer than she had thought. Not behind. Beside her. At her feet — at her left ankle, his face turned partway up toward her, his cheek against the stones.

A man. Not old. Not young the way she was young.

The first thing she catalogued was his coat. A grey wool coat with a darker grey collar, plain, the kind half the city wore. The buttons were horn. The third button from the top was missing and the thread was raw, freshly broken, and the white shirt under it showed a small clean dark place she could not at first read as anything but a stain — and then it sat up in her mind as what it was, which was a wound, a place where the cloth had gone in instead of staying out. Below the wound there was a fan of darker grey on the wool. The fan did not move.

She did not look at his face yet.

She looked at the rest. His left hand was open. His right hand was tucked under his thigh, palm up, the way her aunt slept on the bad nights. He was lying on his side. The line his body made with the brick was a familiar line; it was the line a person made when they had lain down because the lying down was the only thing left to do.

There was frost on his sleeve.

She held that. Frost on his sleeve, white at the cuff, thicker where the wool was thickest. The cold had been on him long enough to draw the wet up out of the cloth and set it. She did not know in hours what that meant. She knew it was not minutes. It was not minutes. The cold had had time.

She looked at his face.

She had not — she had never. He had a thin face and a short beard not quite grown out and a small mark by his right ear that might have been a birthmark or a poppy seed or the heel of someone's thumb. His eyes were not closed. They were not open either. They were the way eyes went when the muscle that held the lid let go.

She did not know him.

She tested it twice, the way Lo had taught her to test a port for fissure — once for the obvious and once for the thing you were afraid of. She did not know him on first look. She did not know him on second look. She had never bought from his stall, she had never sat behind him on a tram, she had never been near him at the Memorial. *Anyone would have remembered him.* Then the second thought: *I would not know if I had remembered him, because if I had remembered him from any day before the eighth of last month it would have been the eighth of last month.* The Wing's joke. The city's joke. Anyone you did not remember could be a stranger or could be the friend you took your supper with for ten years.

She made herself put the knife down.

It took two tries. Her fingers had become the knife and the knife had become her fingers and the lay of them on the brick was difficult to relinquish. She set it on the stones beside her right knee, point away from her, the way you set down a thing you might need to lift again. She wiped her palm on her coat. The palm came away clean. She wiped it again anyway.

She looked at her own hand.

No blood. Not on the palm, not in the lines, not under the nails. The chemical burn she had taken at fourteen helping Lo at the clinic was on the back of her wrist where it always was, a small pink island of skin she had stopped noticing at fifteen and noticed now. Her cuff was dark, but the dark was the alley-wet she had been lying in. She lifted the cuff to her nose. It smelled of stone and dye. Nothing copper. Nothing iron.

The relief that wanted to come — she did not let it come. There were a thousand ways to put a knife in a man and walk away with clean hands. She knew six of them from her aunt's clinic stories and could guess at the rest. She knew, also, that her hands had been doing things for three days she could not account for and that the count of her honest hours since her Review was somewhere shorter than her count of dishonest ones. *Honest* was the wrong word. Honest was not what she meant.

She held the cuff a second longer than she meant to.

bread-smell on the wind, the warm under-bread smell from Lowmaren, a doorway with a metal lip, a hand near her shoulder not touching her, the lip of the doorway high enough she had to step up

She came back to the alley.

That had not been a memory. That had been the room next door to a memory; she had pressed against the wall of it and the wall had given. She did not know whose hand. She did not know what doorway. The bread-smell was Lowmaren and Lowmaren was a fifteen-minute walk and she had no business in Lowmaren and she had been in Lowmaren, some hour, some day, with a hand near her shoulder.

She made herself stand.

It was harder than standing should have been. Her left leg had gone half-numb under her and her right knee took her weight badly and the brick wall behind her did the rest. She stood with her shoulder against the brick and looked down at the man.

He was a man. He had been someone before he had been a man on the stones. He had bought the horn buttons somewhere. He had grown the short beard out for someone or for no one. His left hand was open the way a hand was open when it had been holding something and the something had been taken.

She did not know him.

She had to leave. She had to — she had to choose, which was different from leaving, but the part she had to do first was leave. The lamp at the mouth of Sennet's was four meters away and it stood at the only place a camera covered. The dead end did not have a camera. It had not had a camera for three years; Lo had said so once at the table with the bitterness she used for State things, and Maren had filed it without filing it. Someone had filed it. Someone had filed it and used it.

The thought wanted to go further. She did not let it go further.

She bent for the knife.

Her hand stopped above it. She thought about leaving it. She thought about leaving it on the stones beside him, which was where it wanted to be left, which was where any sensible girl would leave a knife that was not hers and a man who was not hers. She thought about Lo's drawer. She thought about Lo's drawer second and she thought about it for longer than she should have. The drawer with the small clean Cant-issue blades, three of them, one of them with the chip at the heel. The chip at the heel was — was a thing she could not see from where she stood and would not see until she got home. The chip at the heel could not be in her drawer and in this alley at once. It could not. She did not yet need to know which.

She picked up the knife.

She wrapped it inside the cuff of her coat, blade pointed back at her own forearm where if it slipped it would cut her and not the cloth, and she walked toward the mouth of the alley. She walked the way she had learned to walk when she did not want to be heard — heel-first, soft, weight forward. She passed the lamp without looking at it. She did not look back at him. She did not let herself look back at him because she was not sure she would not say something, and the only thing she had to say was *I am sorry,* and she did not know yet what she would be sorry for.

The mouth of Sennet's gave onto Helvet Stair two blocks down from her own door. The street was empty. The sodium light came down yellow on the stones and the stones were wet in the way Cant stones were always wet between fourth-watch and dawn. Above the rooflines the sky had gone the colour the sky went a half-hour before any actual light arrived.

She climbed.

Helvet Stair was four flights from Sennet's level to her landing and another two to the roof. The treads were stone with iron-edged risers and the smell was cabbage and resin all the way up — the cabbage from the Yedrins on the first floor and the resin from Lo's hands and the hundred clinic hands that had come and gone up this stair for thirty years. Maren did the first flight without thinking. She did the second flight without thinking. On the third flight she had to stop.

It was not her body. Her body would have done the four flights. It was something in her chest that had not adjusted to the air being air and not the alley.

She sat on a tread.

The knife inside her cuff pressed against her forearm where she had set it. She did not unwrap it. She put her forehead against her knee and breathed three times, the way Lo had taught her — in for four, hold for two, out for six. The fourth breath she did not count. She thought: *what did I do, in those three days I should not have done.* She thought: *who did I let near me.* She thought: *what did I let near him.* The catalog of selves she had to suspect first.

She did not yet catalog who had been near her.

The window above her on the landing was lit. That was the third-floor window. That was the Cant grandmother whose name Maren had been told twice and lost twice and would not ask a third time — the one who put out a saucer of milk on her sill for a cat that had died last spring. She had been singing earlier, then; that was — that had been the start of waking, which Maren only now had room to admit, the voice coming down from above the alley into the alley. The voice was still going.

It was thin, the voice. Older. Not making a performance.

Maren got up.

She climbed past the second-floor landing. She climbed past the third-floor landing and there the grandmother was, propped in her doorway in a wool wrap, holding a teacup with both hands, looking out at the stair with the soft unfocused look of someone who was singing to her own kitchen. She was singing the rhyme.

*One little hand to take the day*

The grandmother sang it the way she had sung it for fifty years probably, the way Maren's mother had sung it standing at the window with a hand on Maren's shoulder when Maren was four and three and two, the rhyme that every Cant child knew without having been taught it and that the teachers shushed and the mothers sang anyway.

*One little hand to pass it on*

Maren stopped on the landing.

The grandmother did not look at her. The grandmother was inside the song. Her teacup steamed; her port behind her left ear was old, the resin gone yellow with thirty years of clinic dust.

*One little day for the city's sake*

The line went into Maren in a place the line had never gone before.

She had been singing it last week. She had been singing it under her breath last week walking to the tram with a paper of beans for Lo and she had been singing it the same way she had been singing it her whole life, which was the way you sang a song you had been singing your whole life, which was not at all. Now the third line had a hand and a day and a city in it and the hand had been in her hand an hour ago and the day had been her own day, somewhere, some eighth, and the city, and the city —

*Wake up clean, wake up gone.*

The grandmother sang the last line into her teacup. She did not see Maren on the landing. She had a small smile of the kind that did not mean anything by itself.

Maren climbed the last flight.

She did it in seven steps because the seventh tread she did not put her weight on; the seventh tread had a hairline she had known about since she was eight. Stepping over it was the kind of remembering nobody minded.

At the fourth-floor landing her door was the second on the left. She did not look for her key. The flat was not locked. Lo did not lock the flat at any hour she was home, and Lo was home at this hour, because Lo had not gone in to the clinic before dawn since the year Maren was twelve.

She put her hand on the doorknob.

She made herself look at her hand on the doorknob. Clean. Bony. The chemical burn at the wrist. No tremor that she could see and a tremor she could feel along the inside of her forearm in the bone. She breathed in for four. She held for two. She opened the door.

The flat smelled of tea.

Lo was at the stove in her grey work clothes, sleeves rolled to mid-forearm, hair in the clip Maren had bought her at twelve. The kettle had just come off; the steam was going up against the underside of the cabinet. The kitchen was three steps from the door because the flat was four small rooms in a square, and Lo did not turn around at the door opening, because Lo never turned around at the door opening; she knew what Maren's step sounded like the way Maren knew the chip on the heel of the third paring knife.

"Mar," Lo said. "I was about to."

She turned. Her face did the thing it did at the door, the half-second of port-tech reading — gait, shoulder, the angle of the chin — and then she put it away the way she always put it away, and she gave Maren the smile she gave Maren when Maren had come in.

The kitchen knives were in the wooden block by the stove. There were three. Two of the Cant-issue paring blades with worn wooden scales. The third place in the block was empty.

Maren did not look at the block twice.

She did not need to. She had set that block on that counter four nights a week for nine years. She had reached for that block in the dark to peel a potato when Lo was working late and the bulb had gone in the ceiling. The empty slot in the block was the size of the knife wrapped against her forearm under her cuff and she did not need to think about the size of the slot or the size of the knife or the chip at the heel.

Lo opened the cupboard for a cup.

"You're up early," Lo said. "Or you're up late. I never can tell with you."

"Up early."

"Hm." Lo set the cup down. She did not pour yet. "Have you eaten?"

Maren stood inside the door. The knife inside the cuff of her coat pressed where it pressed, and her own kitchen knives stood two-of-three in the block, and the rhyme was still in her chest the way bread-smell was still in her nose, and Lo had asked her the question Lo had asked her every morning of every day of nine years, and Maren had answered the question every morning of every day of nine years with the same answer, which was the answer Lo wanted and which was sometimes true and sometimes not. She set her left hand on the doorframe behind her. She thought: *the bare wall above my cot. The lamp on the shelf above the cot. The pine board behind the lamp. The board is still loose. It has been loose for years. Nothing has changed.* She thought: *I will go to my room. I will change my coat. I will not touch the board.* She thought: *not yet.*

"I had bread on the way," Maren said. "At the corner."

Lo poured the tea.
