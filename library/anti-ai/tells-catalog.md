# AI Tells — Catalog

Use for: every chapter draft. The voice-scrubber loads this and matches against the text. Anything caught here gets rewritten before the chapter is accepted.

These are the patterns that mark prose as AI-generated. They're not bad writing in isolation — they're bad because language models default to them, and readers have learned to feel the seams.

---

## Lexical tells (specific words and phrases)

**Banned outright** — these almost never appear in good fiction and should be cut on sight:

- *delve, delved, delving* — replace with concrete verb (search, dig, work through)
- *navigate, navigating* — only literal navigation; never "navigated the complexity"
- *tapestry, intricate tapestry, rich tapestry* — almost always a metaphor; cut and replace
- *testament to* — replace with the actual thing it's a testament to
- *in the realm of, in the world of* — cut entirely, restate concretely
- *underscores, underscoring* — replace (made clear, proved, drove home)
- *grapple, grappling with* — for non-physical contexts; replace with specific verb
- *resonate, resonated* — almost always vague emotional gesturing
- *myriad, plethora* — replace with "many" or be specific
- *kaleidoscope, kaleidoscopic* — banned as metaphor
- *symphony of, dance of, ballet of* — banned as metaphors
- *intricate, intricacy, intricacies* — replace with the specific complication
- *moreover, furthermore, additionally* — fiction rarely needs these; use *and*, a new sentence, or nothing
- *it's worth noting that, it bears mentioning, it's important to remember* — these are essay phrases; cut
- *whisper, whispered* — heavily overused for non-literal whispers ("the wind whispered")
- *the air was thick with* — banned
- *a stark reminder of* — banned
- *the weight of* — banned as cliché ("the weight of his decision," "the weight of history")

**Banned constructions:**

- *not just X but Y* — overused contrast structure. Pick one.
- *not only X but also Y* — same.
- *isn't just X, it's Y* — same.
- *more than just X* — same.
- *X, yet Y* — used as a paradox-flavor cliché; check if "but" or a new sentence is better.
- *in a world where...* — banned as opening or transition.
- *little did [character] know* — banned as cheap foreshadowing.
- *unbeknownst to [character]* — banned.
- *with a [adjective] [body part]* — overused ("with a heavy heart," "with bated breath," "with a knowing smile")
- *a [adjective] silence* — usually filler ("a pregnant silence," "a deafening silence")
- *[character] couldn't help but [verb]* — replace with the action directly

## Structural tells (paragraph and chapter habits)

- **Tricolons everywhere.** Three-part lists are seductive: "the cold, the dark, and the silence." Fine once a chapter. Three per page is an AI tell. Vary to 2 and 4.
- **Em-dash overdose.** One per page is craft. Three per paragraph is a tell.
- **Semicolon overdose.** Same.
- **The contrast-pivot pattern.** "She had thought she was alone. She was not." Used sparingly = punch. Used every scene = formula.
- **Every paragraph ending on a clinching sentence.** Real prose has paragraphs that end mid-thought, on a beat, or on a fragment. AI prose ends every paragraph like it's a slide.
- **Every chapter ending on a thematic statement.** Cut the last sentence of nearly every AI chapter ending and the chapter is stronger.
- **Symmetric sentence trios.** Three sentences of similar length in a row, each making a similar move. Break the pattern.
- **The "and then she realized" beat at the climax of every scene.** Realizations should be earned, often unspoken, and not labeled.

## Voice tells (narrator-as-AI)

- **Hedging.** "It seemed almost as if..." Real narrators commit.
- **Therapeutic vocabulary.** A medieval blacksmith does not "process her trauma" or "set healthy boundaries." Match the diction to the world.
- **Empathetic universalism.** "Everyone, deep down, wants to be seen." Generic wisdom-statements; cut them all.
- **The summary aside.** "It was, in many ways, the kind of moment that defines a life." Cut.
- **Over-explained emotion.** "She felt a complex mix of grief and relief, the two emotions twining together inside her." Show one, trust the reader.
- **Filtering through perception verbs.** "She saw the door open. She heard footsteps. She felt afraid." Replace with the events directly.
- **Internal monologue as TED talk.** Characters thinking in essay structure with topic sentences and transitions. Real thought is fragmentary, associative, recursive.

## Description tells

- **Light doing too much work.** "Golden light filtered through..." "Pale light spilled across..." If light shows up in the first sentence of more than 1 in 5 scenes, scrub.
- **Eye-color paragraphs.** Long descriptions of eyes — color, depth, what they held. Reader does not need it.
- **Weather as mood barometer.** Rain when sad, sun when hopeful. Use weather, but not as a one-to-one emotional cue.
- **Smell name-checks.** "The scent of [specific thing]" in every setting. Use smell rarely and earn it.
- **Three-adjective stacks.** "Cold, gray, lifeless eyes." Pick the one that does work.

## Dialogue tells

- **Everyone monologues.** Characters speaking in complete, well-organized paragraphs. Real speech is interrupted, contradicts itself, trails off.
- **The wisdom dispenser.** A minor character drops a perfectly themed aphorism. Cut or rewrite as offhand.
- **The recap conversation.** Characters telling each other things they both already know. Replace with action, ellipsis, or a different scene.
- **Beat-tag-beat-tag rhythm.** Every line has an action beat and a tag. Vary: stretches of pure dialogue, then a beat that hits.
- **"He said with a [emotion]."** Cut the emotion or replace with action.

## The grand pattern

If any single page contains **three or more of these tells**, the page reads as AI. The voice-scrubber's job is to catch them. The drafter's job is to not write them in the first place — keep this catalog in working memory while drafting.
