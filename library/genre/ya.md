# Young Adult (YA)

**Use for:** novels with a teen protagonist (typically 14-18) confronting a first-time, identity-shaping experience, written for a teen readership (with significant crossover adult audience).

## Word-count band
- Low: 50k
- Standard: 65k-85k
- High: 100k+ (YA fantasy/sci-fi)
- Contemporary YA skews shorter; speculative YA skews longer.

## Reader contract
- Protagonist is a teen, and the story is told from inside teen experience — not adult nostalgia about it.
- Stakes are felt at teen scale: identity, autonomy, first love, place in the world, family rupture — even when world stakes are larger.
- Agency: teen protagonists drive their own plot; adults do not solve it.
- Emotional honesty about hard things (sex, mental health, violence, grief) without preachiness.
- An ending that opens a door, even if bittersweet. Pure despair is rare; earned hope is the convention.

## Mandatory beats / conventions
- Voice on page one: a teen narrator the reader trusts and likes (or finds compelling).
- Inciting event that disrupts the protagonist's known world by chapter 2.
- A "what I want" goal in tension with "what I need" arc; both visible by act 1's end.
- A friendship or chosen-family thread parallel to the main plot.
- Romance subplot common but not mandatory — when present, it follows romance-adjacent beats.
- Identity question is the engine: who am I, who do I want to be, what world will I accept.
- Climax involves a choice that costs something the protagonist values.
- Resolution names the change — not always solving the external problem, but acknowledging the internal shift.

## Pacing notes
- Chapters 2k-4k; brisk by literary standards.
- Hooks at chapter ends; immediate scene grounding at chapter starts.
- One major plot turn per ~50-70 pages.
- Avoid long timeline ellipses early — teen-time is dense.

## Voice / tone defaults
- First person, present tense is the modern default (especially contemporary); past tense and third limited fully acceptable, especially in fantasy.
- Voice carries the book: specific, opinionated, funny-when-it-can-be, vulnerable.
- Current idiom risks dating; lean on emotional specificity rather than slang.
- Adults are present, complicated, sometimes wrong; rarely all-knowing.

## Common failure modes
- Adult-narrator-in-teen-costume: vocabulary, syntax, and concerns that don't belong to a 16-year-old.
- Issue-driven didacticism: the book as lecture.
- Adults solve the climax; protagonist is rescued rather than acting.
- Insta-love romance with no friction.
- Stakes pitched too low (mean-girl drama without character cost) or too cosmic without the personal anchor.
- Sanitizing teen interiority — readers smell it.

## Touchstones
- Angie Thomas, *The Hate U Give*
- Leigh Bardugo, *Six of Crows*
- Becky Albertalli, *Simon vs. the Homo Sapiens Agenda*
- Adam Silvera, *They Both Die at the End*
- Holly Jackson, *A Good Girl's Guide to Murder*
