# Epic Fantasy

**Use for:** secondary-world (or deeply altered) settings with magic/non-human peoples, stakes that scale beyond the personal, and multi-POV arcs converging on a world-shaping conflict.

## Word-count band
- Low: 110k
- Standard: 150k-200k
- High: 250k+ (multi-volume entries routinely hit 300k-450k)
- Trilogy/series is the default unit. Stand-alone epic fantasy is rare and must announce itself.

## Reader contract
- A coherent, internally-consistent world with rules that hold under pressure (magic, politics, geography, economy).
- Scope: the stakes ultimately touch nations / peoples / the world, not just one family.
- Wonder: at least one set-piece of awe per act (a place, a working of magic, a creature, a revelation).
- A finished arc, even within a series: the volume must close on a meaningful turn, not a mid-sentence cut.
- Earned consequences: deaths, costs, and changes that stick.

## Mandatory beats / conventions
- Hook chapter that puts character + stake + flavor of the world on the page within the first scene.
- A clear inciting disruption to a known order (a death, a prophecy cracking, an incursion, a crowning).
- Quest, war, or succession structure as the spine; foreshadowed throughout.
- Magic system introduced through use before exposition; rules made visible by costs and limits (hard) or by mood and mystery (soft) — pick a lane and signal it early.
- Ensemble cast: 3-6 POVs typical; each POV must change something the others cannot.
- Mid-book "gates of no return" reversal that reframes the goal (the map was wrong, the mentor lied, the prophecy means something else).
- Dark night / fellowship fracture before final convergence.
- Climactic convergence: separated POVs touch the same event from different angles.
- Magic costs paid on-page. Foreshadow major reveals.
- World-building delivered via friction, not lecture: characters bump into the world.

## Subgenre notes
- **High / heroic / noblebright:** moral clarity, mythic register, prophecy and chosen ones welcome. Hope is load-bearing. (Tolkien, Sanderson, Jordan.)
- **Sword & sorcery:** smaller scope, picaresque, episodic, mercenary or roguish protagonists, visceral combat, lush prose, fast pulp pacing (60k-90k is fine). (Howard, Leiber, Wagner.)
- **Grimdark:** moral ambiguity, brutal cause-and-effect, unreliable heroism, institutional rot, war's real cost; payoff is catharsis not triumph. Avoid edginess without consequence. (Abercrombie, Martin, Lawrence.)
- **Progression / cultivation:** explicit power tiers, training arcs, measurable advancement, a reader contract for visible numerical/qualitative growth per arc; xianxia influence is welcome. (Wight, He Who Fights With Monsters, Sanderson as soft progression.)

## Pacing notes
- Chapters 3k-6k average; epic prologues OK if they pay off by chapter 3.
- Rotate POVs to manufacture cliffhangers; never strand a POV for >150 pages without consequence.
- One major set-piece (battle, magical confrontation, revelation) per ~80-120 pages.
- Slow-burn first act tolerated (worldbuilding tax) but anchor with character stakes by chapter 2-3.
- Travel scenes earn their length only by changing character or revealing world.

## Voice / tone defaults
- Third-person limited, past tense, rotating POV is the modern default; omniscient acceptable for mythic registers and older-feeling works.
- Register slightly elevated; avoid 21st-century idiom unless deliberately subverting.
- Sensory worldbuilding woven into action; resist info-dump prologues unless mythic framing earns them.
- Names, oaths, songs, and curses do heavy worldbuilding work.

## Common failure modes
- Worldbuilding without stakes; tourism instead of story.
- Infodump prologues; map-and-glossary as a substitute for clarity.
- Magic that solves problems by surprise (no foreshadowed cost = deus ex magica).
- Cast bloat: POVs with no plot function; sprawl without convergence.
- Prophecy delivered without cost or ambiguity.
- Stakes inflation (each book = bigger evil) without character cost.
- Middle-volume sag: travel + politics with no turn.
- Pastiche: Tolkien/D&D furniture used as shorthand instead of choice.
- Grimdark nihilism with nothing thematically load-bearing under the cruelty.

- Joe Abercrombie, *The Blade Itself*
- N.K. Jemisin, *The Fifth Season*
- R.F. Kuang, *The Poppy War*
