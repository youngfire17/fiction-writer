# Science Fiction

**Use for:** stories whose central conflict, setting, or theme depends on a speculative premise (tech, biology, physics, society) the reader could in principle reason about.

## Word-count band
- Low: 80k
- Standard: 100k-130k
- High: 150k-180k+ (space opera, hard-SF epics)

## Reader contract
- One or more clearly-stated "novum" — the new thing — that drives plot.
- Premise is internally consistent; the plot does not cheat its own rules.
- Extrapolation, not magic: causation flows from the premise.
- The world feels like it exists offstage — economics, politics, daily life implied, not just the cool tech.
- The idea earns its keep: the story could not be told without it.
- Sense of wonder OR cognitive unsettlement at least once per act.

## Mandatory beats / conventions
- Premise on the page early (chapter 1-3); reader knows what kind of SF this is by end of chapter 1 (hard, soft, space opera, near-future, post-human).
- A first complication that demonstrates how the novum reshapes choices.
- A "second-order" implication scene: not just "we have X," but "because we have X, society/people do Y."
- Escalation: implications widen beyond the protagonist (society, ecology, species, time).
- Confrontation with the limit of the premise — its cost, its failure mode, its blowback.
- A grounded human stake braided through the speculative one.
- Climax must hinge on the speculative element; swap it for a wrench and if the story still works, it isn't SF.
- Ending that resolves the human arc and takes a position on the idea; the world is acknowledged as changed.

## Subgenre notes
- **Hard SF:** physics / biology / CS rigor, footnotable; minimize handwaves. (Watts, Egan, Robinson.)
- **Space opera:** scale and color over rigor; factions, fleets, FTL allowed; character-driven. (Leckie, Banks, Corey.)
- **Cyberpunk / biopunk:** style-forward, corporate dystopia, embodied / identity tech, noir register. (Gibson, Kadrey, Newitz.)
- **Social / climate / sociological SF:** the experiment is cultural or systemic; near-future extrapolation. (Le Guin, Butler, Robinson.)

## Pacing notes
- Chapters 3k-5k; near-future thrillers run shorter, space opera longer.
- Tech reveals function as turn beats — pace them like clues.
- Exposition braided into action; avoid the "As you know, Bob" dump.
- A revelation or escalation every 30-50 pages.
- One "wonder" set-piece per act.

## Voice / tone defaults
- Third limited or first; multi-POV in space opera; present tense common in near-future.
- Specificity wins: branded objects, jargon used naturally, no italics-on-every-coined-word, no glossary in place of context.
- Tone ranges from clinical-cool (hard SF) to operatic (space opera) to noir (cyberpunk).
- Use puzzlement, argument, or failure to convey rules; let smart characters be smart.

## Common failure modes
- Idea story with no character, or treatise disguised as a novel.
- Premise as set dressing — story works unchanged on Earth-now.
- Tech that conveniently appears to solve the plot beat (magic-system SF).
- Worldbuilding info-dumps in dialogue or lecture chapters.
- Future inflected only by gadgets — language, politics, sex, work all 2020s.
- Climax decided by force when the novum demanded a thematic answer.
- Cribbing recent science headlines without integrating implications.

## Touchstones
- Martha Wells, *All Systems Red* (Murderbot)
- Ann Leckie, *Ancillary Justice*
- Arkady Martine, *A Memory Called Empire*
- Adrian Tchaikovsky, *Children of Time*
- Ted Chiang, *Stories of Your Life and Others*
