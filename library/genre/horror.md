# Horror

**Use for:** stories whose primary emotional aim is dread, fear, or unease, evoked by an antagonist or condition that violates the natural/moral order.

## Word-count band
- Low: 65k
- Standard: 80k-95k
- High: 110k+ (King, epic horror)
- Novella (25k-40k) is a respected unit in the genre.

## Reader contract
- The book will scare, unnerve, or disturb — at least one sustained sequence of fear.
- The threat is real within the world; the protagonist's danger is not a metaphor only.
- Atmosphere maintained; the dread is not undercut by jokes that don't earn it.
- The protagonist confronts the horror on-page in the climax; pure ambiguity is allowed, pure evasion is not.
- Rules of the threat (even if uncanny) are followable.

## Mandatory beats / conventions
- Normalcy + crack: an opening that establishes the ordinary, then a hairline fracture.
- Foreshock: an early uncanny event, deniable in retrospect.
- Escalation: each encounter with the threat is worse, closer, more personal than the last.
- Isolation: physical, social, or epistemic — the protagonist cannot get help, or is not believed.
- Rules revealed through cost: protagonist learns the threat's nature by losing.
- Mid-book "we have to leave" / "we can't leave" pivot.
- The descent: protagonist enters the threat's domain by choice or necessity.
- Final confrontation: survival, transformation, or destruction — and a final image that lingers.

## Subgenre notes
- **Supernatural / occult:** ghosts, demons, cursed objects; mythology and ritual carry weight.
- **Cosmic / weird:** indifferent vast wrongness; meaning erodes; ambiguity tolerated.
- **Slasher / splatter:** body count, kill set-pieces, survival; pacing tight.
- **Psychological:** the threat may be internal; reality wobbles; unreliable POV.
- **Folk / quiet horror:** landscape, ritual, community as the wrong; slow burn.

## Pacing notes
- Chapters 2.5k-5k; quiet horror tolerates longer, slasher cuts shorter.
- Tempo alternates: dread (slow, sensory) and shock (fast, fragmented).
- Don't show the monster too soon, or too clearly, or too often.
- A scare/escalation beat every 30-50 pages minimum.

## Voice / tone defaults
- First person or close third dominant; present tense common for immediacy.
- Sensory bias: sound, smell, peripheral vision, body.
- Restraint in adjectives; specific concrete wrongness over generic "evil."
- POV characters are fallible witnesses; doubt is a tool.

## Common failure modes
- Tell-not-show fear ("she was terrified" without earned dread on the page).
- Monster overexposure: clear daylight kills the uncanny.
- Protagonists who behave irrationally only to enable scares.
- Rules that flex to whatever the scene needs.
- Tonal whiplash: comedy that punctures the spell without serving it.
- Endings that resolve via off-page rescue or "it was all a dream."

## Touchstones
- Paul Tremblay, *A Head Full of Ghosts*
- Stephen Graham Jones, *The Only Good Indians*
- Shirley Jackson, *The Haunting of Hill House*
- T. Kingfisher, *The Hollow Places*
- Mariana Enriquez, *Our Share of Night*
