# Thriller

**Use for:** plot-forward stories of escalating danger where a protagonist races against time / an antagonist with high, externalized stakes.

## Word-count band
- Low: 70k
- Standard: 85k-100k
- High: 110k-130k

## Reader contract
- Forward momentum: each chapter advances or complicates the threat. If reading slows, the book has failed.
- A visible antagonist (person, system, conspiracy) with intent and capacity.
- A ticking clock — explicit deadline or compounding cost.
- A protagonist with skin in the game — personal cost, not just professional.
- Competence: protagonist is good at something the reader watches them do.
- Twists that re-contextualize without contradicting established facts.
- Payoff: stakes resolve on the page; the threat is faced, not avoided.

## Mandatory beats / conventions
- Cold open: violence, threat, or unmistakable wrongness inside the first 5 pages.
- Inciting threat by chapter 2-3; protagonist locked into the chase against preference.
- Escalation ladder: each act raises stakes — personal -> local -> systemic, or small target -> large target.
- Re-establish the clock regularly; never let the reader forget what's at zero.
- Mid-book reversal: ally is compromised, mission was a lie, antagonist is closer or bigger than thought.
- "All is lost" moment around 75% where the protagonist is stripped of resources or allies.
- Climactic confrontation, physically and morally specific, with the clock at zero.
- Brief denouement: cost paid, scars remain.
- Tagged subgenre beats: legal (the trial), techno (the system goes live), domestic (the home as battlefield), spy (the meet/handoff/extraction), action (the set-piece chase).

## Subgenre notes
- **Domestic / psychological:** internal threat, unreliable narrator, slow-burn dread.
- **Action / spy:** globe-trotting, tradecraft, high body count, set-piece logistics.
- **Legal / political / techno:** institutional antagonist, expertise on display, procedural texture earned.

## Pacing notes
- Short chapters (1.5k-3.5k); many books use sub-chapter scene breaks.
- End-of-chapter hooks are mandatory: question, reveal, threat, hard cut.
- POV cuts between protagonist and antagonist accelerate the second half and build dramatic irony.
- Scene tempo: enter late, leave early; cut transitions.
- Real-time sequences in the final act; compress narrative time as the clock tightens.

## Voice / tone defaults
- Third limited or first; close, present-leaning past tense, or present.
- Lean prose, concrete verbs, minimal adverbs.
- Sensory cues skew toward threat detection: sound, peripheral movement, body responses.
- Dialogue clipped; subtext over speeches; characters want something from every exchange.

## Common failure modes
- Stakes that plateau; danger never escalates.
- Idiot plot: protagonist fails to do the obvious thing for chapters.
- Passive protagonist reacting without driving.
- Coincidence rescues (cavalry, lucky discoveries) instead of earned solutions.
- Villain monologue that gives away the puzzle.
- Twist that contradicts established facts rather than recontextualizing them.
- Romantic subplot that pauses the clock.
- Procedural detail without tension.
- Forgotten ticking clock: set but never enforced.

## Touchstones
- Gillian Flynn, *Gone Girl*
- Lee Child, *Killing Floor*
- Terry Hayes, *I Am Pilgrim*
- Adrian McKinty, *The Chain*
- Blake Crouch, *Recursion*
