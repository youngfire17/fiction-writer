# Romance

**Use for:** stories whose central plot is the emotional development of a romantic relationship, ending in an emotionally satisfying and optimistic resolution.

## Word-count band
- Low: 50k (category / novella territory)
- Standard: 75k-95k
- High: 100k-120k (historical, fantasy romance)

## Reader contract
- Central relationship is THE plot, not a subplot.
- HEA (Happily Ever After) or HFN (Happy For Now). Non-negotiable. Violation = refund.
- Both leads on-page, with interiority for at least one (often both via dual POV).
- Emotional honesty: characters earn the relationship through change.
- Heat level signaled and consistent (closed door, open door, explicit — pick a lane).

## Mandatory beats / conventions
- Meet-cute / meet-fraught: leads encounter each other in first chapter or two, with friction or charge.
- Internal and external conflict, both rooted in character wound — what each lead must learn / overcome to deserve love.
- Forced-proximity or rising-stakes structure that keeps them in each other's orbit.
- First kiss / first intimacy as a structural turn, not filler.
- Midpoint: declaration, vulnerability, or sex as a commitment escalation.
- Dark moment / black moment: the relationship breaks; one or both retreat to their wound.
- Grovel / grand gesture / earned reunion: the wound is addressed, not bypassed.
- HEA or HFN on-page; reader sees the future.
- Tropes are features, not bugs: enemies-to-lovers, fake dating, second chance, marriage of convenience, only-one-bed — signal early, deliver hard.

## Pacing notes
- Chapters 3k-5k; alternating dual POV common, one scene per POV per chapter.
- Beat density: every chapter advances either the relationship or the obstacle to it.
- Sexual/emotional tension is the through-line; long absences from the love interest are deadly.

## Voice / tone defaults
- First person or close third, often dual POV.
- Interiority front: the reader lives in the wanting.
- Voice matches subgenre — historical (period-aware), rom-com (banter-forward), dark romance (intensity), contemporary (texture of real life).
- Sensory specificity around the love object: hands, voice, scent, the look across the room.

## Common failure modes
- Insta-love with no earned shift; or attraction without conflict.
- Third-act breakup driven by a misunderstanding the leads refuse to discuss (idiot plot).
- Love interest as a prize, not a person with their own arc.
- Cheating without on-page reckoning (genre violation in most lines).
- Ambiguous/unhappy ending — that's women's fiction or literary, not romance.
- Mismatched heat level vs. cover/marketing.

## Touchstones
- Emily Henry, *Beach Read*
- Talia Hibbert, *Get a Life, Chloe Brown*
- Casey McQuiston, *Red, White & Royal Blue*
- Tessa Bailey, *It Happened One Summer*
- Lisa Kleypas, *Devil in Winter*
