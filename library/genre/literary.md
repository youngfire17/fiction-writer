# Literary Fiction

**Use for:** character-forward, theme-driven novels whose primary value is the quality of attention — sentence, perception, structure — rather than genre plot machinery.

## Word-count band
- Low: 60k
- Standard: 75k-95k
- High: 110k+ (multi-generational, polyphonic)

## Reader contract
- Sentence-level craft: prose is itself a reason to read.
- Interiority: the reader lives close to consciousness, not above it.
- Specificity of milieu, class, body, work — the world is rendered, not summarized.
- Ambiguity is allowed; closure is optional; meaning is the reader's to assemble.
- The book is about something: at least one thematic question is taken seriously across the whole.

## Mandatory beats / conventions
- An opening that earns trust at the sentence level within the first page.
- A central character (or constellation) with a contradiction, not a goal.
- A precipitating event — but treated as occasion for change of perception, not just plot.
- Time handled deliberately: in-scene, summary, ellipsis, recursion are tools, not defaults.
- A structural turn (perspective shift, time jump, formal break) somewhere mid-book in many contemporary literary novels.
- An ending that resolves at the level of meaning even if plot stays open: image, decision, recognition.

## Pacing notes
- Chapter and scene length are formal choices, not conventions; chapter-less or sectioned books common.
- Tempo can be slow without being inert if perception keeps moving.
- Scenes earn length through density, not duration.
- One major event can carry a chapter if interiority makes it earn the space.

## Voice / tone defaults
- First person and close third are both standard; omniscient is back in serious use.
- Distinct narrator voice expected — syntax, diction, rhythm are character.
- Free indirect style is the workhorse: third person inflected by character idiom.
- Avoid genre tics: cliffhangers, exposition dumps, on-the-nose dialogue, telegraphed reveals.
- Earn metaphor; cut decoration.

## Common failure modes
- Pretty prose around an absent center: no question, no pressure, no person.
- Style as alibi for vagueness; obscurity mistaken for depth.
- MFA-default: middle-class domestic malaise, no specificity, no stakes.
- Theme stated rather than enacted ("she thought about grief" as substitute for grief on the page).
- Plotlessness as virtue: drift without design.
- Ending by epiphany the character has not earned.

## Touchstones
- Marilynne Robinson, *Gilead*
- Rachel Cusk, *Outline*
- Colson Whitehead, *The Underground Railroad*
- Hanya Yanagihara, *A Little Life*
- Yiyun Li, *The Book of Goose*
