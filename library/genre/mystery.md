# Mystery

**Use for:** stories whose central engine is the investigation of a concealed truth — typically a crime — solved by a detective figure (professional or amateur), with the reader playing along.

## Word-count band
- Low: 70k
- Standard: 80k-95k
- High: 100k-110k

## Reader contract
- Fair play: every clue needed to solve is on the page before the solution.
- A puzzle with a definite, satisfying solution — not ambiguity for its own sake.
- A capable, distinctive investigator the reader wants to spend a book with.
- The world of the crime is rendered specifically (village, precinct, profession, milieu).
- A solution that is surprising in fact but inevitable in retrospect.
- Justice of some kind — legal, moral, or ironic — is delivered or pointedly withheld.

## Mandatory beats / conventions
- Body / crime / disappearance on-page or strongly implied by end of chapter 1-2 (cozy may delay slightly).
- Sleuth engaged with stakes by chapter 3.
- Suspect pool established in act 1: 4-7 viable candidates with motive, means, opportunity, and a secret.
- Clues planted fair-play: each major clue introduced before its use, mixed with red herrings that are legitimately misleading, not lies.
- Investigator's method visible — interrogations, forensics, legwork, deduction.
- Midpoint shift: second body / second incident, major reveal, or the investigator becomes a target.
- A false solution the sleuth (and reader) accept and then must dismantle.
- Confrontation / accusation scene OR procedural takedown where the solution is laid out; both "how" and "why" answered.
- Denouement that addresses moral cost on the sleuth and the community.

## Subgenre notes
- **Cozy:** amateur sleuth, small community, off-page violence, recurring cast, warm wit.
- **Police procedural:** team dynamics, jurisdictional friction, forensic detail, paperwork as texture.
- **Noir / hardboiled:** first-person PI, urban rot, moral grey, style-forward terse prose, society itself implicated.
- **Whodunit / puzzle:** closed circle, classical clueing, gathered-suspects reveal.
- **Domestic / psychological:** unreliable narration, the crime may be ambiguous, suspicion inside the home.

## Pacing notes
- Chapters 2.5k-4.5k; investigator-POV chapters can be longer than suspect or witness intercuts.
- Investigation rhythm: interview -> clue -> theory -> complication. Avoid long stretches without new information.
- Vary scene types: interrogation, surveillance, lab / research, social, action.
- Keep at least one open question per scene; never two clues in a row without consequence.

## Voice / tone defaults
- First person (PI, amateur) or close third (procedural) dominant.
- Observational specificity; the sleuth notices what the reader should also have noticed.
- Voice carries cozy (warm, witty), noir (terse, fatalistic), or procedural (technical, controlled).
- Dialogue does heavy lifting; suspects sound distinct; witnesses lie, deflect, withhold.
- The narrator may withhold but must not lie.

## Common failure modes
- Hidden-information cheat: the solution depends on something the reader couldn't know.
- Twin / amnesia / "it was the cat" type unearned reveals.
- Solution by confession from a character the reader had no reason to suspect.
- Detective passive; case solves itself through coincidence.
- Suspect pool too thin (one obvious, three filler).
- Red herrings without internal logic; red herrings that are actually lies, not misdirection.
- Reveal that explains "who" but leaves "why" hollow.
- Romance subplot that eclipses the investigation.

## Touchstones
- Tana French, *The Likeness*
- Anthony Horowitz, *Magpie Murders*
- Richard Osman, *The Thursday Murder Club*
- Attica Locke, *Bluebird, Bluebird*
- Louise Penny, *Still Life*
