# Character Arcs

Use for: choosing and structuring the change (or refusal to change) the lead undergoes across the novel.

## The three arcs

- **Positive change arc (lie -> truth):** Character starts believing a lie that protects the wound. Plot pressure forces them to test the lie, fail with it, and finally act on the truth at the climax. The reward is internal wholeness, often at external cost. Default arc for coming-of-age, redemption, literary realism, most romance leads, most fantasy chosen-one stories.
- **Negative arc (corruption / disillusionment / fall):** Character either (a) doubles down on the lie until it destroys them, (b) glimpses the truth and rejects it, or (c) starts with the truth and is broken into the lie. Default arc for tragedy, noir, crime sagas, prestige-TV antiheroes, certain horror leads.
- **Flat arc (steadfast in a broken world):** Character already knows the truth on page one. The world believes the lie. They hold the truth under pressure and convert others. Default arc for series detectives, classic Westerns, mentor-heroes, much military SF, procedural protagonists where stability is the point.

## Choosing by genre

- Romance: positive for both leads, mirrored lies, climax = vulnerability.
- Mystery series: flat for the sleuth (don't change Poirot); positive for the victim's circle.
- Thriller: flat or mild positive; reader wants competence under pressure.
- Literary: positive or negative; ambiguity is acceptable, vagueness is not.
- Tragedy / noir: negative; signal it early so the ending feels inevitable.
- Epic fantasy: positive for the lead, flat for the mentor, negative for the rival.
- Horror: positive (final girl learns) or negative (protagonist consumed).

## Structural placement

- **Act 1 - plant the lie:** Show the character acting on the lie in a low-stakes scene. Let it work for them once so the reader sees why they cling to it. Name the wound only by implication. The Want should be a strategy the lie endorses.
- **Act 2A - the lie produces wins:** Apply the lie to escalating problems. It still mostly works. End the midpoint with a victory that costs more than it returns.
- **Act 2B - the lie produces losses:** The same behavior now backfires. Allies named. Mirror scene to Act 1 where the strategy fails. Force the ghost to surface (revelation, confession, flashback) so the reader understands the cost of changing.
- **Climax - force the choice:** Construct a scene where the character can only get the Want by acting on the lie, or only get the Need by abandoning it. They cannot have both. The choice is the arc.
  - Positive: choose Need, often lose Want.
  - Negative: choose lie, gain Want, lose self.
  - Flat: refuse the false choice, hold the truth, pay the price, convert a witness.
- **Resolution:** Show the new equilibrium in behavior, not narration. One small action that the old self could not have taken (or, for negative, an action the old self would have refused).

## Common failure modes

- Arc happens off-page during a time skip; the reader sees before and after but not the turn.
- Transformation feels unearned because the lie was never tested at full cost.
- Epiphany delivered as interior monologue with no externalizing action.
- Lie too obviously wrong from page one, so the reader is ahead of the character for 300 pages.
- Negative arc that reads as a positive arc the writer lost the nerve to finish.
- Flat arc with a character who isn't actually competent enough to earn steadfastness; reads as stubbornness.
- Multiple POV characters with identical arcs; ensemble feels redundant.
- Wound revealed in chapter one, removing the engine of curiosity.
- Climax choice is between good and bad rather than between two values the character holds.
