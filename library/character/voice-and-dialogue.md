# Voice and Dialogue

Use for: making every speaking character sound like one specific person and writing dialogue that carries subtext, not exposition.

## Voice parameters (set per character)

Define each axis before drafting. Write the character's voice card once and keep it open while writing their scenes.

- **Vocabulary range:** Concrete vs abstract, plain vs Latinate, technical jargon domain (law, ranching, code), profanity threshold, slang generation. A character who says "litigate" never also says "ain't" without intent.
- **Sentence length and rhythm:** Clipped (four to seven words, frequent periods) vs winding (subordinate clauses, semicolons, qualifiers). Anxiety often shortens; evasion often lengthens. Match to internal state.
- **What they avoid saying:** Each character has a forbidden register. The grieving widow doesn't say "dead." The CEO doesn't say "I don't know." The teenager doesn't say "I'm scared." The avoidance is the characterization.
- **Default emotional pitch:** Where the needle sits at rest: dry, warm, wry, clinical, hot, formal, sardonic. Deviations from this baseline are events.
- **Regionalisms and class markers:** Specific, not generic. "Soda" vs "pop" vs "Coke" (meaning any cola). Idiom from a specific occupation or place. One or two markers consistently, not a thick dialect transcription.
- **Filler and tics:** "Look," "I mean," "Right?", trailing "...so." Use sparingly; one tic per character, deployed under pressure.
- **Pronoun and address habits:** Who they call by first name, surname, title, nickname; whether they use "ma'am," whether they refuse to.

## Tag-free dialogue

- Prefer action beats over "he said angrily." The action shows the emotion: "He set the glass down too softly."
- When three or more lines pass between beats, anchor with one tag or beat to keep the reader oriented.
- Cut adverbs in tags. If the line needs "angrily," rewrite the line.
- Let voice itself identify the speaker. If a reader can't tell who is talking with all tags stripped, the voices aren't distinct enough yet.
- Beats double as scene-grounding: a beat is a chance to show the room, the weather, a prop. Use it.

## Subtext

- Characters lie, deflect, perform, talk past each other, answer the question they wish had been asked.
- The line on the page is rarely the line in the chest. Write what they say; trust the reader to hear what they mean.
- Conflict in dialogue often comes from mismatched goals in the same conversation: she wants comfort, he wants to be right, neither names it.
- Silence and refusal count as dialogue. A character who doesn't answer the question has answered it.
- A scene's emotional climax often arrives as the smallest line ("Okay," "Fine," a name spoken without preamble).

## Patterns that work

- Interruption and overlap (em dashes for cut-offs, ellipses for trailing).
- Repetition with variation: one character echoes another's phrase back, weaponized.
- Asymmetric register: one speaks formally, the other slangy; mismatch creates friction.
- Off-topic deflection: a character changes the subject and the reader feels the wound move.

## Common failure modes

- On-the-nose dialogue: characters state feelings, motives, themes plainly. "I'm angry because you abandoned me at the harbor" is therapy, not drama.
- Info-dump in conversation: two characters telling each other things both already know ("As you know, Sister...").
- Everyone sounding like the narrator: same vocabulary, same rhythm, same wit. Symptom: you can't pass the strip-tags test.
- Dialect rendered phonetically across whole paragraphs; condescending and unreadable. One or two markers is enough.
- Speech tic deployed every line until it becomes the character's whole personality.
- Question-and-answer ping-pong that exists only to deliver plot information.
- Profanity used as flavoring instead of as a calibrated register break.
- Characters answering the literal question instead of the one they're afraid of.
