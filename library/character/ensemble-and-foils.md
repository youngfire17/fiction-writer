Use for: designing a supporting cast and antagonist that pressurize the protagonist's arc instead of decorating it.

# Ensemble and Foils

Every supporting character earns their page count by doing structural work on the protagonist or theme. If a character can be removed without rewriting the protagonist's choices, they are decoration.

## Foils

A foil is a character built to expose the protagonist by contrast or mirror.

- Contrast foil: shares the protagonist's situation but made the opposite choice. Shows the road not taken. The friend who married young while the protagonist left town.
- Mirror foil: shares the protagonist's Lie or Wound and either heals or destroys themselves. Previews the two possible endings of the protagonist's arc.
- Thematic foil: embodies the opposing answer to the book's central question. If the book asks "is forgiveness possible," the foil has answered "no" and built a life on it.
- Best practice: design at least one mirror and one contrast foil for any protagonist. Place them on opposite sides of the protagonist's social orbit so the protagonist must move between them.

## Archetypal roles (use as scaffolding, not formula)

- Mentor: gives the protagonist the tool, framework, or permission to attempt the Want. Often carries a partial truth and a blind spot. Departure (death, betrayal, withdrawal) is usually required so the protagonist must internalize the lesson.
- Threshold guardian: tests whether the protagonist is committed enough to enter the new world. Not the antagonist; more like a bouncer. Can be defeated, bypassed, or won over. Each act often has one.
- Shapeshifter: loyalty is unclear and shifts. Forces the protagonist to question their read of people. The romantic interest whose motives are ambiguous, the colleague who may be feeding information to the rival. Should genuinely shift, not just be revealed.
- Trickster: punctures pretension, including the protagonist's. Often the only character who tells the protagonist the truth about themselves. Generates levity and theme simultaneously. Underused in literary fiction.
- Shadow: external embodiment of the protagonist's Lie taken to its logical conclusion. Often but not always the antagonist. What the protagonist becomes if they refuse to change.
- Herald: brings the call to adventure or the news that forces the inciting incident. Can be minor; often appears once.
- Ally: shares the journey but has their own Want. Should disagree with the protagonist about method at least once.

Most named characters fill more than one role across the book. Don't cast one-per-slot.

## Designing the antagonist

The antagonist is the protagonist of their own novel. Write the book from their POV in one paragraph before drafting.

- They have a Want that is coherent and, from inside their worldview, justified.
- They have a Wound and a Lie of their own. Often the same Wound as the protagonist, healed in a darker direction. This makes them the shadow.
- Their goal must materially block the protagonist's Want, not just oppose them philosophically.
- Their competence must be visible and at least equal to the protagonist's in the domain that matters.
- They should be right about something the protagonist is wrong about. The reader should feel the pull.
- On-page screen time matters less than felt pressure. A villain who never appears can still drive the book if their decisions land in every scene.
- Avoid: cackling, kicking dogs, monologuing motive. Show the worldview through choices under pressure.

## Ensemble dynamics

- Map who knows what about whom. Information asymmetry generates scene tension.
- Give the cast competing Wants so allies create friction, not just support.
- Cluster characters into factions of two or three; one character should bridge factions and pay for it.
- Track who the protagonist confides in across the book. The shift in confidant is itself an arc beat.

## Common failure modes

- Single-trait sidekick: the funny one, the loyal one, the smart one. Reduces to a label and stops generating scenes. Fix by giving them their own Want that occasionally conflicts with the protagonist's.
- Antagonist as obstacle, not person: pure function, no interiority. Reader feels no thematic charge.
- Yes-man ensemble: every supporting character agrees with the protagonist and admires them. No friction, no pressure on the Lie.
- Mentor who never leaves: lingers past their structural usefulness and solves the climax for the protagonist.
- Shapeshifter who is just a twist: the reveal is the entire character. Plant genuine ambivalence so the shift is felt, not sprung.
- Cast of mirrors: everyone shares the protagonist's worldview and class, so the book has no foil pressure.
- Roles assigned one-to-one: a named mentor, a named trickster, etc., like a checklist. Better when a single character carries two functions and trades them across the book.
- Antagonist and protagonist never share a scene with real stakes, so the rivalry is theoretical.
- Romantic interest with no Want outside the relationship.
- Children and elderly characters used only for thematic ornament rather than story function.
