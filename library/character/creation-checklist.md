# Character Creation Checklist

Use for: building a new protagonist, antagonist, or major supporting character from scratch before drafting scenes.

## The ten required fields

Fill every field. Vague answers ("she wants happiness") fail; specific answers ("she wants her sister's name cleared before the inquest closes Friday") work.

- **Want (external goal):** The concrete, observable thing the character pursues. Must be visible on the page. Test: could a camera film them chasing it?
- **Need (internal):** The truth they must accept to be whole. Usually contradicts the Want, or only becomes reachable once the Want is sacrificed.
- **Wound:** The unhealed injury (loss, betrayal, humiliation, abandonment) that distorts their behavior. Specific incident, not a mood.
- **Lie they believe:** The false rule the wound taught them ("If I'm useful, I won't be left"; "Trust gets you killed"). Drives self-sabotage.
- **Ghost (backstory event):** The single dramatized scene from before page one that forged the lie. One image, one moment. Reveal it late, not upfront.
- **Competence:** What they are demonstrably good at. Earns reader respect within the first 30 pages. Not a hobby list; a usable skill the plot will tax.
- **Contradiction:** A trait that cuts against the dominant impression. The ruthless prosecutor who feeds stray cats. Keeps them off the archetype rail.
- **Flaw:** The active weakness the antagonist or plot can exploit. Must cost them something on-page. Not "clumsy" or "too caring."
- **Speech tic:** A repeatable verbal fingerprint: a hedge ("I mean,"), a refusal to swear, a clinical register, a tendency to ask questions instead of answering.
- **Physical specificity:** One concrete, non-generic body detail tied to history or behavior: a thumb broken twice, a chipped incisor, lacquered nails always one color behind fashion.

## Diagnostic questions

- What would they never do, and what scene forces them to do it?
- Whose approval do they still want that they will never get?
- What do they lie about to themselves before breakfast?
- If they got the Want without the Need, what would rot?

## Worked example: Mara Voss, forensic accountant, 38

- Want: Recover the $4.2M her late father skimmed and return it before the audit on June 1.
- Need: Accept that her father was a thief and stop laundering his memory.
- Wound: Age 11, watched her mother evicted while father bought a boat.
- Lie: "If I fix his mistakes, he was a good man and I was loved."
- Ghost: The afternoon she sat on the curb counting moving boxes while her father waved from the marina.
- Competence: Can reconstruct a shell-company tree from three bank statements in an hour.
- Contradiction: Rigid about taxes; shoplifts paperback mysteries.
- Flaw: Cannot accept help; turns allies into auditors.
- Speech tic: Restates the question before answering it.
- Physical: Bites the cap of her pen flat within a week; left thumbnail always short.

## Common failure modes

- Want and Need pointing the same direction, so the arc has no friction.
- Backstory delivered as monologue instead of a single ghost image.
- "Flaw" that is secretly a virtue (works too hard, cares too much).
- Competence the plot never stresses, making it set dressing.
- Speech tic applied for one chapter then forgotten.
- Physical detail that is a stock descriptor (green eyes, tall) instead of a fingerprint.
- Contradiction so large it reads as inconsistency rather than depth.
