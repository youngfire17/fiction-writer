# Iceberg and Reveal

Use for: deciding what worldbuilding material reaches the page, and how. Apply during outlining, drafting, and especially when tempted to insert a paragraph explaining how something works.

## The Iceberg Principle

- Design roughly 90% of your world; show roughly 10%. The unseen mass gives the visible tip its weight and authority.
- Hemingway's original formulation: the dignity of an iceberg is due to only one-eighth being above water. Readers feel the submerged mass even when they cannot see it.
- The author must *know* the trade laws, succession customs, and metallurgy. The reader needs to *feel* that someone knows.
- Knowing more than you show prevents contradictions, lets you answer "what about X" naturally, and gives prose a confident texture.

## What to Submerge

- Comprehensive history beyond what touches the plot.
- Economic mechanics, unless trade is the story.
- Linguistic families, unless language is plot.
- Religious schisms older than the current generation, unless they motivate someone on-page.
- Every culture's full pantheon — show the gods the POV characters actually pray to or curse by.

## Reveal Pacing

- Drip world detail through *character action*. A baker shaking flour from her cuffs while arguing tax law tells you about food, class, and politics in one beat.
- Reveal information when the POV character *would* think it. A native doesn't muse on her own currency; a stranger does. Use travelers, exiles, and outsiders as natural exposition vectors — sparingly.
- Three reveals per scene maximum. Beyond that the reader stops absorbing.
- Withhold deliberately. A name the characters refuse to speak is more powerful than a glossary entry.

## Avoid the Encyclopedia Entry

- Symptoms: paragraphs beginning "The Kingdom of X had been founded three centuries ago when..." Narrator stops the story to lecture.
- Symptoms: characters explaining things both already know ("As you know, Brother, our family has always..."). This is As-You-Know-Bob dialogue.
- Symptoms: italicized history dumps between chapters that the reader skips.
- Fix: cut the lecture, distribute the load across action, dialogue subtext, sensory detail, and a single well-placed sentence of context per chapter.

## The Competence Reveal

- A character casually using a thing teaches the reader more than any explanation.
- Show the swordswoman oiling her blade against rust from sea air — we now know about climate, her profession, and her habits, without exposition.
- Show the mage paying her debt at the soul-broker's stall before buying bread — we learn about magic's cost, the economy, and the mundane integration of the arcane.
- The rule: if a native of your world would do X without comment, your character should also do X without comment. The reader's brain fills in the world.
- Corollary: jargon used confidently teaches faster than jargon defined. "She paid in trade-marks, not crown" — we infer two currencies and their class associations.

## Worldbuilder's Disease

- The compulsion to design before drafting, and to keep designing instead of drafting.
- Symptoms: 40-page appendices before chapter one; conlangs with conjugation tables before any character speaks; maps with named villages no character visits; calendars correlating three lunar cycles for a story spanning one summer.
- Treatment: design only what the next three chapters touch. Backfill as plot demands. Trust that you can invent details that feel consistent if your principles are clear.
- Tolkien is the bad example everyone cites. Tolkien spent decades and was a philologist. You are probably not Tolkien, and even Tolkien cut massively for publication.
- Worldbuilding is procrastination wearing a respectable hat. The book is the deliverable.

## Application: Shifting Continent

- The reader does not need the full mechanism of the rewrite in chapter one. They need to *feel* it through a single grandmother saying "the river used to run east" — and meaning it literally.
- Reserve the deep cosmology for late-book payoff. Let the strangeness accumulate.

## Common Failure Modes

- Front-loading: dumping setting in the first chapter to "establish" the world. Readers bounce.
- Map-first writing: designing the world fully before knowing what story you're telling, then forcing the story to tour the map.
- Glossary dependence: readers shouldn't need to flip back. If they do, the prose isn't carrying its weight.
- Showing the iceberg: trying to display all your research. Restraint reads as depth; abundance reads as insecurity.
- Equal-weight detailing: treating the tavern menu with the same care as the political climax. Allocate detail to what matters.
- Lecture-disguised-as-dialogue: two natives explaining their own world to each other for the reader's benefit.
