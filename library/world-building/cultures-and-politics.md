# Cultures and Politics

Use for: designing the peoples, polities, faiths, and economies of a setting. Apply whenever a new nation, faction, or social group enters the story — and when auditing existing ones for flatness.

## Core Design Questions per Culture

- **What do they value?** Honor, hospitality, lineage, literacy, cleanliness, beauty, cunning, restraint, ferocity. Pick two or three primary values that genuinely conflict with neighbors.
- **What do they fear?** Famine, foreign gods, the sea, their own ancestors, being forgotten, the next rewriting of the land. Fear shapes ritual, law, and architecture.
- **How is power held?** Bloodline, election, conquest, priesthood, wealth, age, magical aptitude, ownership of something scarce. Who can lose power and how?
- **How does the economy actually work?** Where does food come from? Who grows it, who eats it, who taxes it? What is exported, what is imported, what is forbidden? Currency, credit, debt, slavery, guild monopolies.
- **What does daily life look like for non-elites?** The peasant, the dockworker, the laundress, the apprentice scribe. If you cannot describe their morning, you have built a stage set, not a culture.

## Designing Inter-Cultural Conflict

- Move past good-vs-evil. Real conflicts come from incompatible values held by reasonable people. One culture sees river-burial as honor; their neighbor sees it as poisoning the water their children drink. Both are right within their frame.
- Resource conflicts: water rights, ore veins, fishing grounds, trade choke points. These outlast ideologies.
- Honor conflicts: insult cycles, blood feuds, marriage politics. Small offenses become wars when status systems collide.
- Cosmological conflicts: when two religions make exclusive truth-claims that touch land or law (whose dead lie beneath this hill?).
- Avoid the dark lord. A foreign culture should be *legible* — readers should understand why its members do what they do, even if they disagree.

## Religion and Magic Interplay

- If magic is real and observable, religion reorganizes around it. Either: (a) religion claims authority over magic (priesthoods, sacred orders), (b) religion defines itself *against* magic (heresy, purges), or (c) religion competes with magic for the same questions (death, fate, weather).
- Decide what your faith offers that magic cannot: meaning, community, consolation, afterlife, ethics. Magic answers "how"; religion answers "why."
- Multiple religions per culture, usually. Folk practice differs from temple doctrine. The educated theologian and the village charm-maker share a faith only nominally.
- Saints, ancestors, household gods, seasonal observances. Most religion is not theology — it is what grandmother does on the third day after a death.

## Names, Calendars, Food, Kinship

- **Names:** patterns reveal culture. Patronymics (Bjornsson) imply lineage focus; occupational surnames (Smith) imply mobility; theophoric names (Theodore) imply religious saturation. Avoid apostrophe-soup; pick a phonetic palette and stick to it.
- **Calendars:** what does the year hinge on? Harvest, flood, religious cycle, regnal year. Whose calendar is official when cultures meet?
- **Food:** the fastest culture-builder. What's the staple grain, the feast meat, the despised food, the taboo? Who eats with whom? Who serves? Trade goods (spice, salt, sugar) reveal economic reach.
- **Kinship:** nuclear family, extended clan, matrilineal, patrilineal, age-set, fictive (adoption, blood-brotherhood). Inheritance rules drive plots — primogeniture, partible inheritance, ultimogeniture, dowry.

## Avoid

- **Medieval-Europe-with-the-serial-numbers-filed-off:** generic kings, knights, taverns, and serfs in vaguely French clothing. If your culture is European-coded, commit and research; otherwise diverge meaningfully.
- **Monoculture nations:** "The Elves are wise, the Dwarves are gruff." Real polities contain regional dialects, class divisions, religious minorities, rural-urban splits. The capital does not speak for the hinterland.
- **Hat cultures:** the merchant culture, the warrior culture, the scholar culture. Every culture does all of these; emphasis differs.
- **Static cultures:** "the X have always been Y for ten thousand years." Cultures change in decades. Show generational drift.

## Application: Shifting Continent

- Cultures here would be defined partly by *what they carry* across rewrites — songs, recipes, rituals, bloodlines, portable wealth. Geography-bound cultures must adapt or die each generation.
- Likely cultural categories: anchored peoples (who guard places that don't change), nomadic adapters, mourners-of-lost-lands, opportunists who profit from chaos, priesthoods who interpret each rewrite as divine message.
- Politics centers on legitimacy claims across rewrites. "Our family ruled the Vale" means little when the Vale is now an inland sea.

## Common Failure Modes

- Designing cultures top-down (king, court, capital) without bottom-up (farmer, market, kitchen).
- One-axis differentiation: cultures distinguished only by clothing color and accent.
- Economies that don't add up: feudal kingdoms with no visible peasants, trade empires with no ships, cities with no hinterland feeding them.
- Religion as flavor sprinkle rather than as social infrastructure.
- All cultures sharing the protagonist's modern values, except the antagonist's.
- Naming inconsistency: characters from the same culture with names drawn from three real-world languages.
- Conflict reduced to misunderstandings resolvable by the protagonist explaining things.
