# Magic Systems

Use for: designing any supernatural, divine, or arcane force in a setting — including the test book's continent-rewriting magic. Apply before drafting any scene where magic affects plot outcomes.

## Sanderson's Three Laws

- **First Law:** An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is directly proportional to how well the reader understands said magic. If the protagonist resolves the climax with magic, the rules must be established earlier. Surprise resolutions via undisclosed powers are deus ex machina.
- **Second Law:** Limitations are more interesting than powers. What a system *cannot* do, what it *costs*, and what it *breaks* create story. A character who can do anything has no stakes.
- **Third Law:** Expand what you have before adding something new. Deepen existing rules, find unexpected applications, before bolting on new powers. Most "magic creep" failures violate this.

## Hard vs. Soft Systems

- **Hard (Sanderson, Brandon; Fullmetal Alchemist):** Rules explicit to the reader. Magic functions as a logic puzzle. Used to *solve* problems — but only because the reader can audit the solution. Mistborn's metals each have defined effects, costs, and interactions.
- **Soft (Tolkien, Gaiman, Le Guin partly):** Rules hidden or mythic. Magic creates *atmosphere* and *problems*, rarely solutions. Gandalf's power is mostly off-page; when he fights the Balrog, we don't track mana. Use soft magic for wonder, hard magic for puzzles.
- **The danger zone:** Soft magic that solves plot problems. Readers feel cheated. If your wizard rescues the heroes, either the rescue must be costly/limited (hard-ish) or it must create a worse problem.

## The Three Legs: Cost, Limitation, Consequence

- **Cost:** What does the user spend? Stamina (Wheel of Time channeling exhaustion), substance (Mistborn metals consumed), sanity (Elric), time, memory, lifespan. No cost = no tension.
- **Limitation:** What can it not do? Range, target type, prerequisites, vulnerabilities. Allomancy needs metals; Aes Sedai cannot lie outright; Earthsea's true-name magic requires knowing the name.
- **Consequence:** What happens after? Social (channelers are feared and gentled), physical (taint, scarring), metaphysical (drawing attention from worse things). Cost is paid by the user; consequence ripples outward.

## Magic Must Constrain Plot, Not Solve It

- Ask: does this magic system *generate* problems the characters must navigate, or does it *erase* problems? Good systems do both, with the generating dominant.
- The teleport test: if a character can teleport, why doesn't the plot end? Either limit it (line of sight, cost a finger, only to places you've been) or the world has no plot.
- Magic should narrow choices into hard dilemmas, not widen them into easy outs.

## Worked Examples

- **Mistborn (hard):** Eight base metals, each one power. Costs metal supply; limited by what's in your stomach; consequences include being identifiable by other Allomancers. The climax solutions are auditable.
- **Earthsea (medium-hard):** True-name magic. Cost: equilibrium — every working pushes the world out of balance, and unbalanced magic returns ruin. Limitation: you must know the name. Consequence: Ged unleashes a shadow that hunts him for books.
- **Wheel of Time (medium-soft drifting hard):** Saidin/Saidar. Cost: physical exhaustion, burnout, madness (saidin's taint). Limitation: weaves require specific patterns and talents. Consequence: gentling, the Dragon's curse, political terror.
- **Tolkien (soft):** Rings, Maiar, songs of power. We never tabulate mana. Magic feels mythic because we don't see the math.

## Application: Shifting-Continent Magic

- If the land itself rewrites, treat *that* as the central system. Cost (who or what pays?), cadence (every generation — why?), limitation (what cannot change — mountains? coasts? bloodlines?), consequence (loss of place, refugee cultures, the profession of cartography becoming sacred).
- The rewrite must constrain plot: characters cannot rely on maps, ancestral lands, or memorized routes. This is the engine.

## Common Failure Modes

- Adding new powers whenever plot demands it (violates Third Law).
- Magic with no cost — wish-fulfillment, not story.
- Soft magic deployed as hard solution (deus ex machina).
- Rules that change between scenes without in-world reason.
- Power inflation: by book three, the protagonist outpowers all stakes.
- Treating magic as decoration rather than as a constraint on the world's economy, politics, and daily life.
- Forgetting that *everyone* has access to the same physics — if magic exists, society reorganizes around it.
