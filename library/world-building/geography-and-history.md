# Geography and History

Use for: designing the physical world and its temporal depth — terrain, climate, trade, ruins, languages, and the layered past. Apply when sketching the continent, plotting travel, situating cities, or deciding what the protagonist's grandmother remembers that the protagonist does not.

## Geography Drives Plot

- **Trade routes:** goods flow along the easiest path — rivers, coasts, passes, caravan tracks. Wherever trade flows, cities grow, taxes are collected, and conflicts cluster. Plot follows trade because power follows trade.
- **Choke points:** straits, fords, mountain passes, single bridges, desert oases. Whoever holds the choke point holds disproportionate power. Most strategic plots route through one. Thermopylae, the Bosporus, the Khyber, the Strait of Hormuz.
- **Climate:** dictates crops, dictates calendars, dictates wars (campaigning season is when the roads are dry and the granaries full). A monsoon culture and a steppe culture cannot fight each other on the same schedule.
- **Resources:** what is here that is *not* there? Iron, salt, tin, amber, silk, jade, the only deepwater port for 800 miles. Scarcity drives trade, war, and migration. A desert with one well is a kingdom.
- **Distance and friction:** how long does a message take? An army? A rumor? Pre-modern travel is slow and seasonal — empires are shaped by how far a courier can ride before winter.
- **Barriers:** mountains, deserts, seas, rivers, forests, magical zones. Barriers preserve cultural difference. The flatter the land, the more uniform the people. Plains breed empires; archipelagos breed languages.

## Layering History

- **Living memory (0-80 years):** what the oldest characters personally remember. Wars they fought, plagues they survived, rulers they served. Emotionally hot. Distorted by trauma and self-interest.
- **Recent history (80-300 years):** documented, contested, taught. Founding myths of current dynasties. The frame for present politics. Multiple competing narratives.
- **Old history (300-1000 years):** chronicled but mythologized. Heroes have grown larger; villains flatter. Place-names and laws still trace back here.
- **Legend (1000-3000 years):** half-remembered, half-invented. Names of cities now gone. Magic worked differently. Cited but not trusted.
- **Forgotten (3000+ years):** survives only as ruins, loanwords, place-names whose meaning is lost, oddly-shaped artifacts no one can use. Archaeology, not history.
- Each layer should contradict the next. The official chronicle disagrees with the village song which disagrees with the dug-up tablet. Truth is triangulated, never given.

## Ruins, Languages, Archaeology as Plot Fuel

- **Ruins** are mysteries with built-in stakes. Who built this? What killed them? What did they know that we don't? A ruin in the protagonist's path is a free subplot.
- **Loanwords** reveal contact: who traded with whom, who conquered whom. If the word for "wine" is foreign, viticulture came from elsewhere. If "law" is foreign, so did the legal system.
- **Place-names** are fossils. A river called "the river" in a dead language means the namers preceded the current speakers. Layered names (modern + old + older) signal layered conquests.
- **Material culture mismatch:** a peasant farming with iron tools beside a ruined city of unknown alloy. The gap is the story.
- **Forbidden archaeology:** the temple that no one digs because of the curse, the tomb the priesthood seals each generation. These are doors. Open them in act three.
- **Scripts and decipherment:** an unread inscription is a quest. A character who can read what others cannot is power.

## Shifting Continent

- **Rules for change:** define before drafting.
  - *Cadence:* every generation (roughly 25-30 years). Predictable enough to plan around, unpredictable enough to dread. A regular cadence creates demographic cohorts — "the survivors of the Fourth Rewriting."
  - *Scope:* what changes? Rivers reroute, coastlines retreat, mountains rise or subside, forests migrate, islands appear or sink. Cities may relocate, be buried, or be left absurdly inland.
  - *What stays fixed:* anchors. The sun and stars. The two oldest mountains. The Bone Coast. A single deep river. Certain holy sites protected by something. Fixed points let cartographers, navigators, and refugees orient. Without anchors the world is unreadable; with too many, the rewrite is toothless.
  - *What is lost:* roads, fields, graves, borders, the meaning of old maps. Buildings may survive but disconnected from their context. Languages tied to specific landscapes may lose their referents ("the word for marsh-bird, but there is no marsh now").
  - *Triggers and warnings:* does the rewrite arrive with omens — birds fleeing, rivers running backward for an hour, dreams shared across a region? Warning windows generate plot.
- **In-world response:**
  - *Cartography as profession:* mapmakers are sacred or strategic. Pre-rewrite maps are heirlooms or contraband. Post-rewrite remapping is a multi-year state project. Guilds, secrets, forgeries.
  - *Generational memory:* the elders are living archives. To know where the old well was, you ask the eighty-year-old. Cultures may institutionalize elder-keepers, mnemonics, recitation cycles.
  - *Oral tradition:* songs encode geography — "the river runs north past the elm, then east past the standing stone." Memorized travel-poems function as portable maps. Singers are guides.
  - *Magical anchors:* objects, bloodlines, or sites that resist rewriting. Anchor-keepers are political. Anchor-theft is casus belli. An anchor that fails is catastrophic.
  - *Architecture:* portable, modular, or deliberately ruin-resistant. Tent-cities, stone foundations meant to survive as ruins, buildings designed to be re-found.
  - *Law and inheritance:* deeds and borders are continually relitigated. "Pre-Rewriting" and "Post-Rewriting" claims. Lawyers and oracles overlap.
  - *Religion:* the rewrite is interpreted — punishment, renewal, divine breath, the world's dream. Each interpretation supports a different priesthood.
  - *Refugee cultures:* peoples defined by their lost homeland. Mourners-of-the-Vale, the People-Who-Were-Coastal. Diaspora identities harden across rewrites.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Map without weather:** continents drawn with no climate logic — deserts beside rainforests, no rain shadows, rivers that flow uphill or branch without joining.
- **Convenient distances:** travel time stretches or contracts to suit pacing. Either commit to realistic distances or have a magic system that handles transit; do not handwave.
- **Resource-free economies:** capitals with no visible hinterland feeding them, mountain kingdoms exporting goods they have no source for.
- **Flat history:** "a thousand years ago there was a war, and then nothing happened until now." Empty centuries are a tell. Real history is dense and uneven.
- **Single-layer past:** one dead empire, one set of ruins, one lost language. Real landscapes accumulate. Stack the layers.
- **The convenient ruin:** an ancient site that exists only to give the protagonist a sword. Ruins should imply prior worlds, not vending machines.
- **Shifting-continent failures:**
  - *No rules:* the rewrite happens whenever the plot needs a new setting. Reader stops believing.
  - *No anchors:* total flux makes characters' attachment to land incoherent — why mourn what was always going to vanish?
  - *No consequences shown:* a continent that rewrites should produce visible scars — abandoned roads to nowhere, stranded lighthouses, families with hereditary memory-keeping roles. If daily life looks normal, the premise is wasted.
  - *Rewrite as scenery:* using the shifting continent only for spectacle, not for plot pressure. The rewrite should constrain protagonists' choices — force migrations, sever supply lines, separate lovers, reveal old ruins.
  - *Ignoring the cartographer:* in this world, mapmakers are protagonists by default. If they are absent or trivial, the worldbuilding is not earning its premise.
