# Cormac McCarthy

Use for: austere literary frontier or post-apocalyptic prose, biblical cadence, moral weight carried by landscape and silence.

## Voice signature
- Long, conjunction-stitched sentences alternated with severe four-word fragments. The long ones run on by parataxis, not subordination.
- Almost no commas where modern usage expects them. No semicolons. No quotation marks. No apostrophes in many contractions (dont, wont, cant).
- Vocabulary mixes plain Anglo-Saxon monosyllables with sudden archaic or scientific terms (caldera, gambrel, pampooties, alluvial).
- Paragraphs are blocks of description punctuated by terse exchange. Description carries the metaphysics; characters carry the plot.
- Past tense, third person, omniscient but cold. The narrator sees but does not judge.
- Rhythm is King James Bible crossed with Faulkner crossed with a man who has been awake too long.

## Diction tells
- Parataxis: "and...and...and." Clauses joined like beads on wire.
- Definite articles where indefinite would be expected: "the boy," "the man," not names.
- Landscape verbs: things stand, lie, tend, abide. The sun does not rise; it stood up out of the earth.
- Spanish phrases dropped untranslated in the border novels. Latinate nouns for blood and weather.
- "He squatted." "He studied the country." "Out there past men's knowing."
- Negatives as affirmation: not unlike, not without, no small thing.

## Dialogue technique
- No quotation marks. Dialogue is set off by line breaks alone.
- Tags are minimal or absent. Often just: He said.
- Speech is laconic, gnomic, often a question answered with another question.
- Men speak in aphorisms when pressed. Subtext is theological, not psychological.

## Worldview and recurring themes
- Violence as the floor of human nature. The indifference of God or his absence. Fathers and sons under threat. The land older than language. Witness as the last available virtue.

## In-the-style-of pastiche

They rode out before dawn with the horses breathing smoke and the stars still hard in the west. The road went up through scrub oak and broken country and they did not speak. By midmorning they had come to the rim of a dry lake and they sat the horses there and looked down at the white pan of it and at the bones of something large half buried in the alkali. The boy asked what it had been and the man said he did not know. He said that whatever it was had come a long way to die in such a place.

The woman set the bowl before him and stood back. He ate without looking up. Outside the wind moved in the cottonwoods and somewhere down the arroyo a dog was barking and would not be quieted. When he was done he wiped the bowl with the last of the bread and pushed it from him. You can stay the night, she said. He shook his head. I have been too long from where I am going, he said. She did not ask him where that was.

The fire had burned down to coals and the coals to ash and the man sat with the rifle across his knees and watched the dark beyond the dark. There was no moon. There was nothing in the world but the small red eye of the fire and the slow turning of the cold stars and the boy asleep with his hand curled near his face. He thought of his wife. He thought of the country they had crossed. He thought that a man could love a thing and not be saved by it and that this was perhaps the oldest truth there was.

## Do NOT
- Don't make every sentence sixty words. He cuts long with short. Pastiche becomes parody when the breath never stops.
- Don't overdo "and...and...and." Three in a paragraph is plenty.
- Don't write characters who explain themselves. They withhold.
- Don't reach for thesaurus archaisms in every line. One unexpected word per paragraph, not five.
- Don't moralize. The landscape moralizes. The narrator only watches.
