# Stephen King

Use for: contemporary horror, small-town Americana, ordinary-people-in-extraordinary-trouble narration with a chatty, intimate close-third voice.

## Voice signature
- Sentence length swings wildly: long conversational sprawl, then a four-word punch. (He stopped. Listened. Nothing. Then, again.)
- Heavy use of close-third free indirect style; the narrator slides into the character's idiom mid-paragraph.
- Vocabulary is plainspoken, blue-collar American — Maine vowels in the word choice — with occasional ten-dollar words slipped in deadpan.
- Paragraphs vary from one-liners to half-page tides, mimicking the rhythm of a guy telling you a story at a bar.
- Loves the second-person aside to the reader. *You know how that goes.*
- Profanity is offhand, never decorative.

## Diction tells
- Brand names everywhere: Bud Light, Chevy, Crest, Walmart, the Red Sox. Specificity grounds the dread.
- Parenthetical asides mid-sentence, often comic or bleak (and wasn't that the truth).
- Italicized intrusive thoughts as direct character interiority, no tag: *Get out. Get out now.*
- Recurring tics: "all at once," "for a wonder," "in those days," "the thing was."
- Numbered or cataloged lists of small worries, like a character compulsively itemizing.
- Pop-culture references as shorthand for time and class.

## Dialogue technique
- Tags are almost always "said." Action beats do the heavy lifting.
- Dialect rendered phonetically but sparingly — dropped g's, "ayuh," "Christ on a cracker."
- Subtext rides under banter; characters joke when scared and get clipped when they care.
- Kids talk like kids. Adults underestimate them, which is usually the mistake.

## Worldview and recurring themes
- Ordinary American towns rotted from underneath. Childhood as both refuge and crime scene. Addiction, writers, faith failing, the cosmic indifference behind cheerful Main Streets. Evil is patient and specific. Decency is small and often enough.

## In-the-style-of pastiche

The diner was the kind of place where the coffee tasted like it had been brewed in 1974 and kept warm ever since. Donna refilled his cup without asking, the way she had every Tuesday for nine years, and Hal thought (not for the first time) that she might be the only person in Castle Rock who actually saw him. *She knows. She has to know.* Outside, a Buick the color of dried blood pulled into the lot, and the bell over the door did its tired little jingle.

Marcy was eleven and she knew three things for sure. One, her brother was lying about the basement. Two, Mom had been crying in the laundry room again, the dryer running so nobody would hear. Three, whatever was down there wasn't a raccoon, no matter what Dad said, no matter how loud he said it. She climbed the stairs to her room with the Lite-Brite under one arm and her heart doing that funny skipping thing.

He wrote four pages and they were all bad. Bad in the particular way that meant he was lying to himself about something, and the book knew it even if he didn't. Outside the window, snow was coming down over Bangor like God had finally lost interest. He poured another two fingers of Jim Beam (just two, he was being good) and stared at the screen until the cursor stopped looking like a cursor and started looking like a small black mouth.

## Do NOT
- Don't stack pop-culture references — one or two per paragraph, max.
- Don't over-italicize. Intrusive thoughts hit harder when rare.
- Avoid jump-scare prose; King builds dread by lingering on the mundane.
- Don't make every character a writer or a drunk. He earns those; pastiches don't.
- Don't lean on "ayuh." Maine flavor is seasoning, not the meal.
