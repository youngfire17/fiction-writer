# Ursula K. Le Guin

Use for: anthropological science fiction and quiet fantasy, contemplative omniscient narration, cultures-as-protagonists, prose that thinks while it describes.

## Voice signature
- Sentences are measured and balanced, often with a clause that turns the thought back on itself. Length is moderate; rhythm is iambic without being showy.
- Omniscient or distant third dominant, with the narrator's intelligence visible — she will pause to consider a custom or a word.
- Vocabulary is precise, slightly formal, Anglo-Saxon-leaning, with anthropological and ethical abstractions used plainly (kinship, gift, balance, debt).
- Paragraphs are shapely and self-contained, each one closing on a small completed thought rather than a cliffhanger.
- Frequent use of generalization-then-instance: a statement about how a people live, then one person doing the thing.
- Lyrical when the moment earns it, never decorative for its own sake.

## Diction tells
- "One" as a pronoun when speaking generally ("one did not refuse such an offer").
- Coined languages and titles built from real linguistic intuition: kemmer, shifgrethor, Ekumen. Always usable as ordinary nouns.
- The Taoist negative: not-doing, not-knowing, the use of emptiness.
- Compound words pressed together to estrange the familiar (kinwomen, soulbrother, daymoon).
- Concrete sensory anchors after abstraction: she names a color or a smell to bring a philosophical sentence to earth.
- "Perhaps" and "it may be that" as honest hedges, never as evasion.

## Dialogue technique
- Tags are quiet: said, answered, asked. No adverbial showmanship.
- Speech is courteous, often indirect, sometimes proverbial. Characters from different cultures misunderstand each other in believable ways, and the misunderstanding is the scene.
- Silence is a participant. She marks pauses with paragraphing or with the listener's gaze.
- Power differentials show up in pronouns, in who speaks first, in who is allowed to interrupt.

## Worldview and recurring themes
- The other is not the enemy; the failure to imagine the other is. Anarchism and gift economy. The ecology of belief. Aging and craft. Patience as a moral capacity. Stories as how a people survive themselves.

## In-the-style-of pastiche

On Werel they did not say a child was born. They said a child had come, as one might say a guest had come, with the implication that the child had chosen and might in time choose otherwise. Tiyo had been seven years coming, by the count of his mothers, and even now he sometimes seemed to be still considering. He sat on the doorstone with the dog and watched the rain rinse the kitchen yard, and Sera, watching him from the window, thought: he is deciding whether to stay another year.

It is hard to describe a city one has only walked in twice, but I will try, because the boy asked. The streets of Veo Deyu are narrow and they curve, not for any reason of hill or river but because the people who laid them out did not believe in straight lines between neighbors. A straight line, they said, is for strangers. So one walks always a little aside, and one arrives, when one arrives, by being expected.

The old weaver did not look up when I came in. This was not rudeness; it was courtesy of a kind I had not yet learned. To greet a guest before the thread was finished would be to tell the guest that the thread mattered less than they did, and the weaver did not lie. I sat on the stool by the door. After some while she tied off the row, set the shuttle down, and said, You have come about your sister.

## Do NOT
- Don't write in a hush of vague mysticism; her clarity is the point. Abstractions land because they are precise.
- Don't pile coined words on a first page. One or two, used naturally, do the work.
- Don't make the narrator emotionally cold. The distance is wise, not detached.
- Avoid male-default pronouns and breezy worldbuilding shortcuts; she earned the right to be slow.
- Don't end paragraphs on a cliffhanger; end them on a thought.
