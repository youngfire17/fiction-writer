# George R. R. Martin

Use for: low-magic political fantasy, tight third-person rotating POVs, sensory medievalism, feasts and battles rendered with equal physicality, dread that builds across chapters.

## Voice signature
- Sentence length is moderate-to-long with frequent comma-spliced rhythmic triples ("the wind, the cold, and the dark beyond the Wall").
- Tight third POV, each chapter named for the viewpoint character; the prose absorbs that character's class, education, and prejudices.
- Vocabulary blends plainspoken Anglo-Saxon and quasi-medieval flourish: ser, m'lord, craven, mummer, broken man. No anachronisms.
- Paragraphs cluster sensory detail — food, weather, heraldry, smells — into a slow accretive realism, then close with a barbed thought.
- Internal monologue in italics, short, often bitter or self-correcting: *He is no friend of mine.*
- Recurring refrains and house words used as choric anchors ("Winter is coming," "the things I do for love").

## Diction tells
- Heraldry rendered in correct blazon: argent, sable, gules, a direwolf rampant.
- Food described with menu specificity: capon stuffed with leeks and mushrooms, honeyed locusts, blood sausage, lemoncakes.
- Recurring tic: a one-line italic thought interrupting a paragraph of action.
- "Half a hundred," "a thousand thousand," "as much chance as a snowball in the seven hells." Numbers as folk hyperbole.
- Names with archaic spellings (Eddard, Catelyn, Jeyne). Bynames over surnames for smallfolk (Hot Pie, Dunk the Tall).
- "M'lord," "Aye," "Mayhaps," used by lower-born characters, never by nobles.

## Dialogue technique
- "Said" dominant, with class-marked verbs ("muttered," "drawled") used sparingly. Tags often at the end with a noble's title attached.
- Lords speak in measured cadences and proverbs. Common folk speak in shorter clauses and contractions. Children speak plainly and are usually right.
- Subtext rides on what is not said in a council chamber; the political scene is a chess match with dialogue as the pieces.
- Threats are couched in courtesies. Compliments carry knives.

## Worldview and recurring themes
- There are no clean victories. Honor is expensive and often fatal. Power corrupts on a sliding scale. Smallfolk pay for great lords' games. Family is the deepest loyalty and the deepest wound. Winter, in every sense, is coming.

## In-the-style-of pastiche

The hall smelled of woodsmoke and wet wool and the stew the cook had been boiling since dawn. Ser Aemon sat at the low table with the other squires, his trencher untouched, watching his father at the high seat. Lord Wyman was laughing at something the septon had said, his red face redder still with wine, and Aemon thought: *He does not know. He still does not know.* The raven had come at midday. The raven had been black. Aemon had cut its message from its leg himself and the wax had been grey, the seal a stag, and his hands had not stopped shaking since.

"You'll find no welcome here, ser." The girl could not have been more than twelve, barefoot in the mud of the yard, but she held the rusted billhook as if she meant to use it. Behind her the burned shell of the holdfast smoked still. Brienne lowered her visor a finger's breadth. "I am not your enemy, child." "We had a lord, two moons past," the girl said. "He told us the same."

Tyrion poured himself another cup and considered the small council from beneath the rim of it. Pycelle had fallen asleep again, or was pretending to. Varys's hands lay folded in his sleeves like two pale doves. Littlefinger was smiling, which meant Littlefinger had won something already and the rest of them had not yet noticed. *A dwarf, a eunuch, a maester, and a whoremonger,* he thought. *Gods help the realm. We are all it has.*

## Do NOT
- Don't drown the page in heraldry; the blazon is seasoning, not the dish.
- Don't make every POV sound the same — class, age, and education must color the prose.
- Avoid modern idiom ("okay," "guys," "deal with it"). One slip ruins the spell.
- Don't kill a character for shock; the deaths land because the politics earned them.
- Don't let the italic thoughts moralize. They should puncture, not preach.
