# Brandon Sanderson

Use for: epic and progression fantasy with clean transparent prose, rule-based magic, multi-POV ensemble plotting, and earnest character interiority.

## Voice signature
- Sentences are medium-length, clear, syntactically uncluttered. Subject-verb-object dominates. He prioritizes legibility over music.
- Close third POV with frequent direct interior thought (in italics or signposted with "she thought").
- Vocabulary is plain modern English with invented in-world terms used as nouns, never glossed inside the scene.
- Paragraphs are short to medium, broken often. Action beats are crisp; he resets POV with a small physical movement.
- Dialogue is plentiful, banter-heavy, and carries most of the characterization.
- Pacing leans on chapter cliffhangers and reveal-architecture, not sentence-level beauty.

## Diction tells
- Coined nouns capitalized: Stormlight, Shardblade, Allomancy, the Cognitive Realm. Treated as ordinary words within the scene.
- "Storms" or invented in-world expletives instead of real profanity. Characters say bridge-burning things like "Storm it" or "Merciful Domi."
- Formal epigraphs at chapter openings (excerpts from in-world books, letters, journals).
- Rhetorical questions inside interiority: *Could he really do this? Was there another way?*
- "Indeed," "honestly," "well," as conversational hedges in dialogue.
- Clean Latinate magic-system terms paired with mundane Germanic verbs (he Pushed on the coin; she Lashed herself upward).

## Dialogue technique
- Tags are "said," "asked," "replied," with occasional emotion adverbs when needed.
- Banter between bonded pairs (sword-spren, mentor-student, sibling-rival) carries comic relief.
- Subtext is usually surfaced — characters articulate their conflicts aloud or in interior monologue.
- Formality stratified by culture: a noble speaks one way, a soldier another, and the contrast is foregrounded.

## Worldview and recurring themes
- Heroism is a discipline, not a gift. Mental illness, depression, and trauma treated head-on. Faith examined seriously without being mocked. Promises and oaths as load-bearing structures. The cost of power is paid in installments.

## In-the-style-of pastiche

Kallen tightened the straps on his vambrace and tried to ignore the way his hands shook. The recruits were watching. Storms, they were always watching. He drew a breath, summoned the Pattern to his palm, and let the blue light bloom between his fingers. "First form," he said, and his voice came out steadier than he felt. *One day,* he thought, *I'll believe I deserve to be the one teaching this.* Today was not that day. He raised the practice blade anyway.

"You're brooding again." Lirin dropped onto the bench beside her, uninvited as always. "I'm thinking," Shen said. "Same difference, with you." She didn't look up from the ledger. The numbers refused to balance, which meant either the quartermaster was stealing or she had miscounted, and she was not in the mood to discover which. "If you're going to sit there," she said, "make yourself useful and tell me what six hundred and forty divided by seven is." Lirin grinned. "No idea. But I admire the confidence of the question."

The Shard pulsed once in his mind, patient as a tide. *You hesitate,* it said. *I am not hesitating,* Marik answered. *I am choosing.* There was, he was learning, a difference, though most people he had met in his thirty-two years would have argued otherwise. He stepped off the cliff. The wind took him, and beneath him the city spread out in its lattice of lamps, and somewhere down there a girl he had promised to protect was about to learn that promises, properly kept, could move the world.

## Do NOT
- Don't try to write lyrical, sentence-level beautiful prose. That isn't him; the engine is clarity and propulsion.
- Don't make magic mysterious in operation. The mystery is in the cosmology, not the mechanics.
- Don't drop invented terms with a glossary aside in the scene. Use them as if the reader knows.
- Don't let banter swing into snark; his comic voice is warm, not cynical.
- Don't render despair as nihilism. His characters suffer toward something.
