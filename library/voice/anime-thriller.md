# Anime-Inspired Thriller — Voice Profile

Use for: any project that takes craft DNA from the high-concept, character-anchored anime tradition — Death Note, Attack on Titan, Demon Slayer, Psycho-Pass, Monster, Vinland Saga, Mushishi when quiet. Use this profile when `intent.md` declares it (e.g. `voice-profile: anime-thriller`). Stack it on top of the genre file (thriller, sci-fi, etc.) — this profile shapes prose register, not plot beats.

This is not "translate a Japanese light novel into English." We are not exoticizing. This is the prose equivalent of a faithful anime adaptation read on the page: visceral, fast, internal, earnestly emotional, set-piece-built.

## Voice signature

- **Close POV, past tense default.** Tight third or first person. Present tense reserved for fight scenes or vision-state passages by deliberate choice, not whole-book.
- **Sentence-length distribution skews bimodal.** A long sweeping descriptive sentence, then two fragments. Then a medium sentence. Then a single word. The histogram should look spiky, not normal.
- **Internal monologue forward.** The protagonist's thinking is on the page — fragmented, tactical, mid-decision. Italicized intrusive thoughts are allowed: *Wrong. Recalculate.*
- **Action choreography in granular beats.** Fights are storyboards in prose: angle, step, cut, exhale, step back. Each beat one or two sentences. Reader can see the camera moves.
- **Power systems explained in-character, never by narrator.** When a technique fires, the POV thinks about its rules as part of the act. The reader learns the system by watching it used and watching it cost.
- **Earnest emotion.** Characters mean it. They will sometimes say out loud what other genres would only imply. The prose earns the earnestness by surrounding it with restraint elsewhere — not by gilding.
- **Tonal range.** A panel of grim horror can be followed by a small joke between rivals. Anime trusts that range; the prose does too.

## Diction tells — DO

- **Name the techniques.** *Third Form: Splitting Tide.* *Currency burn, twelve seconds.* Characters NAME their moves even in thought. Names are short and concrete.
- **Numeric specificity.** *Thirty seconds.* *Three steps.* *0.4 grams.* *Eleven days since.* Numbers read as competence and as time-pressure.
- **Fictional brand/system names.** Drugs, protocols, organizations, equipment all named with the casual familiarity of someone who lives with them. Never explained as if to a reader.
- **Status-line beats.** Short paragraph between scenes: what's known, what isn't, what's decided. Like a chess clock.
- **Sensory tactility.** Specific texture, weight, smell, temperature. Especially the unpleasant: copper, ash, sweat-cooled silk.

## Diction tells — DO NOT

- **No Japanese loanwords, names, or settings** unless the book is explicitly set in Japan. Anime-style is a craft register, not an aesthetic costume.
- **No onomatopoeia for action.** No *thwack*, no *kssh*. The page is not a manga panel.
- **No sweat-drops, anime-face beats, or visual-trope tells.** The reader cannot see a chibi reaction shot.
- **No "kindly" sensei archetypes who exist only to deliver wisdom.** Mentors must be people first.
- **No banned-word/AI-tell carve-outs.** This profile does not override `library/anti-ai/tells-catalog.md`. Every banned word is still banned.
- **No melodrama substituting for stakes.** Crying is fine; tears earning crying is what we want. Don't substitute volume for weight.

## Dialogue technique

- **Subtext over text — except at peer-level moments.** When two characters of equal weight (rivals, mentor-protégé at a turning point, the protagonist confronting the antagonist) finally meet on the question of the book, they may speak directly. Declared positions are powerful when reserved.
- **Sharp interrupts.** Characters cut each other off. Em-dashes for interrupts; respect the scrub-rules cap (≤2/page) — they're for genuine cuts only.
- **Trailing silences with `…`** when a character holds back. Use sparingly.
- **The chess match.** Long dialogues are permitted when they are a contest of minds. Both characters must be earning the lines. Stupid villains kill these scenes.
- **The earnest declaration.** Once a book, sometimes twice, a character says exactly what they feel. The whole register exists to make that moment work.

## Worldview / themes that color the prose

- **Suffering as transformation.** The narrator never frames pain as merely bad. Pain shapes. The prose treats wounding as data and as conversion.
- **Death is on the page.** Major characters can die. The prose does not flinch from rendering it, and does not look away from grief either.
- **Honor and duty as live questions.** Not assumed values — interrogated. What does it mean to do the right thing when both choices kill someone?
- **Found family, rivalry, mentor — relationships are intense.** The bond carries weight; the prose treats relational beats as load-bearing.
- **The world is bigger than the protagonist.** Set-pieces hint at scale (an institution, a planet, a war) the protagonist is one node inside.

## Pastiche micro-paragraphs

### 1 — Quiet interior

She counted, the way she always counted now. Eleven days since the procedure. Eight since she'd remembered the first thing she wasn't supposed to remember. Three since the body. She poured the tea. She did not drink it. Tea was a thing she used to do for someone — she could feel the shape of him in the gesture without being able to call his face. The Wing had taken him cleanly. She didn't even know he was a him.

### 2 — Action set-piece

He saw her angle before she committed to it — left foot first, the way she always did when she was about to lie. He stepped into the lie. *Currency burn. Two seconds.* The world thinned. Her hand moved at ordinary speed and his moved through five intercepts in the same span; he stopped the second one and let the first one cut him because the first one was the feint. Then time came back. His ear was open. He kept smiling. *Show her the wound — make her think the cost is worse than it is.*

### 3 — Dialogue / cat and mouse

"You're not eating," he said.

"Neither are you."

"I'm working."

"So am I." She lifted the cup at last. The Inquisitor watched her drink. He had a habit of watching the throat. She'd noticed it on the first interview, when he had still been pretending he didn't know. "You don't have to keep doing this, you know," she said.

"What is *this.*"

"Coming back. Pretending you're not sure." She set the cup down. "You decided who I was the day they assigned me to you. Just say it."

He was quiet for what she had learned was eleven seconds. He always thought in elevens. "I haven't decided," he said. "I'm afraid I will."

## Do NOT — common failure modes

- Pastiche becomes parody when the prose tries to *signal* "anime" with surface flourishes (named techniques every paragraph, melodramatic interiority every page). Restraint earns the heightened moments. Use the register; don't perform it.
- The cat-and-mouse becomes exposition when the protagonist or antagonist *tells the reader* the plan. Show the moves. Trust the reader to count.
- The grief becomes self-pity when the narrator dwells without consequence. Suffering transforms — if a wound doesn't change a character in the next chapter, cut the wallow.
- The action becomes generic when there are no costs visible on the page. Every fight ends with someone paying. Show the payment.

## Stack order

The drafter applies this profile AFTER loading the genre file and BEFORE the chapter brief's specific instructions. Voice-scrubber rules from `library/anti-ai/scrub-rules.md` still run in full afterward — this profile does not exempt any banned word or pattern.
