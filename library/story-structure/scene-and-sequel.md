# Scene and Sequel (Swain / Bickham)

Use for: drafting and diagnosing at the chapter/scene level; turning a beat sheet into actual prose units; fixing pacing problems where "stuff happens" but momentum stalls.

## The Two Units

Fiction at the chapter level alternates between Scenes (external, forward) and Sequels (internal, processing). Both are mandatory; either alone produces a broken book.

### Scene = Goal / Conflict / Disaster

- Goal. Protagonist enters wanting something specific, concrete, and time-bounded in this scene. Not "find the killer" — "get the bartender to name the supplier before closing." Vague goals produce flat scenes.
- Conflict. Direct opposition: another character, a system, a deadline, a hostile environment. Escalates in waves — the protagonist tries, is blocked, tries harder, is blocked harder. Three escalations is a workable default.
- Disaster. The scene ends worse than it began. Options: No (goal denied), No and furthermore (denied plus new problem), Yes but (achieved at a worse cost), Yes and (only at false victories like the midpoint). "Yes" without complication ends a chain and should be rare.

### Sequel = Reaction / Dilemma / Decision

- Reaction. Emotional fallout from the disaster. Grief, rage, numbness, denial. Length scales to the disaster's weight; a major reversal earns pages, a minor one earns a paragraph.
- Dilemma. The protagonist surveys bad options. Every available path has a cost. If there is a clearly correct move, the sequel is fake — return and raise the stakes.
- Decision. Choice of a new goal, which becomes the Goal of the next Scene. This is the engine: Decision -> next Scene's Goal closes the loop and produces forward motion.

## Alternation and Chaining

- Default rhythm: Scene, Sequel, Scene, Sequel. In practice, action-heavy genres compress sequels to a few lines; literary fiction expands them to chapters.
- A Sequel can precede the Scene it sets up (chapter break in between) or be embedded inside the next scene's opening.
- Chain disasters into escalation by ensuring each Decision raises the stakes of the next Goal. The protagonist should never re-attempt the same goal at the same level — either they want more, risk more, or have less.
- Track the disaster type across the manuscript. A book of nothing but "No" disasters feels bleak and grinding; nothing but "Yes but" feels evasive. Mix, with the heaviest "No and furthermore" landing at pinch points and Plot Turn 2.

## Diagnosing a Flat Scene

Run this checklist on any scene that feels off:

- Is the Goal stated or implied within the first page? If not, the reader has no rooting interest.
- Is the Goal scene-sized, not story-sized? "Survive" is a story goal; "get out of this building" is a scene goal.
- Is the opposition embodied — a person, force, or ticking clock — on the page? Internal hesitation alone is a Sequel masquerading as a Scene.
- Does the scene end worse than it began? If the protagonist exits with their goal achieved and no new problem, the chain stops.
- Does the next chapter's opening Goal flow from this scene's disaster? If not, you have two scenes with no causal link — coincidence, not story.
- Are there at least two escalations of conflict, not just one exchange? One-beat scenes feel thin.

## Sequel Failure Modes

- Skipping the Sequel entirely. Reader feels whiplash; the protagonist seems unaffected by events.
- Sequel with no Dilemma. Protagonist just feels bad, then the next scene happens. No real choice = no agency.
- Sequel that resolves the disaster instead of choosing the next problem. The disaster's weight evaporates.
- Sequels that all sound the same emotionally. Vary register: rage, calculation, grief, dark humor, dissociation.

## When Not to Use This

- Highly experimental, lyric, or mosaic forms where causal momentum is deliberately suppressed. Scene-and-sequel assumes a protagonist with agency pursuing goals; novels built on drift, fragmentation, or pure interiority will fight the model.
