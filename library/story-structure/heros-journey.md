# Hero's Journey (Campbell / Vogler)

Use for: mythic, fantasy, coming-of-age, and quest narratives where the protagonist is transformed by crossing into a qualitatively different world. Especially useful when the story's engine is identity change, not just plot resolution.

## The 12 Stages (Vogler's distillation)

- Ordinary World. Establish the hero's baseline competence, lack, and the rules of their reality. The reader needs a "before" to measure transformation against.
- Call to Adventure. A summons, threat, or invitation that points to the Special World. Should target a specific lack from the Ordinary World.
- Refusal of the Call. The hero hesitates from fear, duty, or insufficiency. Reveals interior obstacles; without it, commitment feels free and therefore weightless.
- Meeting the Mentor. Hero gains a gift, lesson, or relationship that makes crossing possible. The mentor need not be old or wise — a rival, a book, or a dead parent's letter can carry the function.
- Crossing the Threshold. Active, committed entry into the Special World. The rules change; old skills may not apply.
- Tests, Allies, Enemies. Hero learns the new world by encounter. Plant every faction, ally, and rule needed for the climax.
- Approach to the Inmost Cave. Preparation for the central ordeal. Often a planning sequence or a quiet beat that lets stakes consolidate.
- Ordeal (Belly of the Whale). Symbolic death. Hero faces the worst thing and is changed. This is the structural midpoint, not the climax.
- Reward (Seizing the Sword). Hero gains the prize — object, knowledge, reconciliation. False sense of victory; consequences haven't landed.
- The Road Back. Pursuit begins. The Special World rejects the hero; getting home is harder than getting in.
- Resurrection. Final test. Hero confronts a higher-stakes echo of the Ordeal and proves transformation by acting differently than they did at the start.
- Return with the Elixir. Hero returns to the Ordinary World bearing something — knowledge, power, healing, a child, a story — that improves it.

## Full vs. Partial Use

- Use the full 12 when transformation is the point and the genre invites myth (epic fantasy, portal fantasy, secondary-world quest, religious or initiation story). Word counts above 100k support the full shape.
- Use partial (Call, Threshold, Ordeal, Resurrection, Return) for thrillers, romances, and contemporary fiction where the mythic apparatus would feel grandiose. Treat the four pillars as a structural skeleton hidden under genre clothes.
- Skip Mentor and Refusal when the protagonist is already committed (procedurals, military fiction, sequels). The Call collapses into the Inciting Incident.

## Adapting for Non-Mythic Genres

- Romance: Special World = the relationship; Threshold = the first vulnerable disclosure; Ordeal = the third-act break; Resurrection = the grand gesture.
- Mystery: Special World = the case's reality beneath surface appearances; Reward = the solution; Resurrection = confronting the killer.
- Literary: keep the Refusal and Ordeal, externalize little, let the elixir be perceptual.

## Common Failure Modes

- Refusal of the Call trap: writers stage the refusal as a single line ("I can't") and move on. A real refusal requires concrete cost — the hero tries to live the old life and fails, or accepts and then backs out. Without it, the commitment in Crossing the Threshold has no weight.
- Belly of the Whale trap: the Ordeal is staged at 75% (where the All Is Lost belongs), starving the second half. The Ordeal is the midpoint symbolic death; the Resurrection at ~85-90% is the literal/final test. Conflating them collapses the back half.
- Mentor as exposition dump: if the mentor only delivers worldbuilding, cut them. Mentor must give something costly to take.
- Return with no Elixir: hero comes home unchanged or empty-handed. The arc fails. Define the elixir before drafting Act 3.

## When Not to Use This

- Slice-of-life, ensemble, and anti-transformation stories (where the point is that the protagonist cannot change). The journey's promise of return-with-elixir will fight the theme.
