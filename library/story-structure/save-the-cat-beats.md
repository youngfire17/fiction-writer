# Save the Cat: 15 Beats

Use for: commercial fiction with a clear protagonist and external goal (thriller, romance, mystery, YA, upmarket); when the three-act spine is too loose and you need a tighter beat-by-beat scaffold. Percentages reference Jessica Brody's novel adaptation of Snyder's screenwriting model.

## The Beats

- Opening Image (0-1%). A snapshot of the protagonist's flawed status quo. Sets tone, genre, and the "before" half of the change arc. Example: Katniss hunting in District 12 (The Hunger Games).
- Theme Stated (~5%). Someone, usually not the protagonist, says the lesson the story will teach. Often ignored on first read. Example: Haymitch's "stay alive" reframes survival vs. defiance.
- Setup (1-10%). Show the protagonist's world, wants, and the three things broken in their life. Plant every tool/person needed later. Example: Bilbo's quiet hobbit-hole life and longing.
- Catalyst / Inciting Incident (~10%). External disruption. Example: Gandalf invites Bilbo; Hagrid arrives for Harry.
- Debate (10-20%). Should I go? The protagonist resists. Ends when the answer becomes yes. Example: Frodo deciding to leave the Shire.
- Break Into Two (~20%). Protagonist commits and enters the upside-down world. Door closes behind them.
- B Story (~22%). Introduce the relationship or subplot that carries the theme. Often a love interest, mentor, or foil. Example: Sam's loyalty thread; Hermione/Ron.
- Fun and Games (20-50%). The "promise of the premise" — the marketing trailer scenes. Lightest section even in dark books. Example: Hogwarts wonder; heist team assembling.
- Midpoint (~50%). False victory or false defeat. Stakes raise; clock starts; protagonist shifts from want to need (or the reverse). Example: Capture of the goblet at the Yule Ball reframes Goblet of Fire.
- Bad Guys Close In (50-75%). External pressure rises while internal flaws sabotage the team. Allies fracture.
- All Is Lost (~75%). Whiff of death — literal or symbolic. Mentor dies, plan fails, secret revealed. Example: Dumbledore's death (Half-Blood Prince).
- Dark Night of the Soul (75-80%). Protagonist sits in the wreckage and confronts the theme. The need surfaces.
- Break Into Three (~80%). Synthesis: A-plot solution arrives via B-story lesson. Protagonist re-engages with a new plan.
- Finale (80-99%). Five-point execution: gather team, storm the castle, high tower surprise, dig deep down, execution of the new plan. Antagonist defeated; protagonist proves change.
- Final Image (~99-100%). Mirror of opening image showing the "after." Example: Katniss boarding the train home, transformed.

## Common Failure Modes

- Soggy midpoint: the 50% beat is a fight scene but doesn't flip want-to-need or raise stakes. Make it a decision or revelation, not just spectacle.
- Late B Story: introduced after 30% means the theme has no carrier through Fun and Games; the climax then feels unearned.
- Theme Stated absent or too on-the-nose: either plant it conversationally before 8% or cut and let the Dark Night carry the load alone.
- All Is Lost without a death (literal/metaphorical/relational): the dark night will feel hollow. Kill something the reader values.
- Fun and Games that ignores the premise: if your logline is "wizard school" and your 20-50% has no wizard school, restructure.
- Finale that introduces a new power, weapon, or ally: cheats the contract. Plant it before All Is Lost.

## When Not to Use This

- Ensemble novels with no single protagonist, literary character studies, and structurally experimental work. Save the Cat assumes a clean want-to-need arc on one POV; force-fitting flattens multi-thread narratives.
