# Three-Act Structure with Beats

Use for: planning the spine of any commercial novel; sanity-checking pacing; generating a first-pass beat sheet when genre conventions don't dictate a more specific template.

## Shape

- Act 1 = roughly 25% (setup, status quo, exit from ordinary world).
- Act 2 = roughly 50% (escalating obstacles, false victories, deepening stakes); split in half by the midpoint.
- Act 3 = roughly 25% (final approach, climax, new normal).

## The Nine Load-Bearing Beats

Percentages are of total length. Anchors: 90k commercial novel / 140k epic-fantasy doorstop.

- Hook (0-3%, ~0-2.7k / 0-4.2k). Opening image or question that promises tone, genre, and a problem worth 90,000+ words. Must establish point of view and at least one source of friction in the protagonist's life.
- Inciting Incident (~10-12%, ~9-11k / 14-17k). External event that disturbs the status quo and forces the protagonist to consider a new course. Protagonist need not accept yet; they must notice.
- Plot Turn 1 / Lock-In (~20-25%, ~18-22k / 28-35k). Protagonist commits to the story problem; door to ordinary world closes. After this point, retreat costs more than advance.
- Pinch 1 (~37%, ~33k / 52k). First hard reminder of the antagonistic force; a setback or threat-on-screen that demonstrates the opposition's reach. Raises stakes, narrows options.
- Midpoint (~50%, ~45k / 70k). False victory or false defeat that reframes the story. Protagonist shifts from reactive to proactive (or vice versa); new information changes the goal or the cost. Best midpoints flip the question being asked.
- Pinch 2 (~62%, ~56k / 87k). Second, larger blow from the antagonist; allies lost, plans broken, a mentor or subplot character often dies/leaves here. Forces the protagonist toward the all-is-lost.
- Plot Turn 2 / All Is Lost (~75%, ~67k / 105k). Lowest point. The protagonist's plan has failed; the thing they thought they needed is gone or proven wrong. The "dark night" follows immediately.
- Climax (~88-95%, ~80-86k / 124-133k). Direct confrontation with the central opposition. Protagonist uses the lesson learned at the midpoint plus the change forged in the dark night. No new tools introduced here.
- Resolution (~95-100%, last 4-5k / 7k). Show the new equilibrium. Mirror the opening image to make change legible. Tie off promise made at the hook; leave only the threads you intend for a sequel.

## Common Failure Modes

- Act 2 sag: pinch 1 and the midpoint land too close together, or the midpoint is a plot event with no thematic reframe. Fix by pushing the midpoint to a decision, not an explosion.
- Late inciting incident: past 15% the reader is bored. Trim setup or open in motion.
- Climax dependent on a new gadget/ally introduced after 75%. Plant it before pinch 2.
- Resolution longer than 8%: feels like a victory lap. Cut to the mirror image of the hook.

## When Not to Use This

- Pure literary mood pieces, vignette collections, or experimental structures (braided timelines, reverse chronology) where the three-act shape will flatten the form. Use scene-level structure instead and let the macro shape emerge.
