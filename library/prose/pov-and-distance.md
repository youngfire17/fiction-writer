# POV and Psychic Distance

Use for: choosing point of view, controlling how close the narrator sits to the character's consciousness, and diagnosing POV slippage.

## The four main POVs

- **First person ("I")**: maximum intimacy with one consciousness. Voice carries the book. Use for: unreliable narrators, voice-driven novels, memoir-adjacent fiction, confessional tone. Risks: claustrophobic; protagonist must be present for every scene; harder to convey what the narrator can't see or won't admit (though that limitation is often the point).
- **Close third ("she")**: third-person grammar with first-person interiority. The default contemporary choice. Use for: most literary and commercial novels. You can render the character's thoughts in their own idiom without "I." Risks: writers drift into accidental omniscience or filter heavily.
- **Omniscient**: a narrator with access to all minds and information, often with a distinct voice of their own (Tolstoy, Eliot, Adichie's *Half of a Yellow Sun*). Use for: large casts, historical sweep, ironic distance, thematic commentary. Risks: in unskilled hands reads as head-hopping; modern readers may resist if the narrator's voice isn't compelling.
- **Second person ("you")**: implicates the reader, or addresses an absent person, or distances a traumatized narrator from self. Use for: short bursts, specific effects (Bright Lights, Big City; "you" as protective distance from horror). Risks: wears thin past short-story length unless the conceit is load-bearing.

## Gardner's psychic distance (1-5 scale)

1. **Far / cinematic**: "It was winter of the year 1853. A large man stepped out of a doorway."
2. **Mid-far**: "Henry J. Warburton had never much cared for snowstorms."
3. **Mid-close**: "Henry hated snow."
4. **Close**: "God how he hated this snow."
5. **Deep / interior**: "Snow. Goddamn miserable shit-fucking snow."

- Each level moves deeper into the character's idiom, syntax, and unfiltered thought.
- A scene typically opens at 1-2 (orienting the reader) and slides to 3-5 as the character engages. Slide deliberately. Don't snap.
- Bad slide: 2 → 5 → 2 → 4 in one paragraph. Reader gets seasick.
- Good slide: descending into a character's panic, you let the syntax fragment, vocabulary roughen, sensory detail narrow.

## Head-hopping vs. controlled multi-POV

- **Head-hopping**: switching whose mind we're inside within a scene without signal. Disorients. "She thought he looked tired. He thought she looked beautiful." — within one paragraph, fatal.
- **Controlled multi-POV**: switch at scene or chapter breaks. Name or anchor the new POV character in the first paragraph. Stick with them until the next break.
- Omniscient is not head-hopping. Omniscient has a unifying narrator-voice doing the moving; the reader feels guided, not lost.
- Test: can the reader, mid-scene, name whose head we're in without looking back?

## Filtering and how to strip it

- Filter words distance the reader by inserting the character's perception as a verb: *saw, heard, felt, noticed, watched, realized, thought, wondered, remembered, decided.*
- Before: "She saw that he was angry. She noticed his hands were shaking. She felt afraid."
- After: "He was angry — his hands shook. Her stomach dropped."
- Exception: filters are useful when you want distance. A character dissociating might *watch herself* pick up the knife. That's a deliberate filter, not a default one.

## Accidental omniscience

- In close third on Character A, you cannot say "Character B felt suddenly hopeful." A doesn't have access to that.
- You can write: "B's face softened, and for a moment he looked younger." A observes; the reader infers.
- Internal states of non-POV characters must come through behavior, dialogue, or A's interpretation (which may be wrong — and that's useful).

## Common failure modes

- Defaulting to close third without choosing it. Pick the POV consciously; let the book require it.
- Filter-word infestation in close third. The fix is a search-and-strip pass.
- Psychic distance whiplash: snapping from cinematic wide to deep interior without transition.
- "She didn't know that..." or "Little did he realize..." — these are omniscient intrusions in a close third book.
- Conveniently overheard exposition because the POV character can't otherwise know the information. Better: let the gap stay a gap, or change POV.
- First-person narrators who describe themselves in mirrors. Find another way.
- Multi-POV without distinct voices. If you covered the chapter headers, could the reader tell whose chapter it is?
