# Sentence Craft

Use for: shaping prose at the line level — rhythm, verb choice, syntactic variety, and the music that distinguishes draft prose from finished prose.

## Core principles

- A paragraph is a melody. Sentences are notes of varying length. Same-length sentences flatline.
- Mix a 3-word punch with a 30-word sweep. The short one lands because the long one set it up.
- Strong verbs do the work of weak verbs plus adverbs. "Crept" beats "walked quietly." "Slammed" beats "closed loudly."
- Concrete nouns over abstractions. "A '78 Buick rusting in the yard" beats "an old car." Specificity is authority.
- Cadence is built on stresses, not syllable counts. Read aloud. If your tongue trips, the reader's eye will.
- Latinate words (utilize, ascertain, commence) lengthen and soften. Anglo-Saxon words (use, find out, start) hit harder. Choose deliberately.

## Sentence shapes

- **Periodic sentence**: main clause held until the end. Builds tension. "Through the smoke, past the screaming, over the body of the man she had loved for seventeen years, she ran."
- **Cumulative (loose) sentence**: main clause first, modifiers piling after. Feels observational. "She ran, smoke in her throat, her hand bleeding, the screaming behind her now."
- **Periodic for revelation. Cumulative for immersion.** Alternate.
- **Parallelism**: matched grammatical structures lend authority and rhythm. "He came to forget, to drink, to disappear." Break parallelism intentionally to surprise.
- **Fragments** work when (a) they sound like the POV character's thought, (b) they punctuate after a long sentence, or (c) they isolate an image. "Snow. Then more snow." Fragments overused become tic.

## Before / after

1. Adverb crutch
   - Before: "She walked slowly into the room, looking around nervously."
   - After: "She edged into the room, her eyes ticking corner to corner."

2. Abstraction
   - Before: "He felt great sadness at the loss of his childhood home."
   - After: "He stood in the driveway. The maple was gone. They had paved where the maple was gone."

3. Flat verbs
   - Before: "The car went down the road fast."
   - After: "The car tore down the road, kicking gravel."

4. Monotone rhythm
   - Before: "He opened the door. He stepped inside. He closed the door. He locked it."
   - After: "He opened the door, stepped inside, closed it. Locked it."

5. Buried subject
   - Before: "There was a man standing by the window who seemed to be watching them."
   - After: "A man stood by the window, watching them."

6. Soft verb + noun
   - Before: "She made a decision to leave."
   - After: "She decided to leave." Or better: "She left."

7. Periodic in service of reveal
   - Before: "She realized he was her father, after all these years, after the letters and the silence and the photograph."
   - After: "After the letters, the silence, the photograph she had kept folded in her wallet for eleven years, she understood: he was her father."

## Rhythm techniques

- Vary openings. Don't start three consecutive sentences with "She." Lead with a participle, a prepositional phrase, a dependent clause, dialogue.
- Place the stressed word at sentence end where it lands hardest. "He killed her with a hammer" is weaker than "He killed her. With a hammer."
- Polysyndeton (and...and...and) accumulates and exhausts. Asyndeton (no conjunctions) accelerates.
- White space is rhythm. A one-line paragraph is a held breath.

## Common failure modes

- Adverbs propping up vague verbs ("ran quickly," "said angrily").
- Every sentence the same length and shape — usually 12-18 words, subject-verb-object.
- "Was" / "were" plus -ing constructions ("was running") where simple past would punch ("ran").
- Filter words: "she saw," "she heard," "she felt," "she noticed" — strip and put the perception directly on the page.
- Throat-clearing openers: "It was at this point that..." "There came a moment when..."
- Specificity inflation: piling adjectives ("the old, decrepit, ancient, weather-beaten house") instead of finding the one right noun.
- Reading only on screen. Read aloud. The ear catches what the eye approves.
