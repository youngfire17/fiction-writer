# Show, Don't Tell — and When to Tell

Use for: deciding whether a moment needs dramatized scene or compressed summary, and rendering interior states without naming them flatly.

## What the rule actually means

- "Show, don't tell" is shorthand for: prefer scene (dramatized, moment-by-moment, sensory) over summary (compressed, narrated, abstract) **at the points where emotional or thematic weight lives**.
- It is not a prohibition on summary. Summary is essential. A novel made entirely of scene is exhausting and shapeless.
- Show: "He shoved the plate away. The fork rang against the tile." Tell: "He was angry." The first lets the reader feel the anger; the second informs.
- The reader's pleasure is in inferring. Showing is faith in the reader's intelligence. Telling does the work for them.

## Scene vs. summary

- **Scene**: real-time, sensory, dialogue, gesture. Reader is present. Slows the clock.
- **Summary**: narrator compresses time, distills, generalizes. Speeds the clock.
- A novel breathes by alternating. Long scene, short summary bridge, long scene. Or: short scene, long summary, scene.
- Rule of thumb: dramatize the turning points. Summarize the connective tissue.

## When telling is correct

- **Time compression**: "For three years he drove the same route, picked up the same coffee, said hello to the same woman at the counter." You don't dramatize three years.
- **Low-stakes transitions**: getting from the office to the bar. "By eight he was at Doyle's." Don't render the commute unless something happens on it.
- **Suppressed interiority**: a POV character actively not feeling something can be told flatly, in their voice. "He was fine. He was completely fine. He ordered another drink." The tell reveals the suppression.
- **Backstory exposition**: a paragraph of summarized history is often kinder than a flashback scene that grinds momentum.
- **Tonal authority**: omniscient narrators tell constantly, and well. Telling with voice is not the same as telling with vagueness.
- **Pace control**: after a dense dramatic scene, a few lines of summary let the reader exhale.

## Showing emotion concretely

Render interior states through:

- **Body**: heart, breath, throat, stomach, jaw, hands, knees, skin temperature. But beware the same five clichés (heart pounded, stomach dropped). Find the specific tic of this character. He flexes his bad knee when he lies. She bites the inside of her cheek.
- **Action**: what does the character do with their hands, their feet, the object in front of them? Anger picks up the salt shaker and sets it down two inches to the left.
- **Environment**: weather, light, objects, sound — filtered through mood. A grieving character notices the dust on the lampshade. A jealous character notices the way the other man laughs.
- **Dialogue and its absence**: what the character says, what they don't say, the question they answer with another question.
- **Syntax and rhythm**: a panicking character's sentences shorten and fragment. A numb character's sentences flatten and list.

## Before / after

1. Named emotion
   - Tell: "She was nervous about the interview."
   - Show: "In the elevator she checked her teeth in the brushed-steel panel. Twice."

2. Reported anger
   - Tell: "He was furious with his brother."
   - Show: "He read the text. He read it again. He set the phone face-down on the counter, very carefully, as if it might detonate."

3. Sadness
   - Tell: "She felt sad about her father."
   - Show: "She found his reading glasses on the kitchen table and could not, for a long moment, pick them up."

## The tear problem

- "She felt sad" is bad. Replacing it with four paragraphs about a single tear sliding down a cheek past a freckle past a scar is worse.
- Overshowing telegraphs as hard as telling. The reader sees the writer working.
- The trick is the oblique detail: the character does something near the grief, not at it. They fold the towels. They notice the dishwasher needs unloading. The reader supplies the feeling.
- Earn big emotional renderings by withholding them. One precise gesture beats a paragraph of trembling.

## Common failure modes

- Naming the emotion and then showing it: "She was furious. Her hands shook, her jaw clenched..." Pick one. Usually the show.
- Telegraphing: signaling the feeling to come ("she had no idea what was about to happen"). Trust the scene.
- Generic body cues: pounding hearts, churning stomachs, dropped jaws. Specify or cut.
- Showing what should be summarized: three pages of a character commuting because the writer doesn't trust transitions.
- Summarizing what should be shown: the climactic confrontation rendered in a paragraph of past-perfect.
- "She felt" as a tic. Almost always cuttable in close third.
- Confusing showing with cataloging. Five sensory details about a room is not showing; it's inventory. Showing means the details do emotional work.
