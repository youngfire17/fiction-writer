# Dialogue Mechanics

Use for: writing and editing spoken exchanges — tags, beats, rhythm, subtext, and the punctuation of speech.

## Tags vs. beats

- **Tag**: "he said," "she asked." Grammatically attached to the line. Tells the reader who is speaking.
- **Beat**: an action, gesture, or piece of business in its own sentence near the line. Tells the reader who is speaking and does other work too.
- Tag: `"I'm leaving," she said.`
- Beat: `She picked up her coat. "I'm leaving."`
- Beats outperform tags whenever the action characterizes, sets pace, or grounds the speaker in the room. Tags outperform beats when you need to be invisible.

## Said is invisible — earn other tags

- "Said" disappears on the page. The reader's eye skims it as punctuation. This is a feature.
- "Asked" is also nearly invisible and fine for questions when context doesn't already make it one.
- Said-bookisms ("she ejaculated," "he expostulated," "she chortled," "he hissed" on a line without sibilants) are visible, comic, and pull the reader out.
- Earn a non-said tag with a reason: volume genuinely matters, or the manner isn't inferable from the line itself.
- Adverb-on-tag ("she said angrily") usually signals the line isn't doing the work. Rewrite the line or use a beat.

## Action beats as characterization

- A beat is free real estate for character. What the speaker does while talking reveals more than the words.
- `"I'm fine." She rinsed the same glass for the third time.`
- `"Of course I trust him." He didn't look up from his phone.`
- Inert beats ("he nodded," "she smiled") are wallpaper. Make the beat specific to this character now.
- Beats also pace the exchange. No beat is fast; long beat slows the room.

## Interrupt patterns, overlap, silence

- **Interruption mid-line**: em dash inside the quote. `"I just thought we could —" / "We can't."`
- **Trailing off**: ellipsis. `"I suppose if you really..."` Used sparingly; ellipsis is sugar.
- **Overlap**: dialogue stacked without beats, fast back-and-forth, can omit tags entirely once speakers are established.
- **Silence as line**: a beat where dialogue is expected. `"Well?" / He looked at the floor.` Silence answers; the reader fills in.
- **Non-answers**: a character responds to a different question than the one asked. This is how real people deflect and how subtext gets carried.

## Punctuating dialogue with action

- New speaker, new paragraph. Always. This is how the reader tracks who is talking when tags are absent.
- A beat attached to a line goes in the same paragraph as that line. Listener's beat gets its own paragraph.
- Comma inside the quote before a tag: `"Get out," he said.` Period inside the quote before a beat: `"Get out." He pointed at the door.`

## Subtext layering

- Characters rarely say what they mean. They say what they can manage to say.
- The line on the page is the surface; the want and the wound are beneath it. The reader feels the gap.
- `"How was work?" / "Fine."` is an entire marriage if the scene before earned it.
- Two characters can carry on the explicit conversation and the real conversation at once. The explicit one is about the roof. The real one is about whether to leave.

## Before / after

1. Tag adverb -> beat
   - Before: `"I don't care," she said angrily.`
   - After: `"I don't care." She turned the page hard enough to tear it.`
2. Said-bookism
   - Before: `"Watch out!" he ejaculated.`
   - After: `"Watch out!" he said.` (Or no tag — the exclamation does the work.)
3. Inert beat
   - Before: `"I love you," he said, smiling.`
   - After: `"I love you." He was still holding the car keys.`
4. Over-tagged exchange
   - Before: `"Where were you?" she asked. / "Out," he said. / "Out where?" she asked. / "Just out," he said.`
   - After: `"Where were you?" / "Out." / "Out where?" / He shrugged.`
5. On-the-nose
   - Before: `"I'm furious with you for forgetting our anniversary," she said.`
   - After: `"There's leftover cake in the fridge," she said. "I had some yesterday."`

## Common failure modes

- Said-bookisms ("ejaculated," "expostulated," "chortled").
- Tagging every line when speakers are already clear from alternation.
- Adverbs propping up tags instead of the line carrying the tone.
- Wallpaper beats: nods, smiles, sighs, shrugs on autopilot. Specify or cut.
- Characters monologuing — paragraph-long speeches no one would deliver uninterrupted. Let the listener react or interrupt.
- Everyone sounds the same. Each character needs idiolect: vocabulary, sentence length, tics.
- Exposition smuggled into dialogue ("As you know, Bob..."). If both characters know it, they don't say it.
- On-the-nose lines where characters announce their feelings. Subtext is the medium.
- Punctuation drift: tag-comma vs. beat-period confusion; new speaker without new paragraph.
- Ellipsis and em-dash overuse. Each should do specific rhythmic work.
